Lesson 9.1

Addition Strategies: Making Tens and Hundreds

Smart strategies make adding fast and accurate!

What Are We Learning Today?

You already know how to add -- but today we learn strategies that make addition faster and easier in your head! Instead of just counting up, these strategies help you use what you already know about numbers to add efficiently.

Key Words to Know

Sum -- The answer to an addition problem. In 34 + 52 = 86, the sum is 86.
Addend -- Each number being added. In 34 + 52, both 34 and 52 are addends.
Make a Ten -- A strategy where you break one addend apart to make the other addend reach 10 (or 20, 30...).
Decompose -- To break a number into smaller parts to make calculating easier.
Number Bond -- A diagram showing how a whole number is made up of two parts.

Strategy 1: Make a Ten (or Hundred)

When one addend is close to a round number (10, 20, 100...), adjust both numbers to make a "nice" number first!

Example: 47 + 35

47 is 3 away from 50. Take 3 from 35:

47 + 3 = 50, and 35 - 3 = 32

Now: 50 + 32 = 82

Example: 298 + 46

298 is 2 away from 300. Take 2 from 46:

298 + 2 = 300, and 46 - 2 = 44

Now: 300 + 44 = 344

Strategy 2: Break Apart (Decompose) an Addend

Split one number by place value, then add each part separately.

Example: 154 + 232

Break 232 into 200 + 30 + 2

154 + 200 = 354

354 + 30 = 384

384 + 2 = 386

Strategy 3: Use Known Facts and Doubles

Doubles

6 + 6 = 12, so 6 + 7 = 13 (one more)

8 + 8 = 16, so 8 + 9 = 17

These are called doubles plus one!

Commutative Property

Add in the easier order! 3 + 97 is easier as 97 + 3 = 100

Always start with the bigger number when counting up.

Strategy 4: Number Bonds -- Part + Part = Whole

86
54
32

54 + 32 = 86  |  32 + 54 = 86  |  86 - 54 = 32  |  86 - 32 = 54

Key Idea: One number bond gives you FOUR related facts -- two addition and two subtraction. Addition and subtraction are inverse operations, just like multiplication and division!

Try It Yourself!

  1. Use the "make a hundred" strategy to solve: 197 + 45
  2. Use the break-apart strategy to solve: 263 + 124
  3. Use doubles-plus-one to solve: 7 + 8
  4. Draw a number bond for 75 and write all four related facts.

Remember This!

Make a ten/hundred -- adjust to a round number first.

Break apart -- decompose by place value then add piece by piece.

Doubles plus one -- use what you already know!

Lesson 9.2

Adding 2- and 3-Digit Numbers with Regrouping

Master the standard algorithm for addition -- including carrying!

What Are We Learning Today?

When the digits in a column add up to 10 or more, we need to regroup (also called "carrying"). This is the standard algorithm -- the step-by-step method taught in school. Today we master adding with regrouping in both the tens AND hundreds columns!

Key Words to Know

Algorithm -- A step-by-step procedure for solving a math problem.
Regrouping -- When 10 ones become 1 ten, or 10 tens become 1 hundred -- also called "carrying."
Carrying -- Moving the extra ten (or hundred) to the next column to the left.

The Standard Algorithm -- Step by Step

1
Line up the numbers by place value (ones under ones, tens under tens, hundreds under hundreds).
2
Add the ONES column first. If the sum is 10 or more, write the ones digit and carry the tens digit to the top of the tens column.
3
Add the TENS column (including any carried number). Regroup again if needed.
4
Add the HUNDREDS column. Write the full sum.

Example 1: No Regrouping

  3 4 5

+ 2 3 2

-------

  5 7 7

5+2=7 (ones), 4+3=7 (tens), 3+2=5 (hundreds). No regrouping needed!

Example 2: Regroup Once

 1

  2 4 8

+ 1 3 5

-------

  3 8 3

8+5=13: write 3, carry 1. Then 1+4+3=8 (tens). 2+1=3 (hundreds).

Example 3: Regroup Twice

 1 1

  3 6 7

+ 2 5 8

-------

  6 2 5

7+8=15: write 5, carry 1. 1+6+5=12: write 2, carry 1. 1+3+2=6.

Always start from the RIGHT (ones column) and work LEFT. Never add from left to right -- you might miss a carry!

Try It Yourself!

  1. Solve with the standard algorithm:   456 + 327   |   183 + 249
  2. Solve (regroup twice):   375 + 456   |   487 + 364
  3. A school collected 347 cans in Week 1 and 285 cans in Week 2. How many cans in total?
  4. Check your answer to #3 using the break-apart strategy from Lesson 9.1.

Remember This!

Always add right to left: ones first, then tens, then hundreds.

If a column sum is 10 or more: write the ones digit, carry the tens digit.

Always check your answer using a different strategy!

Lesson 9.3

Subtraction Strategies: Counting Up and Back

Smart strategies make subtraction easier than you think!

What Are We Learning Today?

Subtraction can feel trickier than addition -- but with the right strategies, it becomes much easier! Today we learn mental math strategies for subtraction: counting up (thinking addition), counting back, and using place value to break apart numbers.

Key Words to Know

Difference -- The answer to a subtraction problem. In 85 - 32 = 53, the difference is 53.
Minuend -- The number being subtracted FROM. In 85 - 32, the minuend is 85.
Subtrahend -- The number being subtracted. In 85 - 32, the subtrahend is 32.
Count Up -- A subtraction strategy where you count forward from the smaller number to the larger number.

Strategy 1: Count Up (Think Addition!)

Instead of taking away, ADD UP from the smaller number to the larger number. The amount you add is the difference!

Example: 83 - 57 = ?

Start at 57. Count up to 83:

57 + 3 = 60 (get to a round number)

60 + 20 = 80

80 + 3 = 83

Total added: 3 + 20 + 3 = 26

So 83 - 57 = 26

When to use this: Count up works especially well when the two numbers are close together, such as 82 - 75 (just count up 7).

Strategy 2: Count Back

Start at the larger number and count backwards by the subtrahend.

Example: 65 - 30 = ?

Start at 65. Count back by tens: 55, 45, 35

65 - 30 = 35

When to use this: Count back works best when subtracting small or round numbers (subtract 10s or single digits).

Strategy 3: Break Apart the Subtrahend

Split the number you are subtracting into parts and subtract one piece at a time.

Example: 152 - 47 = ?

Break 47 into 40 + 7

152 - 40 = 112

112 - 7 = 105

Strategy 4: Adjust to a Round Number

Example: 400 - 198 = ?

198 is close to 200. Subtract 200 instead:

400 - 200 = 200

But we subtracted 2 too many, so ADD 2 back:

200 + 2 = 202

Try It Yourself!

  1. Use count-up to solve: 72 - 48
  2. Use break-apart to solve: 163 - 45
  3. Use the round-number strategy to solve: 300 - 197
  4. Which strategy would YOU choose for 500 - 299? Explain why.

Remember This!

Count up -- add from small to large number (great for close numbers).

Break apart -- split the subtrahend and subtract in pieces.

Round number -- adjust to a near hundred, then fix the answer.

Lesson 9.4

Subtracting 2- and 3-Digit Numbers with Regrouping

Master borrowing -- the key skill for subtracting larger numbers!

What Are We Learning Today?

Sometimes when we subtract, the top digit in a column is smaller than the bottom digit. When this happens, we need to regroup (also called "borrowing") -- we take 1 ten from the tens column and trade it for 10 ones. Today we master this essential skill!

Key Words to Know

Regrouping (Subtraction) -- Trading 1 ten for 10 ones (or 1 hundred for 10 tens) when the top digit is too small to subtract from.
Borrowing -- Another name for regrouping in subtraction -- you "borrow" from the next column.

The Standard Algorithm for Subtraction

1
Line up the numbers by place value.
2
Start with the ONES column. If the top digit is smaller than the bottom, regroup: borrow 1 ten from the tens column (cross it out and write one less), and add 10 to the ones column.
3
Subtract the ones column.
4
Repeat for the tens column, regrouping from the hundreds if needed.
5
Subtract the hundreds column.

Example 1: Regroup Once

  3 14

  4 4

  5 42

- 2  7

------

  3 1 5

542 - 27: ones 2 < 7, borrow from tens. 12-7=5. Then 3-2=1 (tens). 5-0=5 (hundreds).

Example 2: Regroup Twice

 2 13 12

 3 4

  4  2  5

- 1  8  7

--------

  2  3  8

425 - 187: 5<7, borrow → 15-7=8. 1<8, borrow → 11-8=3. 3-1=2.

Subtracting Across a Zero -- Extra Care!

Example: 400 - 156 = ?

We cannot borrow from the tens (it is 0!)

So we borrow from the hundreds FIRST:

400 → 3 hundreds, 10 tens, 0 ones

Then borrow from tens for ones:

3 hundreds, 9 tens, 10 ones

Now subtract: 10-6=4, 9-5=4, 3-1=2 → 244

Tip for zeros: When subtracting from a number with a zero, look to the left to find a non-zero digit to borrow from. Work your way right, regrouping step by step.

Try It Yourself!

  1. Solve with the standard algorithm:   734 - 258   |   561 - 375
  2. Solve (subtract across zero):   500 - 234   |   703 - 456
  3. Check your answer to 734 - 258 by adding: 258 + ___ = 734
  4. A store had 425 items. They sold 178. How many are left?

Remember This!

When the top digit is smaller than the bottom -- REGROUP!

Borrow 1 ten = 10 extra ones. Borrow 1 hundred = 10 extra tens.

For zeros: borrow from the first non-zero digit to the left.

Always check: Difference + Subtrahend = Minuend.

Lesson 9.5

Estimating Sums and Differences

Get a quick, close answer without calculating exactly!

What Are We Learning Today?

An estimate is an approximate answer -- close but not exact. Estimating is useful when you need a quick answer, want to check if your exact answer is reasonable, or do not need a precise result. Today we learn to estimate sums and differences by rounding first!

Key Words to Know

Estimate -- An approximate answer that is close to the exact answer.
Reasonable -- An answer that makes sense and is close to the estimate.
Front-End Estimation -- Using only the leading (first) digit of each number to estimate quickly.
Overestimate -- An estimate that is higher than the exact answer.
Underestimate -- An estimate that is lower than the exact answer.

How to Estimate -- Round First, Then Calculate

Estimating a Sum

Exact: 347 + 263

Round to nearest 100:

347 → 300

263 → 300

Estimate: 300 + 300 = 600

Exact answer: 610 -- close!

Estimating a Difference

Exact: 782 - 319

Round to nearest 100:

782 → 800

319 → 300

Estimate: 800 - 300 = 500

Exact answer: 463 -- reasonable!

Using Estimation to Check Answers

Always ask: "Is my exact answer close to my estimate?" If not, check your work!

ProblemEstimateExact AnswerReasonable?
456 + 312500 + 300 = 800768Yes -- close to 800!
634 - 281600 - 300 = 300353Yes -- close to 300!
278 + 345300 + 300 = 600900No -- check your work!

Over and Under Estimating

Overestimate

Both numbers rounded UP.

247 + 163: round to 300 + 200 = 500. Exact = 410. Estimate is higher.

Underestimate

Both numbers rounded DOWN.

247 + 163: round to 200 + 100 = 300. Exact = 410. Estimate is lower.

Real-world use: When shopping, always OVERESTIMATE so you know you have enough money. Better to think you need more and have some left over than to think you need less and come up short!

Try It Yourself!

  1. Estimate by rounding to the nearest hundred:   523 + 441   |   867 - 324
  2. Estimate by rounding to the nearest ten:   73 + 48   |   92 - 35
  3. A student got 623 as the answer to 287 + 448. Use estimation to check -- is 623 a reasonable answer?
  4. You want to buy items costing $3.75 and $4.25. Should you overestimate or underestimate to make sure you have enough money? Why?

Remember This!

Round first, then add or subtract -- that is your estimate!

Use estimation to check if an exact answer is reasonable.

When shopping or budgeting, it is better to overestimate!

Lesson 9.6

Solving Addition and Subtraction Word Problems

Use everything you know to solve real-life math situations!

What Are We Learning Today?

Word problems bring math to life! Today we practice identifying whether to add or subtract using key words, then solve using the 4-step strategy. We also tackle two-step problems -- where you need to perform two operations to reach the answer.

Key Words to Know

Two-Step Problem -- A word problem that requires two separate calculations to solve.
Equation -- A mathematical statement that two expressions are equal. Example: 345 + 127 = 472.
Unknown -- The missing value you are solving for.

Key Words for Addition and Subtraction

ADD when you see...

  • in all, total, altogether
  • combined, both together
  • how many more do they have
  • gained, collected, received
  • increased by, added

SUBTRACT when you see...

  • how many more, how many fewer
  • how many are left, remain
  • difference between
  • lost, spent, gave away, used
  • decreased by, reduced

One-Step Problem (Addition)

The school library has 348 fiction books and 215 nonfiction books. How many books are there in all?

Understand: I know 348 fiction and 215 nonfiction. I need the total.

Plan: "In all" = add.   348 + 215 = ___

Solve: 348 + 215 = 563 books

Check: 563 - 215 = 348 ✓   Estimate: 300 + 200 = 500 -- reasonable!

One-Step Problem (Subtraction)

A bag held 500 marbles. Jake took out 237. How many marbles are left?

Understand: Started with 500, removed 237. Need what remains.

Plan: "Left" = subtract.   500 - 237 = ___

Solve: 500 - 237 = 263 marbles

Check: 263 + 237 = 500 ✓

Two-Step Problem!

A baker made 250 cookies on Monday and 175 cookies on Tuesday. She sold 318 cookies over the two days. How many cookies does she have left?

Step 1: Find the total cookies made. 250 + 175 = 425

Step 2: Subtract the cookies sold. 425 - 318 = 107 cookies left

Check: 107 + 318 = 425, and 425 - 175 = 250 ✓

Try It Yourself!

  1. Maria collected 423 stamps. Her friend gave her 156 more. How many stamps does she have now?
  2. A store had 600 toys. They sold 247 on Saturday and 189 on Sunday. How many toys are left? (Two steps!)
  3. Team A scored 347 points and Team B scored 289 points. How many more points did Team A score?
  4. Write your OWN two-step word problem using addition and subtraction. Solve it with all four steps.

You Finished Unit 9!

Key addition words: in all, total, combined, gained.

Key subtraction words: left, fewer, difference, spent, lost.

Two-step problems need TWO equations -- solve them in order!

Always check using the inverse operation!
