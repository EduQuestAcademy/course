Lesson 1.1

Asking and Answering Questions About a Story

Learn how to ask great questions and find the answers right inside a story!

What Are We Learning Today?

Have you ever read a story and wondered, "What just happened?" or "Why did that character do that?" That's your brain asking questions! Great readers ask questions before, during, and after they read. Finding the answer in the story is called using text evidence.

Key Words to Know

Question -- Something you ask to find out more information.
Text Evidence -- A detail from the story that proves your answer is correct.
Explicit -- Stated directly in the text -- the answer is right there!
Inference -- A smart guess using text clues plus what you already know.

The 5 Question Words -- The "5 Ws"

Question WordWhat It AsksExample
WHOA person or characterWho is the main character?
WHATA thing, event, or actionWhat happened at the end?
WHEREA place or locationWhere did the story happen?
WHENA time or momentWhen did the adventure begin?
WHYA reason or causeWhy did the character run away?
Memory Tip: Remember the 5 Ws -- Who, What, Where, When, Why. Add HOW to make it the "5 Ws and 1 H"!

Read This Short Story

The Lost Kitten

One rainy afternoon, Maya was walking home from school when she heard a tiny sound coming from behind a bush. She stopped and looked -- there was a small, wet kitten shivering in the cold. Maya gently picked it up and wrapped it in her scarf. "Don't worry," she whispered. She brought the kitten to the animal shelter two streets away, where workers promised to find it a loving home. Maya walked home with a warm feeling in her heart.

Asking and Answering the 5 Ws

QuestionAnswer from the Text
WHO is the story about?Maya, a girl walking home from school.
WHAT did Maya find?A small, wet kitten behind a bush.
WHERE did Maya take the kitten?The animal shelter two streets away.
WHEN did this happen?On a rainy afternoon after school.
WHY did Maya pick up the kitten?Because it was shivering in the cold and needed help.

Try It Yourself!

The Big Game: Sam had practiced every day for three weeks. Today was the basketball championship. His hands shook as he laced his sneakers. When the game started, he missed his first two shots. But he didn't give up. In the last minute, he caught the ball and threw it -- swish! Sam's team won!

  1. Write a WHO question and find the answer in the text.
  2. Write a WHAT question and find the answer in the text.
  3. Write a WHY question and explain how you found the answer.

Remember This!

Ask questions using WHO, WHAT, WHERE, WHEN, or WHY.

Look back at the text for your answer.

Use real words from the story as your evidence.

Lesson 1.2

Story Elements: Characters, Setting, and Plot

The building blocks of every great story!

What Are We Learning Today?

Every story is built from the same three building blocks. When you understand these three elements, you can understand any story you read!

  • Characters -- WHO the story is about
  • Setting -- WHERE and WHEN the story takes place
  • Plot -- WHAT happens from beginning to end

Key Words to Know

Character -- A person, animal, or creature in a story.
Setting -- The time and place where a story happens.
Plot -- The sequence of events from start to finish.
Problem -- The challenge the main character must face.
Solution -- How the problem gets solved at the end.

Characters

People, animals, or creatures. The main character is the most important one. We learn about characters through what they say, do, feel, and think.

Setting

WHERE (a forest, a school, outer space) and WHEN (long ago, in the future, on a Tuesday morning). Settings can be real or made up!

Plot

Beginning -- meet characters and setting, problem appears.
Middle -- character tries to solve it.
End -- problem is solved!

Read and Identify the Elements

The Missing Trophy

Ella loved her swimming trophy more than anything. One morning she woke up and it was gone! She searched everywhere but could not find it. She asked her little brother Jake. His face turned pink. He led her to his room -- the trophy sat on his shelf. "I just wanted to show my friends," he said quietly. Ella took a deep breath. "Next time, just ask me," she said with a smile. They went down for breakfast together.

Story Elements in "The Missing Trophy"

ElementIn the Story
CharactersElla (main character) and Jake (her brother)
SettingTheir home -- Ella's and Jake's bedrooms, in the morning
ProblemElla's trophy was missing
SolutionJake admitted taking it; Ella forgave him

Try It Yourself!

Think of a story you know. In your notebook, write:

  1. Who are the characters? Who is the main character?
  2. What is the setting -- where and when?
  3. What is the problem? How is it solved?

Remember This!

CHARACTERS -- Who is the story about?

SETTING -- Where and when does it happen?

PLOT -- Beginning, Middle, End + Problem and Solution

Lesson 1.3

Fables, Folktales, and Myths

Amazing stories from cultures all around the world!

What Are We Learning Today?

People have been telling stories for thousands of years -- long before books! These ancient stories were passed from parents to children. Today we learn about three special types of stories from cultures around the world.

Key Words to Know

Fable -- A short story with animal characters that teaches a life lesson.
Moral -- The life lesson at the end of a fable.
Folktale -- An old story passed down through generations by telling and retelling.
Myth -- An ancient story created to explain things in nature or the world.
Central Message -- The big idea or lesson the author wants you to take away.

Fables

Short stories with animal characters that end with a moral. Written by Aesop 2,500 years ago!

Examples: The Tortoise and the Hare, The Fox and the Crow, The Ant and the Grasshopper

Folktales

Told out loud and passed down through generations. Every culture has its own!

Examples: Cinderella (500+ versions worldwide!), Anansi the Spider, The Three Little Pigs

Myths

Ancient stories that explain nature -- why the sun rises, where thunder comes from, how the world began.

Examples: Why the Zebra Got Its Stripes, Persephone and the Seasons

Read This Fable

The Fox and the Crow

A crow found a piece of cheese and flew to a tree branch to eat it. A clever fox saw the crow and said, "Oh, beautiful crow! You must have a wonderful singing voice! Please sing for me!" The crow was so flattered that she opened her beak to sing -- and the cheese fell right down to the fox, who snapped it up and ran away laughing.

Moral: Do not let flattery fool you. People who use fake compliments to trick you do not really care about you!

Quick Comparison

TypeCharactersPurposeEnds With
FableUsually animalsTeach a life lessonA moral
FolktalePeople, animals, magical beingsShare cultureOften happily ever after
MythGods, heroes, creaturesExplain natureAn explanation

Try It Yourself!

Think of a fable, folktale, or myth you know. Write:

  1. What type of story is it? How do you know?
  2. Who are the main characters?
  3. What is the central message or moral?
  4. What details from the story teach that message?

Remember This!

FABLE -- Animal characters + moral (life lesson)

FOLKTALE -- Passed down through generations

MYTH -- Explains something in nature

Describing Characters: Traits, Feelings, and Motivations

Getting to know story characters really well!

What Are We Learning Today?

Have you ever felt like you really knew a story character -- almost like they were a real friend? Today we learn how to describe characters by looking at their traits, feelings, and motivations.

Key Words to Know

Character Trait -- A word describing a character's personality (brave, kind, stubborn).
Feeling / Emotion -- How a character feels at a moment in the story.
Motivation -- The reason WHY a character does something.
Inference -- A smart guess using text clues and what you know.

Character Trait Words

BraveKindHonestGenerousDeterminedSelfishSneakyGreedyCuriousShyStubbornImaginative

How Do We Find Character Traits?

Clue TypeExampleTrait It Shows
What they DOShe shares her lunch with a hungry classmate.Generous, kind
What they SAY"I won't give up, no matter what!"Determined, brave
What they THINKHe worried about saying the wrong thing.Anxious, shy
What OTHERS say"You can always count on Maria," said her teacher.Reliable, trustworthy

Read and Analyse

The New Student

When Jaylen walked into the classroom on his first day, he noticed a girl sitting alone, staring at her desk. Everyone else was laughing with friends. Without hesitating, Jaylen walked over and sat next to her. "Hi, I'm Jaylen. Want to be partners for the science project?" The girl's face lit up. Jaylen knew exactly how it felt to be the new kid with no one to talk to.

Notice: The author never says "Jaylen is kind." We figure that out from his ACTIONS -- that's an inference! His motivation is empathy -- he remembered his own experience of feeling left out.

Try It Yourself!

Think of a character from a book you know. Write:

  1. Two character traits that describe them.
  2. What did the character do or say that shows those traits?
  3. What is the character's motivation -- why do they do what they do?
  4. How do the character's feelings change from beginning to end?

Remember This!

TRAITS -- personality words (brave, kind, curious)

FEELINGS -- emotions at a moment (happy, scared, proud)

MOTIVATION -- the reason WHY a character acts

Lesson 1.5

Literal vs. Nonliteral Language

When words mean exactly what they say -- and when they don't!

What Are We Learning Today?

Have you heard "It's raining cats and dogs!" and looked outside for falling animals? Of course not! Some words mean exactly what they say. Others are used in a creative, imaginative way. Today we learn the difference!

Key Words to Know

Literal Language -- Words that mean exactly what they say. No hidden meaning.
Nonliteral Language -- Words used creatively where the meaning is different from the actual words.
Idiom -- A phrase with a hidden meaning. Example: "butterflies in my stomach."
Simile -- Comparison using "like" or "as." Example: "as fast as lightning."
Metaphor -- Says one thing IS another. Example: "The classroom was a zoo."
Personification -- Giving human qualities to non-humans. Example: "The stars danced."

Literal Language

Words mean exactly what they say.

"The dog is brown and white."

"She ate three apples for lunch."

"It rained heavily on Tuesday."

Nonliteral Language

Words used in a creative, imaginative way.

"It's raining cats and dogs."

"I have butterflies in my stomach."

"She flew down the hallway."

Types of Nonliteral Language

TypeWhat It IsExampleReal Meaning
IdiomPhrase with hidden meaning"Break a leg!"Good luck!
SimileComparison using like or as"He was as quiet as a mouse."He was very quiet.
MetaphorSays one thing IS another"The moon is a silver coin."The moon is round and shiny.
PersonificationHuman qualities to non-humans"The wind whispered."The wind made a soft sound.

Try It Yourself!

Decide if each sentence is LITERAL or NONLITERAL. If nonliteral, write what it really means:

  1. "The sun smiled down on the children."
  2. "She put her backpack next to the door."
  3. "He was so hungry he could eat a horse!"
  4. "The cat sat on the warm windowsill."
  5. "Her voice was as sweet as honey."

Remember This!

LITERAL -- Words mean exactly what they say.

NONLITERAL -- Words are used creatively; meaning is different.

Types: Idioms, Similes, Metaphors, Personification

Lesson 1.6

Parts of a Story: Chapters, Scenes, and Stanzas

How stories, plays, and poems are organised into parts!

What Are We Learning Today?

Just like a pizza is cut into slices to make it easier to eat, stories, plays, and poems are divided into smaller parts to make them easier to read and understand!

Key Words to Know

Chapter -- A section of a longer book that organises the story into smaller parts.
Scene -- A section of a play. Each scene takes place in one location at one time.
Stanza -- A group of lines in a poem that belong together, separated by a blank line.
Cliffhanger -- When a chapter ends at an exciting moment to keep you reading!
Stage Directions -- Instructions in a play telling actors what to do. Not spoken aloud.

Quick Comparison Chart

PartFound InWhat It Does
ChapterBooks and novelsOrganises a long story into smaller sections
ScenePlays and dramasShows action in one place and time
StanzaPoemsGroups related lines together like a paragraph

Read This Poem -- Notice the Stanzas!

Stanza 1

The sun woke up and stretched its rays,
And painted the morning gold.
The birds began their cheerful songs,
A story to be told.

Stanza 2

The flowers opened one by one,
And lifted up their heads.
While butterflies danced in the breeze,
Above their flowery beds.

Notice: Stanza 1 describes the sun and birds. Stanza 2 adds flowers and butterflies. Each stanza BUILDS on the previous one!

Try It Yourself!

  1. Think of a chapter book you know. How does each new chapter build on the one before?
  2. What is a cliffhanger? Can you think of a chapter that ended with one?
  3. What is the difference between a chapter, a scene, and a stanza?

Remember This!

CHAPTER -- section of a book

SCENE -- section of a play (one place and time)

STANZA -- group of lines in a poem

Each new part builds on the parts before it!

Lesson 1.7

Point of View: Narrator vs. Characters

Who is telling the story -- and how does that change what we know?

What Are We Learning Today?

Imagine a car accident happened on your street. A driver, a pedestrian, and a bystander all saw the same event -- but each has a different story to tell! In stories, WHO is telling the story matters a lot. Today we learn about point of view.

Key Words to Know

Point of View -- Who is telling the story and how they see events.
Narrator -- The voice that tells the story to the reader.
First-Person -- Narrator IS a character. Uses "I," "me," "my," "we."
Third-Person -- Narrator is OUTSIDE the story. Uses "he," "she," "they," character names.

First-Person Point of View

The narrator IS in the story. We only know what they see, hear, and feel.

"I walked into the dark cave and my heart began to pound."

Key words: I, me, my, we, our

Third-Person Point of View

The narrator watches from OUTSIDE. Can tell us about multiple characters.

"She walked into the dark cave and her heart began to pound."

Key words: he, she, they, character names

Different Characters, Different Points of View

CharacterTheir Point of View on the Same Event
GoldilocksThe house looked empty and the porridge smelled delicious -- she just wanted to rest!
The Three BearsSomeone broke into their home, ate their food, and broke their furniture!
Same story. Very different points of view! Understanding each character's perspective helps us understand WHY they act the way they do.

Try It Yourself!

  1. Is your favourite book written in first-person or third-person? How do you know?
  2. Choose two characters from a story. How do they see the same event differently?
  3. Would the story change if told from a different character's point of view? How?

Remember This!

FIRST-PERSON -- Narrator is IN the story. Uses "I" and "me."

THIRD-PERSON -- Narrator watches from OUTSIDE. Uses "he," "she," "they."

Different characters have different points of view about the same event!

How Illustrations Help Tell the Story

Discover how pictures and words work together to create amazing stories!

What Are We Learning Today?

Have you ever looked at a picture in a book and already felt something -- nervous, happy, or excited -- before reading a single word? Illustrations are not just decorations. They are a powerful part of storytelling!

Key Words to Know

Illustrator -- The artist who creates the pictures for a book.
Mood -- The feeling or atmosphere an illustration creates for the reader.
Facial Expression -- The look on a character's face that shows how they feel.
Body Language -- How a character's body position shows their feelings.

What Can Illustrations Do?

What Illustrations Can DoExample
Show character feelingsA character's tears or wide eyes tell us how they feel.
Create moodDark colours = scary; bright colours = happy.
Show details not in the wordsText says "she entered the room" -- picture shows a messy, mysterious room.
Help visualise the settingA snowy mountain picture helps readers see where the story takes place.
Add humourCharacter claims to be brave -- picture shows them hiding under a bed!

Colours and Mood

Dark blues and blacks
Mysterious, scary, lonely
Warm yellows and oranges
Happy, cosy, exciting
Soft pinks and greens
Gentle, peaceful, hopeful

Analyse This Illustration Description

"A small girl stands at the edge of a vast, dark forest. She is tiny compared to the tall trees. Her lantern is the only spot of warm yellow light. The sky is deep purple-black."

DetailWhat It Tells Us
Dark forest and skyMysterious, possibly dangerous setting
Girl is tiny compared to treesShe feels small and alone
Lantern is the only lightShe is brave -- facing darkness with courage

Try It Yourself!

Think of an illustration from a book you know. Write:

  1. Describe what you see in the illustration.
  2. What mood does it create? What details create that mood?
  3. Does the illustration show something the words do NOT say?
  4. How does it help tell the story better?

Remember This!

Illustrations show mood, details, character emotions, and setting.

Colours, light, and shapes create the feeling of a story.

Sometimes illustrations show things the words do not say!

Lesson 1.9

Comparing Stories by the Same Author

Discover patterns and connections across an author's books!

What Are We Learning Today?

Have you ever read two books by the same author and thought, "These feel similar somehow"? Authors have their own special style -- favourite themes, types of characters, and ways of writing. Today we learn how to compare and contrast two stories by the same author!

Key Words to Know

Compare -- To look for what is SIMILAR between two things.
Contrast -- To look for what is DIFFERENT between two things.
Theme -- The big idea or life lesson running through a story.
Author's Voice -- The unique style that makes an author's writing recognisable.
Venn Diagram -- Two overlapping circles used to compare and contrast two things.

What to Look for When Comparing

Story ElementQuestions to Ask
CharactersAre the same characters in both? Do both have similar types of heroes?
SettingAre the settings similar or different?
ThemeDo both stories share a similar big idea or lesson?
Author's StyleDoes the author use humour? Beautiful descriptions? Short sentences?

Example: Two Magic Tree House Books by Mary Pope Osborne

Dinosaurs Before Dark

  • Setting: Prehistoric times
  • Characters: Jack and Annie
  • Problem: Stranded with dinosaurs

Knights and Castles

  • Setting: Medieval England
  • Characters: Jack and Annie
  • Problem: Trapped in a castle
What's the SAME?What's DIFFERENT?
Same characters: Jack and AnnieDifferent settings and time periods
Both use the magic tree houseDifferent historical challenges
Both have a theme of adventure and learningDifferent facts learned

Try It Yourself!

Choose two books by the same author. In your notebook, draw a Venn diagram and fill it in:

  1. What are the titles and author?
  2. What is SIMILAR between the two books?
  3. What is DIFFERENT between the two books?
  4. What do the similarities tell you about what this author cares about?

You Finished Unit 1!

COMPARE -- Find what is SIMILAR.

CONTRAST -- Find what is DIFFERENT.

Authors often use the same themes, character types, and style across their books -- that's their author's voice!
