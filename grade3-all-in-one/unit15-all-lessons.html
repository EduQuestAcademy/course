Lesson 15.1

Weather and Climate

Weather changes day by day -- climate describes patterns over many years!

What Are We Learning Today?

Have you heard someone say "climate is what you expect, weather is what you get"? Today we learn the important difference between weather (what's happening in the atmosphere right now) and climate (the long-term patterns of an area), and the tools scientists use to measure both.

Key Words to Know

Weather -- The condition of the atmosphere at a specific place and time (today's temperature, clouds, wind, rain).
Climate -- The average weather patterns of an area over a long period of time (30+ years).
Temperature -- How hot or cold the air is. Measured in degrees Fahrenheit (°F) or Celsius (°C).
Precipitation -- Any form of water falling from clouds: rain, snow, sleet, hail.
Wind -- Moving air caused by differences in air pressure.
Humidity -- The amount of water vapour in the air. High humidity feels muggy and damp.
Meteorologist -- A scientist who studies and forecasts weather.

Weather vs. Climate -- The Key Difference

Weather (Short-Term)

What is happening right NOW in the atmosphere.

"It is raining in Kennewick today."

"It will be 85°F tomorrow."

"There is a thunderstorm this afternoon."

Climate (Long-Term)

The PATTERN of weather in an area over 30+ years.

"Eastern Washington has hot, dry summers and cold winters."

"The Amazon rainforest gets rain almost every day."

Memory Trick: "Climate is what you expect; weather is what you get!" You know Seattle has a rainy climate -- but you never know exactly when the next storm will hit. That specific storm is weather!

Washington State Weather and Climate

West of Cascades (Wet side)

Mild, rainy climate. Seattle gets about 38 inches of rain per year. Rarely very hot or very cold. Dense forests thrive here.

East of Cascades (Dry side)

Semi-arid climate. Kennewick gets only about 8 inches of rain per year! Hot summers (100°F+), cold winters. Irrigation needed for agriculture.

The Cascade Mountains create a "rain shadow" -- they block moist Pacific air, making eastern Washington much drier than western Washington!

Remember This!

Weather = short-term atmospheric conditions right now.

Climate = long-term average weather patterns (30+ years).

Thermometer measures temperature. Rain gauge measures rainfall.

The Water Cycle

Water is always moving -- follow its incredible journey around Earth!

What Are We Learning Today?

Did you know the water you drink today might have been in a dinosaur's pond 65 million years ago? Earth has a fixed amount of water that cycles continuously through the oceans, atmosphere, land, and living things. This endless journey is called the water cycle!

Key Words to Know

Evaporation -- Liquid water turning into water vapour (gas) when heated by the sun.
Condensation -- Water vapour cooling and turning back into liquid water droplets (forming clouds).
Precipitation -- Water falling from clouds as rain, snow, sleet, or hail.
Collection -- Water gathering in oceans, lakes, rivers, and groundwater.
Transpiration -- Water released by plants through their leaves into the atmosphere.
Runoff -- Water that flows over land into streams and rivers after precipitation.
Groundwater -- Water that seeps into the soil and collects underground in aquifers.

Each Stage in Detail

Washington's Water Cycle in Action!

The Pacific Ocean evaporates enormous amounts of water. Wind carries the moist air eastward. When it hits the Olympic Mountains and Cascades, it is forced upward. As it rises, it cools and condenses, creating enormous rainfall and snowfall on the western slopes. This is why the Hoh Rainforest on the Olympic Peninsula is one of the wettest places in the continental US!

Remember This!

Evaporation → Condensation → Precipitation → Collection

The sun drives the water cycle by providing energy for evaporation.

Earth's water is recycled -- the same water has been cycling for billions of years!

Types of Clouds and Precipitation

Read the sky like a scientist -- clouds tell us what weather is coming!

What Are We Learning Today?

Clouds are not just fluffy decorations in the sky! Each type of cloud tells us something different about the weather. Learning to identify clouds is one of the oldest and most useful skills in weather forecasting. Today we learn the main cloud types and what each one means for the weather ahead!

Key Words to Know

Cumulus -- Puffy, white, cotton-ball clouds. Fair weather when small; can grow into thunderstorms.
Stratus -- Flat, grey, blanket-like clouds that cover the whole sky. Bring drizzle or grey skies.
Cirrus -- Thin, wispy, high clouds made of ice crystals. Often signal a weather change coming.
Cumulonimbus -- Massive, tall thunderstorm clouds. Bring heavy rain, lightning, and strong winds.
Nimbostratus -- Dark, thick stratus clouds that bring steady, prolonged rain or snow.
Fog -- Stratus clouds at ground level. Forms when warm moist air meets a cool surface.

Cloud Identification Guide

Types of Precipitation

Rain

Liquid water droplets. Forms when cloud droplets combine and fall. Most common precipitation in Washington state.

Snow

Ice crystals that form when water vapour freezes directly into solid crystals. Requires below-freezing temperatures.

Sleet

Rain that freezes into ice pellets before hitting the ground. Forms when rain falls through a freezing layer of air.

Hail

Balls of ice formed in powerful thunderstorm clouds. Strong updrafts toss ice pellets up and down, adding layers of ice until too heavy to stay airborne.

Cloud Name Clues!

Cloud Name Prefixes and Suffixes:
Cumulo- = heap/pile (puffy)  |  Strato- = layer/sheet (flat)  |  Cirro- = curl/wisp (high)
-nimbus/-nimbo- = rain-bearing  |  Alto- = middle height
So: CumuloNIMBUS = puffy + rain = huge storm cloud! NIMBOstratus = rain + layer = steady rain blanket!

Try It Yourself!

  1. Go outside and look at the clouds. What type do you see? What weather might be coming?
  2. You see wispy, high white clouds in the morning. Should you plan for rain tomorrow? What type are they?
  3. What type of cloud brings thunderstorms? How does it look different from a fair-weather cumulus cloud?
  4. Describe the difference between sleet and hail. How does each form?

Remember This!

Cirrus = wispy, high, weather change coming.

Cumulus = puffy, fair weather (unless very tall = thunderstorm!).

Stratus = grey blanket, drizzle.  |  Cumulonimbus = THUNDERSTORM!

Earth's Layers and Landforms

From the centre of the Earth to its highest mountains -- explore our planet's structure!

What Are We Learning Today?

Earth is not just a solid ball of rock! It has distinct layers like an onion, each with different properties. And Earth's surface -- the part we live on -- is shaped by incredible forces into mountains, valleys, plains, and more. Today we journey from Earth's core to its highest peaks!

Key Words to Know

Crust -- Earth's outermost solid layer. The thin shell we live on (5-70 km thick).
Mantle -- The thick layer of hot, semi-solid rock beneath the crust. The largest layer.
Outer Core -- A layer of liquid iron and nickel. Its movement creates Earth's magnetic field!
Inner Core -- A solid ball of iron and nickel at Earth's very centre. Extremely hot (~5,000°C)!
Tectonic Plates -- Giant pieces of Earth's crust that move slowly, causing earthquakes, volcanoes, and mountains.
Landform -- A natural feature of Earth's surface (mountain, valley, plain, canyon, plateau).
Erosion -- The wearing away and movement of rock and soil by water, wind, ice, or gravity.

Earth's Four Layers

CRUST -- 5 to 70 km thick. The solid surface we live on. Oceanic crust (ocean floor) is thinner and denser. Continental crust (land) is thicker and lighter.
MANTLE -- About 2,900 km thick. Hot, slow-moving rock. The upper mantle can flow slowly over millions of years -- this movement moves tectonic plates!
OUTER CORE -- About 2,200 km thick. Liquid iron and nickel at ~4,000°C. Its swirling movement generates Earth's magnetic field -- which protects us from solar radiation!
INNER CORE -- Radius about 1,200 km. Solid iron and nickel despite temperatures of ~5,000°C -- held solid by enormous pressure. The very heart of our planet!

Washington State Geology is Amazing!

Washington has extraordinary geological features: the Cascade Volcanoes (including Mount Rainier at 14,411 ft -- the highest peak in the Cascades!), the Columbia River Gorge carved by Ice Age floods, the scablands of eastern Washington carved by the Missoula Floods, and ongoing tectonic activity with the Juan de Fuca Plate subducting under North America -- making the Pacific Northwest a geologically active zone.

Earth's layers: Crust → Mantle → Outer Core → Inner Core

Tectonic plates move → create mountains, volcanoes, earthquakes.

Erosion by water, wind, and ice shapes Earth's surface over time.

Lesson 15.5

Rocks, Soil, and Fossils

The ground beneath your feet tells the story of Earth's past!

What Are We Learning Today?

Every rock has a story. Rocks record billions of years of Earth's history -- from ancient lava flows to the remains of creatures that lived 500 million years ago. Today we learn the three types of rocks, how soil forms, and how fossils preserve clues about prehistoric life!

Key Words to Know

Igneous Rock -- Rock formed from cooled magma or lava. Example: granite, basalt, obsidian.
Sedimentary Rock -- Rock formed from layers of sediment (sand, mud, shells) pressed together. Example: sandstone, limestone, shale.
Metamorphic Rock -- Rock that has been changed by intense heat and pressure. Example: marble (from limestone), slate (from shale).
Rock Cycle -- The slow process by which rocks transform from one type to another over millions of years.
Soil -- A mixture of weathered rock, minerals, water, air, and organic matter (dead plants/animals).
Fossil -- The preserved remains or trace of an organism that lived long ago, found in rock.
Paleontologist -- A scientist who studies fossils to learn about prehistoric life.

The Three Types of Rocks

Igneous (Fire Rocks)

Form when magma (underground) or lava (surface) cools and solidifies.

Examples: Granite (slow-cooling = large crystals), Basalt (fast-cooling = small crystals), Obsidian (very fast cooling = glassy)

Washington has huge basalt formations from ancient lava flows!

Sedimentary (Layered Rocks)

Form when sediment (sand, mud, shells) is deposited in layers and compressed over millions of years.

Examples: Sandstone, Limestone (from shells), Shale, Coal

Fossils are almost always found in sedimentary rock!

Metamorphic (Changed Rocks)

Form when existing rocks are subjected to extreme heat and pressure deep inside Earth, changing their structure.

Examples: Marble (from limestone), Slate (from shale), Quartzite (from sandstone)

Soil -- More Than Just Dirt!

Healthy soil is one of Earth's most valuable resources. It takes hundreds of years to form just one inch of topsoil!

What's in Soil?Why It Matters
Weathered rock and mineralsProvides nutrients like iron, calcium, potassium for plants
Humus (decomposed organic matter)Rich in nutrients; holds water; makes soil dark and fertile
WaterDissolves nutrients so plants can absorb them through roots
Air (in pore spaces)Oxygen for plant roots and soil organisms
Living organismsEarthworms, bacteria, fungi -- all improve soil structure

Fossils -- Windows into the Past

How Fossils Form

  1. Organism dies and is buried quickly in sediment
  2. Soft parts decay; hard parts (bones, shells) remain
  3. Minerals slowly replace the organic material
  4. Sediment hardens to rock around the fossil
  5. Erosion eventually exposes it at the surface

Types of Fossils

  • Body fossils: actual preserved remains (bones, teeth, shells)
  • Trace fossils: evidence of activity (footprints, burrows, eggs)
  • Mould fossils: impressions left in rock
  • Amber fossils: organisms trapped in ancient tree resin

Fossils form in sedimentary rock -- layers trap and preserve remains.

Soil takes hundreds of years to form -- it is a precious resource!

Natural Resources and Conservation

Earth provides everything we need -- our job is to use it wisely!

What Are We Learning Today?

Almost everything you use every day came from Earth -- the metal in your pencil, the cotton in your shirt, the water in your cup. These are called natural resources. Some resources can be replaced quickly; others take millions of years to form. Today we learn to distinguish them and understand why conservation matters!

Key Words to Know

Natural Resource -- Anything from nature that people use: water, soil, air, plants, animals, minerals, fossil fuels.
Renewable Resource -- A resource that can be replaced naturally in a reasonable amount of time. Example: sunlight, wind, water, trees (if managed sustainably).
Nonrenewable Resource -- A resource that cannot be replaced once used because it takes millions of years to form. Example: coal, oil, natural gas, most minerals.
Fossil Fuel -- Coal, oil, and natural gas formed from ancient organisms over millions of years. Nonrenewable.
Conservation -- Using natural resources carefully and efficiently so they last longer.
Pollution -- Harmful substances released into air, water, or soil by human activities.

Renewable vs. Nonrenewable Resources

Renewable Resources

Can be replenished naturally.

  • Solar energy (sunlight)
  • Wind energy
  • Water (hydropower)
  • Trees (if replanted)
  • Geothermal energy
  • Tidal energy

Nonrenewable Resources

Take millions of years to form -- once gone, gone!

  • Coal (compressed ancient plants)
  • Oil / petroleum
  • Natural gas
  • Metals (iron, copper, gold)
  • Most minerals

How We Use Natural Resources

Trees / ForestsLumber, paper, oxygen, habitat, foodRenewable if replanted sustainably

The 3 Rs of Conservation

REDUCE

Use LESS in the first place. Turn off lights. Take shorter showers. Buy only what you need. Reducing is the most powerful of the three Rs!

REUSE

Use items again instead of throwing them away. Refillable water bottles. Cloth shopping bags. Repairing instead of replacing.

RECYCLE

Turn waste materials into new products. Paper, glass, plastic, and metals can all be recycled, saving energy and raw materials.

Washington State and Clean Energy

Washington state is a leader in clean energy! The Columbia River provides hydroelectric power -- about 70% of Washington's electricity comes from hydropower dams! Eastern Washington is also home to enormous wind farms. Washington gets more of its electricity from renewable sources than almost any other state. The goal is to reduce dependence on fossil fuels and protect the environment for future generations.
