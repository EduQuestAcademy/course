Lesson 13.1

What Living Things Need to Survive

All living things share basic needs -- discover what they are!

What Are We Learning Today?

From the tiniest ant to the tallest redwood tree to you -- all living things have basic needs that must be met to stay alive and healthy. Scientists call these needs life requirements. Today we learn what all living things need and how plants and animals meet those needs differently!

Key Words to Know

Living Thing (Organism) -- Any living creature -- bacteria, plant, fungus, or animal.
Needs -- Things an organism must have to survive and grow.
Nutrients -- Substances in food or soil that provide energy and building materials for growth.
Shelter -- A place that protects a living thing from weather and predators.
Photosynthesis -- The process plants use to make their own food using sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide.
Nonliving -- Things that have never been alive -- rocks, water, air, sunlight.

What ALL Living Things Need

Water

Every living cell needs water to function. Without it, organisms dry out and die.

Energy / Food

Animals eat food. Plants make their own food through photosynthesis using sunlight.

Air / Gases

Animals need oxygen. Plants need carbon dioxide AND oxygen for different processes.

Space / Shelter

All living things need space to grow and a safe place to live and reproduce.

How Plants and Animals Meet Their Needs Differently

NeedHow Plants Get ItHow Animals Get It

WaterAbsorb through roots from soilDrink water, eat moist food
AirTake in CO2 through tiny pores in leavesBreathe oxygen through lungs or gills

Living vs. Nonliving

Living Things:

  • Grow and develop
  • Reproduce (make more of themselves)
  • Respond to their environment
  • Need water, food, air
  • Made of cells

Nonliving Things:

  • Do not grow on their own
  • Do not reproduce
  • Do not respond to environment
  • Examples: rocks, water, air, sunlight, fire
Important: Water, sunlight, and air are NONLIVING -- but they are essential for life! They are the ingredients that living things need, but they are not alive themselves.

Real-World Connection: Washington State Forests

Washington state has incredible forests! The trees in places like the Olympic National Forest get everything they need: rainwater from the Pacific Ocean, sunlight, nutrients from the rich volcanic soil, and carbon dioxide from the air. This is why Washington forests are some of the greenest, most productive in the world!

Try It Yourself!

  1. List 4 basic needs that ALL living things share.
  2. How does a plant get its food differently than an animal?
  3. Is fire living or nonliving? It moves and grows -- but is it alive? Explain.
  4. What would happen to a plant if it got no sunlight? No water? Why?

Remember This!

All living things need: food/energy, water, air, and space/shelter.

Plants MAKE their own food. Animals FIND or HUNT their food.

Nonliving things like sunlight and water are essential ingredients for life!

Plant Parts and Their Functions

Every part of a plant has a job -- discover what each one does!

What Are We Learning Today?

A plant is like a perfectly designed machine -- every single part has a specific job to keep the plant alive and healthy. Today we explore the six main parts of a plant and what each one does, from the roots underground to the flowers at the top!

Key Words to Know

Roots -- Plant part underground that absorbs water and nutrients from soil and anchors the plant.
Stem -- The stalk that supports the plant and carries water and nutrients between roots and leaves.
Leaves -- Flat green parts where photosynthesis happens -- the plant's food factory!
Flower -- The reproductive part of a plant that produces seeds for new plants.
Fruit -- The part that forms around seeds to protect them and help them spread.
Seed -- Contains a tiny new plant (embryo) and food supply to start growing.
Chlorophyll -- The green pigment in leaves that captures sunlight for photosynthesis.

Six Plant Parts and Their Functions

StemAbove ground, main stalkSupports the plant upright; transports water and food between parts; stores food in some plants

Photosynthesis -- The Leaf's Amazing Job

The Photosynthesis Equation:

Sunlight + Water + Carbon Dioxide → Sugar (food) + Oxygen

Leaves are perfectly designed for photosynthesis: they are flat and wide to capture maximum sunlight, and they have tiny holes called stomata that let carbon dioxide in and oxygen out.

Did you know? The oxygen in the air you are breathing right now was produced by plants and trees through photosynthesis! Every breath you take depends on plants.

How Plants Grow and Reproduce

From seed to sprout to flower -- the amazing journey of plant growth!

What Are We Learning Today?

How does a tiny seed become a towering tree? Plants have a fascinating growth cycle that begins with a seed and ends with the production of new seeds. Today we learn about germination, plant growth, pollination, and the clever ways plants spread their seeds!

Key Words to Know

Germination -- When a seed starts to grow and a sprout pushes through the soil.
Sprout -- The first tiny plant that emerges from a germinating seed.
Pollination -- The transfer of pollen from one flower to another, which allows seeds to form.
Pollinator -- An animal (often a bee, butterfly, or bird) that carries pollen from flower to flower.
Seed Dispersal -- The spreading of seeds away from the parent plant to new locations.
Annual -- A plant that completes its full life cycle in one year.
Perennial -- A plant that lives for many years and blooms repeatedly.

A seed needs water, warmth, and oxygen to germinate. It does NOT need sunlight at first -- it uses the food stored inside the seed!

Pollination -- How Flowers Make Seeds

What is Pollination?

Flowers produce pollen (tiny golden grains). When pollen moves from one flower to another of the same species, seeds can form!

Who Pollinates?

  • Bees -- attracted by bright colours and nectar
  • Butterflies -- love red and orange flowers
  • Hummingbirds -- attracted to red tubular flowers
  • Wind -- carries light pollen (grass, trees)
Bees are superheroes! About 1/3 of all the food humans eat depends on bees for pollination -- including apples, almonds, blueberries, and many vegetables. Without bees, our food supply would be in serious trouble!

  2. Why do flowers have bright colours and sweet nectar?
  3. A dandelion has feathery seeds. What is its dispersal method? Why is this design clever?
  4. Find a piece of fruit (apple, orange, berry). Find the seeds inside. What type of dispersal do you think this plant uses?

Remember This!

Germination needs water, warmth, and oxygen -- not sunlight at first.

Pollination allows flowers to form seeds.

Seed dispersal: wind, animals, water, or explosion.

Animal Groups: Vertebrates and Invertebrates

Scientists sort animals into groups based on shared characteristics!

What Are We Learning Today?

There are over 8 million species of animals on Earth! Scientists organize them into groups based on shared characteristics. The most fundamental division is between animals WITH a backbone (vertebrates) and animals WITHOUT a backbone (invertebrates).

Key Words to Know

Vertebrate -- An animal WITH a backbone (spine). Example: fish, frogs, snakes, birds, mammals.
Invertebrate -- An animal WITHOUT a backbone. Example: insects, worms, spiders, crabs.
Mammal -- A warm-blooded vertebrate with hair/fur that feeds its young with milk.
Reptile -- A cold-blooded vertebrate with scales. Most lay eggs.
Amphibian -- A cold-blooded vertebrate that lives in water AND on land. Examples: frogs, salamanders.
Warm-Blooded -- Body temperature stays the same regardless of the environment (mammals and birds).
Cold-Blooded -- Body temperature changes with the environment (fish, reptiles, amphibians).

The Five Groups of Vertebrates

MammalsWarm-blooded, hair/fur, feed young milk, breathe airDog, whale, bat, human, bear, deer
BirdsWarm-blooded, feathers, wings, lay eggs, breathe airEagle, penguin, robin, ostrich, owl
ReptilesCold-blooded, dry scales, most lay eggs, breathe airSnake, turtle, lizard, crocodile, gecko
AmphibiansCold-blooded, moist skin, hatch in water, breathe through skin and lungsFrog, toad, salamander, axolotl
FishCold-blooded, scales, gills (breathe underwater), finsSalmon, shark, goldfish, tuna

Invertebrates -- The Largest Group!

About 97% of all animal species are invertebrates! They have no backbone but may have other support structures like shells or exoskeletons.

Insects

6 legs, 3 body parts (head, thorax, abdomen), most have wings. Examples: bees, butterflies, beetles, ants

Arachnids

8 legs, 2 body parts. Examples: spiders, scorpions, ticks, mites

Crustaceans

Hard shell (exoskeleton), usually in water. Examples: crabs, lobsters, shrimp

Worms & Others

Earthworms, jellyfish, snails, sea stars, sponges

  2. What makes mammals different from all other vertebrate groups?
  3. A frog starts life in water and moves to land as an adult. Which vertebrate group is this?
  4. How many legs do insects have? How is this different from arachnids?

Remember This!

Vertebrates = have a backbone (mammals, birds, reptiles, amphibians, fish)

Invertebrates = no backbone (insects, spiders, worms, crabs) -- 97% of all animals!

Mammals: warm-blooded + hair + milk. Birds: warm-blooded + feathers.

Animal Adaptations

Special features that help animals survive in their environment!

What Are We Learning Today?

Why does a polar bear have thick white fur? Why does a cactus store water? Why does a cheetah have spots? Every organism has special features called adaptations that help it survive in its specific environment. Adaptations develop over thousands of generations and are perfectly matched to where the animal lives!

Key Words to Know

Adaptation -- A physical feature or behavior that helps an organism survive in its environment.
Physical Adaptation -- A body part or feature that helps survival. Example: sharp claws, thick fur, long beak.
Behavioral Adaptation -- An action that helps survival. Example: migrating, hibernating, camouflage.
Camouflage -- Colouring or patterns that help an animal blend into its surroundings.
Mimicry -- When a harmless animal looks like a dangerous one to fool predators.
Hibernation -- A deep winter sleep that conserves energy when food is scarce.
Migration -- Seasonal movement to a warmer location for food and warmth.

Physical Adaptations

Polar BearThick white fur, layer of fatKeeps warm in Arctic cold; white fur = camouflage in snow
DuckWebbed feet, waterproof feathersWebbed feet for swimming; feathers repel water
EagleSharp talons, hooked beakCatch and tear apart prey

Behavioral Adaptations

Hibernation

Bears, groundhogs, and some bats sleep through winter when food is scarce. Heart rate and body temperature drop dramatically to save energy.

Migration

Gray whales travel 10,000+ miles each year. Arctic terns travel pole to pole! Birds fly south for winter where food and warmth are available.

Camouflage Behaviour

Arctic foxes, snowshoe hares, and ptarmigan birds change colour white in winter -- perfect camouflage against snow!

Washington State Connection: Pacific Salmon

Pacific salmon have incredible adaptations! They hatch in freshwater rivers in Washington, migrate to the Pacific Ocean to grow, then return to the EXACT river where they were born to spawn (reproduce). They can detect the unique chemical signature of their home river. After spawning, they die -- but their bodies fertilise the forest, feeding bears, eagles, and even the trees!

Try It Yourself!

  1. Describe TWO adaptations of a duck and explain how each one helps it survive.
  2. What is the difference between camouflage and mimicry?
  3. Why do some animals hibernate instead of migrating? What are the advantages of each?
  4. Think of an animal you know. Describe ONE physical and ONE behavioral adaptation it has.

Remember This!

Physical adaptations: body parts that help survival (claws, fur, beaks).

Behavioral adaptations: actions that help survival (hibernate, migrate, camouflage).

Adaptations develop over many generations to match the environment!

Life Cycles

The stages every living thing goes through from birth to reproduction!

What Are We Learning Today?

Every living thing goes through a series of stages from birth to death called a life cycle. Some life cycles are short (a housefly lives about 28 days), others are extraordinarily long (some bristlecone pine trees are over 5,000 years old!). Today we explore the life cycles of plants, insects, frogs, and mammals.

Key Words to Know

Life Cycle -- The series of stages an organism goes through from birth to death.
Metamorphosis -- A dramatic change in body form during a life cycle.
Complete Metamorphosis -- Four stages: egg, larva, pupa, adult. (Butterflies, beetles, bees)
Incomplete Metamorphosis -- Three stages: egg, nymph, adult. (Grasshoppers, dragonflies)
Larva -- The worm-like stage after hatching (caterpillar, maggot, grub).
Pupa -- The resting, transforming stage inside a cocoon or chrysalis.
