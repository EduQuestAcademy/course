Lesson 5.1

Nouns: Common, Proper, Abstract, and Plural

Nouns name people, places, things, and ideas -- they are everywhere!

What Are We Learning Today?

A noun is a word that names a person, place, thing, or idea. Nouns are one of the most important parts of speech -- almost every sentence has at least one! Today we learn about four types of nouns and how to make them plural.

Key Words to Know

Noun -- A word that names a person, place, thing, or idea.
Common Noun -- A general name for a person, place, or thing. NOT capitalised. Example: city, dog, teacher.
Proper Noun -- The specific name of a person, place, or thing. Always CAPITALISED. Example: Seattle, Sage Crest Elementary.
Abstract Noun -- A noun that names an idea or feeling you cannot touch. Example: happiness, freedom, courage.
Plural Noun -- More than one of something. Usually formed by adding -s or -es.

Four Types of Nouns

Common Nouns

General names -- NOT capitalised.

city, school, dog, teacher, river, book, country

Proper Nouns

Specific names -- ALWAYS capitalised.

Seattle, Sage Crest Elementary, Fido, Columbia River, Washington

Abstract Nouns

Ideas and feelings you cannot touch.

happiness, freedom, courage, love, justice, childhood, peace

Forming Plural Nouns

RuleHow to Form PluralExamples
Most nounsAdd -scat → cats, book → books, dog → dogs
Ends in -s, -sh, -ch, -x, -zAdd -esbus → buses, dish → dishes, box → boxes
Ends in consonant + yChange y to i, add -esbaby → babies, city → cities, party → parties
Ends in -f or -feChange to -vesleaf → leaves, knife → knives, wolf → wolves
Irregular pluralsCompletely different word!child → children, mouse → mice, foot → feet, tooth → teeth
Remember: Proper nouns are ALWAYS capitalised -- even in the middle of a sentence! Common nouns are only capitalised at the beginning of a sentence.

Try It Yourself!

  1. Find THREE common nouns and THREE proper nouns in a book or article you are reading.
  2. Write the plural of these nouns: church, baby, leaf, child, fox, wish
  3. Write a sentence using ONE abstract noun (happiness, courage, kindness, etc.).

Remember This!

Common -- general, not capitalised  |  Proper -- specific, ALWAYS capitalised

Abstract -- ideas and feelings you cannot touch

Most plurals add -s or -es, but watch out for irregular plurals!

Lesson 5.2

Pronouns and Pronoun-Antecedent Agreement

Use pronouns correctly so your writing is clear and easy to follow!

What Are We Learning Today?

A pronoun replaces a noun so we do not have to repeat the same word over and over. Instead of "Maya walked to the store. Maya bought Maya's favourite snack," we say "Maya walked to the store. She bought her favourite snack." Today we learn about pronouns and how to make sure they match the nouns they replace!

Key Words to Know

Pronoun -- A word that replaces a noun. Example: he, she, they, it, we, I, you.
Antecedent -- The noun that a pronoun replaces. The pronoun must match it!
Pronoun-Antecedent Agreement -- The pronoun must match the noun it replaces in number (singular/plural) and gender.
Possessive Pronoun -- A pronoun that shows ownership: my, your, his, her, its, our, their.

Common Pronouns

TypeSingularPlural
Subject PronounsI, you, he, she, itwe, you, they
Object Pronounsme, you, him, her, itus, you, them
Possessive Pronounsmy, your, his, her, itsour, your, their

Pronoun-Antecedent Agreement

The pronoun MUST match the noun it replaces. If the noun is singular, use a singular pronoun. If plural, use a plural pronoun.

"Maya forgot her backpack." (Maya = singular, her = singular -- they match!)
"Maya forgot their backpack." (Maya = singular, their = plural -- they do NOT match!)
"The boys played outside. They had a great time." (boys = plural, they = plural -- they match!)
"The boys played outside. He had a great time." (boys = plural, he = singular -- mismatch!)

Try It Yourself!

  1. Replace the underlined nouns with correct pronouns: "Sam left Sam's lunch at home. Sam was hungry all day."
  2. Fix the pronoun error: "The students finished their projects. He was very proud."
  3. Write three sentences using three different pronouns (he, she, they).

Remember This!

Pronouns replace nouns so we avoid repetition.

The pronoun must agree with the noun it replaces -- singular matches singular, plural matches plural!

Lesson 5.3

Verbs: Regular, Irregular, and Tenses

Verbs show action and tell us WHEN something happens!

What Are We Learning Today?

A verb is the action word in a sentence -- it tells us what someone or something does, is, or has. Verbs also tell us WHEN something happens through tense (past, present, or future). Today we learn about regular verbs, tricky irregular verbs, and the three simple tenses.

Key Words to Know

Verb -- A word that shows action or a state of being.
Regular Verb -- A verb that forms its past tense by adding -ed. Example: walk → walked.
Irregular Verb -- A verb that changes completely in the past tense. Example: run → ran, go → went.
Tense -- The form of a verb that shows WHEN the action happens (past, present, or future).

The Three Simple Tenses

TenseWhenHow to FormExample
PastAlready happenedAdd -ed (regular) OR change the word (irregular)I walked. / I ran.
PresentHappening nowBase form (add -s for he/she/it)I walk. / She walks.
FutureWill happen laterwill + base verbI will walk. / She will run.

Common Irregular Verbs -- Memorise These!

"Yesterday, she ran to the park." (ran = correct irregular past tense of run)
"Yesterday, she runned to the park." (runned = WRONG -- run is irregular!)

Try It Yourself!

  1. Write the past tense of these verbs: jump, swim, talk, bring, see, go, eat, write
  2. Write one sentence in each tense (past, present, future) using the verb "play."
  3. Find THREE irregular verbs in a book or story you are reading.

Remember This!

Regular verbs add -ed in the past tense.

Irregular verbs change completely -- memorise them!

Past = already happened  |  Present = now  |  Future = will happen

Lesson 5.4

Adjectives: Comparative and Superlative

Adjectives describe nouns -- and comparatives help us compare things!

What Are We Learning Today?

An adjective is a word that describes a noun -- it tells us what something is like. Adjectives make our writing more vivid and interesting. Today we also learn about comparative adjectives (comparing two things) and superlative adjectives (comparing three or more).

Key Words to Know

Adjective -- A word that describes a noun. Example: tall, blue, happy, enormous, cold.
Comparative Adjective -- Compares TWO things. Usually add -er or use "more." Example: taller, more beautiful.
Superlative Adjective -- Compares THREE or MORE things. Usually add -est or use "most." Example: tallest, most beautiful.

Forming Comparative and Superlative Adjectives

AdjectiveComparative (2 things)Superlative (3+ things)
talltallertallest
fastfasterfastest
happyhappierhappiest
bigbiggerbiggest
beautifulmore beautifulmost beautiful
excitingmore excitingmost exciting
goodbetterbest
badworseworst
Rules: Short adjectives (1-2 syllables) usually add -er/-est. Long adjectives (3+ syllables) usually use more/most. Watch for doubles: big → bigger (double the g!). Watch for y → i: happy → happier.
"She is taller than her brother." (comparing two people)
"He is the tallest student in the class." (comparing three or more)
"She is the most tallest." (NEVER use -est AND most together!)

Try It Yourself!

  1. Write the comparative and superlative forms of: small, clever, funny, bad, cold
  2. Write two sentences using comparative adjectives to compare two animals.
  3. Write one sentence using a superlative adjective to describe the best or worst thing about school.

Remember This!

Adjectives describe nouns.

Comparative (-er / more) -- comparing TWO things.

Superlative (-est / most) -- comparing THREE or MORE things.

NEVER use -est and "most" together -- that is a double superlative error!

Lesson 5.5

Adverbs

Adverbs tell us HOW, WHEN, WHERE, and HOW OFTEN something happens!

What Are We Learning Today?

If adjectives describe nouns, adverbs describe verbs, adjectives, and other adverbs! They answer questions like HOW? WHEN? WHERE? and HOW OFTEN? Many adverbs end in -ly, but not all of them do.

Key Words to Know

Adverb -- A word that describes a verb, adjective, or another adverb.
Manner Adverb -- Tells HOW something is done. Most end in -ly. Example: quickly, carefully, loudly.
Time Adverb -- Tells WHEN something happens. Example: yesterday, soon, now, later, already.
Place Adverb -- Tells WHERE something happens. Example: here, there, outside, nearby, everywhere.
Frequency Adverb -- Tells HOW OFTEN something happens. Example: always, never, sometimes, often, rarely.

Types of Adverbs with Examples

TypeQuestion It AnswersExamples
MannerHow?quickly, slowly, carefully, loudly, gently, happily
TimeWhen?yesterday, today, soon, now, later, already, finally
PlaceWhere?here, there, outside, nearby, everywhere, inside
FrequencyHow often?always, never, sometimes, often, rarely, usually

Adjective vs. Adverb -- Know the Difference!

Adjective -- describes a NOUN

"She is a quick runner."

Quick describes the noun "runner."

Adverb -- describes a VERB

"She runs quickly."

Quickly describes the verb "runs" -- tells HOW she runs.

Try It Yourself!

  1. Add an adverb to each sentence to make it more interesting: "The dog barked." / "She finished her homework." / "It rained."
  2. Identify whether each underlined word is an ADJECTIVE or ADVERB: "She spoke softly." / "He is a soft speaker." / "They never give up."
  3. Write four sentences -- one using each type of adverb (manner, time, place, frequency).

Remember This!

Adverbs describe verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs.

They answer: How? When? Where? How often?

Many adverbs end in -ly -- but not all! (soon, here, never, always)

Conjunctions: Coordinating and Subordinating

Conjunctions join ideas together -- they are the glue of sentences!

What Are We Learning Today?

A conjunction is a joining word that connects words, phrases, or clauses. Without conjunctions, we could only write short, choppy sentences. With them, we can build longer, more interesting sentences that show how ideas are related!

Key Words to Know

Conjunction -- A word that joins words, phrases, or clauses together.
Coordinating Conjunction -- Joins two equal parts (words, phrases, or independent clauses). Remembered by FANBOYS.
Subordinating Conjunction -- Joins a dependent clause to a main clause, showing a relationship like cause, time, or condition.
FANBOYS -- A memory tool for coordinating conjunctions: For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So.

Coordinating Conjunctions -- FANBOYS

ConjunctionUseExample
ForGives a reasonShe stayed home, for she was ill.
AndAdds ideas togetherI like cats and dogs.
NorNeither optionHe neither ran nor walked.
ButShows contrastI was tired, but I kept going.
OrShows a choiceDo you want juice or water?
YetShows contrast/surpriseIt was cold, yet she swam.
SoShows resultIt rained, so we stayed inside.

Common Subordinating Conjunctions

becausesincealthoughwhilewhenbeforeafterifunlessuntileven thoughas soon as
"Because it was raining, we stayed inside." (because shows REASON)
"Although she was tired, she finished her homework." (although shows CONTRAST)
"We will leave when the bell rings." (when shows TIME)

Try It Yourself!

  1. Join these two sentences using a coordinating conjunction: "I was hungry." + "There was no food in the fridge."
  2. Complete these sentences with a subordinating conjunction: "___ it snowed, school was cancelled." / "She studied hard ___ she wanted to pass."
  3. Write three sentences using three different FANBOYS conjunctions.

Remember This!

Coordinating conjunctions -- FANBOYS (For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So)

Subordinating conjunctions -- because, although, when, if, since, unless...

Conjunctions are the glue that joins ideas in a sentence!

Capitalization and Punctuation

Learn the rules for capital letters and punctuation marks!

What Are We Learning Today?

Capitalisation and punctuation are like the traffic signs of writing -- they tell the reader when to stop, pause, ask a question, or show excitement. Without them, writing would be impossible to read! Today we review the most important rules every 3rd-grade writer needs to know.

Key Words to Know

Capitalisation -- Using a capital (uppercase) letter at the start of specific words.
Punctuation -- Marks used in writing to organise sentences and show meaning (. , ! ? " ' ...).
Comma -- A mark (,) used to create a pause, separate items in a list, or join clauses.
Apostrophe -- A mark (') used to show ownership (Maya's book) or a contraction (don't = do not).

When to Use Capital Letters

Names of placesKennewick, Washington, Sage Crest Elementary
Days and monthsMonday, January, Thursday
Holidays and special eventsThanksgiving, Independence Day
Titles before namesPresident Lincoln, Mrs. Jones

Try It Yourself!

  1. Fix the capitalisation errors: "my friend james lives in portland, oregon. every friday we play soccer."
  2. Add the correct punctuation: "Where are you going _ she asked _ I am going to the park _ he replied"
  3. Write a sentence using: a proper noun, a comma in a list, and an apostrophe for ownership.

Remember This!

Capitalise: sentence starts, the word I, proper nouns, days and months, titles.

Use a period for statements, question mark for questions, exclamation for excitement.

Use commas in lists, apostrophes for ownership and contractions.

Spelling: Suffixes and High-Frequency Words

Master spelling patterns and the words that appear most often in writing!

What Are We Learning Today?

Good spelling makes your writing clear and professional. Today we review important spelling rules for adding suffixes and practise the high-frequency words that appear most often in reading and writing. Knowing these patterns and words will make you a much more confident writer!

Key Words to Know

Spelling Pattern -- A rule or group of letters that appear together in many words.
Double the Consonant -- When a short-vowel word ends in one consonant, double it before adding -ed or -ing. Example: run → running.
Drop the E -- When a word ends in silent e, drop the e before adding -ed or -ing. Example: hope → hoping.
Change Y to I -- When a word ends in consonant + y, change y to i before adding -es or -ed. Example: carry → carried.

Three Important Spelling Rules for Suffixes

Rule 1: Double the Consonant

Short vowel + single consonant at end = double before -ed/-ing

run → running, sit → sitting, hop → hopped, big → bigger, swim → swimming

Rule 2: Drop the Silent E

Word ends in silent e = drop the e before -ed/-ing

hope → hoping, bake → baking, smile → smiled, write → writing, ride → riding

Rule 3: Change Y to I

Consonant + y at end = change y to i before -es/-ed

carry → carried, baby → babies, happy → happier, try → tried, cry → cried
