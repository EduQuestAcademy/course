Lesson 16.1

Matter: Properties and States

Everything around you is made of matter -- learn to describe and classify it!

What Are We Learning Today?

Look around you -- your chair, the air you breathe, the water in your glass. All of it is matter! Matter is anything that has mass and takes up space. Today we learn to describe matter using its properties and understand the three states matter can exist in.

Key Words to Know

Matter -- Anything that has mass and takes up space. Everything physical is made of matter!
Mass -- The amount of matter in an object. Measured in grams or kilograms.
Volume -- The amount of space matter takes up.
Property -- A characteristic that describes matter: colour, texture, hardness, flexibility, conductivity.
Solid -- A state of matter with a definite shape and definite volume. Particles are tightly packed.
Liquid -- A state of matter with a definite volume but no definite shape (takes the shape of its container).
Gas -- A state of matter with no definite shape or volume. Spreads to fill any container.

The Three States of Matter

SOLID

Definite shape + definite volume

Particles are tightly packed and vibrate in place. Strong forces hold them together.

Examples: rock, ice, wood, metal, bone

LIQUID

No definite shape + definite volume

Particles are close but can slide past each other. Takes the shape of its container.

Examples: water, juice, milk, lava, mercury

GAS

No definite shape + no definite volume

Particles move rapidly and spread far apart. Fills any container completely.

Examples: air, steam, oxygen, helium, carbon dioxide

Matter is Made of Atoms!

Everything is made of incredibly tiny particles called atoms. Atoms are so small that millions of them could fit on the period at the end of this sentence! Different types of atoms make up different materials. In a solid, atoms are packed tightly and barely move. In a liquid, they slide around. In a gas, they bounce around rapidly in all directions!

Try It Yourself!

  1. Classify each as solid, liquid, or gas: ice cube, steam from a kettle, honey, a rock, the air in a balloon.
  2. Name THREE physical properties of a wooden pencil.
  3. If you pour water into a square container and then into a round container, the water changes shape but not volume. Which state of matter does this describe?
  4. What is the difference between mass and volume?

Remember This!

Matter = anything with mass and volume.

Solid: definite shape + volume. Liquid: definite volume, no definite shape.

Gas: no definite shape or volume -- expands to fill any container.

Lesson 16.2

Changes in Matter: Physical and Chemical

Matter can change -- but some changes can be undone and some cannot!

What Are We Learning Today?

Matter is always changing! Ice melts, wood burns, bread rises. But not all changes are equal. Some changes only alter the form of matter (physical changes), while others create an entirely new substance (chemical changes). Learning to tell them apart is a key science skill!

Key Words to Know

Physical Change -- A change that alters the form or appearance of matter but does NOT create a new substance. Usually reversible.
Chemical Change -- A change that creates a new substance with different properties. Usually irreversible.
Reversible -- A change that can be undone. Example: melting ice can be refrozen.
Irreversible -- A change that CANNOT be undone. Example: you cannot un-burn wood.
Mixture -- Two or more substances combined but NOT chemically joined. They can be separated.
Solution -- A special mixture where one substance is completely dissolved in another (like salt in water).

Physical Changes -- Same Substance, Different Form

Physical Change Examples:

Chemical Changes -- New Substance Created!

Signs of a Chemical Change:

  • Colour change (unexpected)
  • Gas bubbles produced
  • Light or heat released
  • New smell produced
  • Solid forms in liquid (precipitate)
  • Cannot easily be reversed

Try It Yourself!

  1. Classify each as physical or chemical change: toasting bread, folding a piece of paper, mixing vinegar and baking soda, melting butter, rusting metal, cutting a watermelon.
  2. What are THREE signs that a chemical change has occurred?
  3. Why is burning wood a chemical change but melting ice only a physical change?
  4. Can you reverse a chemical change? Give an example to support your answer.

Remember This!

Physical change: same substance, different form. Usually reversible.

Chemical change: NEW substance created. Usually irreversible.

Signs of chemical change: bubbles, colour change, heat, light, new smell.

Lesson 16.3

Forces: Push, Pull, and Gravity

Forces make things move, stop, or change direction -- discover how!

What Are We Learning Today?

Why does a ball fall when you drop it? Why does a door open when you pull it? The answer is forces! A force is a push or a pull that acts on an object. Forces are everywhere -- and understanding them explains how everything in our world moves. Today we explore forces including the most powerful of all: gravity!

Key Words to Know

Force -- A push or a pull that acts on an object and can change its motion, shape, or direction.
Gravity -- A force that pulls all objects toward each other. Earth's gravity pulls everything toward its centre.
Friction -- A force that opposes motion when two surfaces rub against each other. Slows things down.
Applied Force -- A force directly applied by a person or object. Example: pushing a box, kicking a ball.
Balanced Forces -- Equal forces in opposite directions. Object does not move (no net force).
Unbalanced Forces -- Unequal forces that cause an object to move, change speed, or change direction.
Net Force -- The overall force after all forces are combined.

Forces Change Motion

Force AppliedEffect on ObjectReal-World Example

Push backward (friction)Object slows down and stopsBrakes on a bike
Force from the sideObject changes directionWind blowing a kite sideways
Gravity downwardObject falls toward EarthDropping a book
Balanced forcesObject stays stillBook sitting on a table

Gravity -- The Universal Force

What is Gravity?

Gravity is a force of attraction between all objects with mass. The MORE mass an object has, the STRONGER its gravitational pull. Earth is so massive that its gravity pulls everything toward its centre.

Gravity Facts

  • Keeps you on the ground
  • Makes things fall
  • Keeps the Moon orbiting Earth
  • Keeps Earth orbiting the Sun
  • Works over any distance!

Friction -- The Force That Slows Things Down

High Friction (rough surfaces)

Lots of resistance. Objects slow down quickly.

Examples: sandpaper on wood, rubber sole on concrete, car tyres on road

Low Friction (smooth surfaces)

Less resistance. Objects slide easily.

Examples: ice skates on ice, ball bearings in a wheel, lubricated engine parts

Friction is both helpful and harmful! Helpful: car brakes, grip on floors, pencil writing on paper. Harmful: wears out tyres and shoes, slows machines down, causes heat in engines.

Try It Yourself!

  1. Name TWO examples each of a push force and a pull force in everyday life.
  2. A book is sitting still on a table. Are the forces balanced or unbalanced? How do you know?
  3. Why do cars need both engine force AND brakes? What force does each one create?
  4. Why would it be impossible to walk on perfectly frictionless ground? What would happen?

Remember This!

A force = a push or a pull that changes motion.

Gravity pulls all objects toward Earth's centre.

Friction opposes motion and slows objects down.

Balanced forces = no movement. Unbalanced forces = movement!

Lesson 16.4

Motion and Magnetism

Describe how things move and discover the invisible force of magnets!

What Are We Learning Today?

Motion is everywhere -- a bouncing ball, a spinning wheel, a flowing river. Scientists describe motion using speed, direction, and position. And then there is magnetism -- a mysterious force that acts at a distance, pushing and pulling without even touching! Today we explore both.

Key Words to Know

Motion -- A change in the position of an object over time.
Speed -- How fast an object moves. Calculated as distance divided by time.
Direction -- The way an object is moving (forward, backward, up, down, left, right).
Magnet -- An object that produces a magnetic force, attracting certain metals.
Magnetic Poles -- The two ends of a magnet (north pole and south pole) where the force is strongest.
Attract -- When opposite poles of magnets pull toward each other (N + S attract).
Repel -- When like poles of magnets push away from each other (N + N or S + S repel).
Magnetic Field -- The invisible area around a magnet where its force acts.

Describing Motion

ConceptWhat It MeansExample
PositionWhere an object is located"The ball is 3 metres to the left of the goal"
SpeedHow fast = distance / timeA cheetah runs 70 mph; a snail moves 0.03 mph
DirectionWhich way it movesNorth, south, up, clockwise
AccelerationSpeeding up or slowing downA car speeding up at a green light
VibrationBack-and-forth motionA guitar string vibrating to make sound

Magnetism -- The Invisible Force

The Rules of Magnets

Opposite poles attract:

North + South = PULL together

Like poles repel:

North + North = PUSH apart

South + South = PUSH apart

Magnetic Materials

NOT all metals are magnetic! Only:

  • Iron (and steel)
  • Nickel
  • Cobalt

NOT magnetic: aluminium, copper, gold, plastic, wood, glass

Magnets in Real Life

ApplicationHow Magnetism Is Used
CompassA magnetised needle points to Earth's magnetic north pole
Electric motorsMagnets create rotation to power fans, cars, and appliances
MRI machinesPowerful magnets create detailed images inside the human body
Maglev trainsMagnetic repulsion lifts the train, eliminating friction!
Fridge magnetsMagnetic strip attaches to iron in the fridge door
Credit cardsMagnetic strip stores data

Earth is a Giant Magnet!

Earth has its own magnetic field generated by the movement of liquid iron in its outer core. This field creates a north and south magnetic pole. Compass needles align with Earth's magnetic field, always pointing toward magnetic north -- which is how navigation worked before GPS was invented!

Try It Yourself!

  1. If you hold a north pole of one magnet next to the north pole of another, what happens? Why?
  2. Which of these are magnetic: a steel paper clip, a copper penny, a iron nail, a plastic button?
  3. A car travels 60 miles in 1 hour. What is its speed?
  4. How does a compass work? What force is involved?

Remember This!

Opposite poles ATTRACT. Like poles REPEL.

Only iron, nickel, and cobalt are attracted to magnets.

Speed = distance / time.

Earth has its own magnetic field -- that is why compasses work!

Lesson 16.5

Forms of Energy

Energy makes everything happen -- discover its many amazing forms!

What Are We Learning Today?

Energy is the ability to do work or cause change. Without energy, nothing in the universe would move, grow, or change. Energy comes in many forms and -- crucially -- it can change from one form to another but it is NEVER created or destroyed. This is the Law of Conservation of Energy!

Key Words to Know

Energy -- The ability to do work or cause change.
Kinetic Energy -- The energy of motion. Anything moving has kinetic energy.
Potential Energy -- Stored energy. An object has potential energy because of its position or condition.
Thermal Energy -- Heat energy. The total kinetic energy of all the particles in a substance.
Light Energy -- Energy carried by light waves (electromagnetic radiation).
Sound Energy -- Energy carried by vibrations through matter.
Electrical Energy -- Energy from moving electrons through a conductor.
Chemical Energy -- Energy stored in the bonds of chemicals (food, fuel, batteries).

The Major Forms of Energy

Kinetic (motion) Potential (stored) Thermal (heat) Light Sound Electrical Chemical
Form of EnergyDescriptionReal-World Examples
KineticEnergy of motion -- faster + heavier = moreRunning, a rolling ball, flowing water
PotentialStored energy ready to be releasedA stretched rubber band, water behind a dam, a raised hammer
Thermal (Heat)Energy from moving particles inside matterA campfire, the sun's warmth, cooking on a stove
LightEnergy that travels as electromagnetic wavesSunlight, lamp light, laser, firefly
SoundEnergy that travels as vibrations through matterMusic, thunder, voices, a barking dog
ElectricalEnergy from moving electronsLightning, wall outlets, batteries, electric eels
ChemicalEnergy stored in chemical bondsFood (calories), gasoline, batteries, wood

Energy Transfers and Transformations

Energy constantly transforms from one form to another:

Solar Panel

Light energy → Electrical energy

Light Bulb

Electrical energy → Light energy + Heat energy

Human Body

Chemical energy (food) → Kinetic energy (movement) + Heat energy

Hydroelectric Dam

Potential energy (stored water) → Kinetic energy (flowing water) → Electrical energy

The Big Rule: Energy is NEVER created or destroyed -- it only changes form! This is the Law of Conservation of Energy. When you eat food, the chemical energy in it does not disappear -- it becomes kinetic energy (movement) and thermal energy (body heat).

Try It Yourself!

  1. A roller coaster is at the TOP of a hill before descending. Does it have more kinetic or potential energy at the top? What about at the bottom?
  2. When you turn on a flashlight, what energy transformation occurs?
  3. Name the form of energy stored in: food, a stretched bow, a moving car, a campfire.
  4. Washington state gets much of its electricity from hydroelectric dams. Trace the energy transformations from the original source (sun) to your light switch.

Remember This!

Energy is never created or destroyed -- it only changes form!

Kinetic = energy of motion. Potential = stored energy.

Energy can transform: light → electrical → heat → sound → motion!

Lesson 16.6

Light, Sound, and Heat

Three essential forms of energy that shape our everyday experience!

What Are We Learning Today?

Light lets us see. Sound lets us communicate. Heat keeps us alive. These three forms of energy are among the most important in our daily lives. Today we take a deeper look at how each one works, how it travels, and how we experience it every single day!

Key Words to Know

Reflection -- When light bounces off a surface.
Refraction -- When light bends as it passes from one material to another (e.g., water to air).
Absorption -- When a material takes in light (or heat) rather than reflecting it.
Vibration -- The rapid back-and-forth movement that creates sound waves.
Pitch -- How high or low a sound is, determined by the frequency (speed) of vibration.
Volume -- How loud or soft a sound is, determined by the amplitude (size) of vibration.
Conduction -- Transfer of heat through direct contact between materials.
Convection -- Transfer of heat through the movement of a fluid (liquid or gas).
Radiation -- Transfer of heat through electromagnetic waves without requiring matter. (How the sun heats Earth!)

Light Energy

How Light Travels

Light travels as waves at 186,000 miles per SECOND -- the fastest speed in the universe! Light can travel through a vacuum (empty space) -- that is how sunlight reaches Earth.

Reflection, Refraction, Absorption

Reflection: mirrors, shiny surfaces bounce light back.

Refraction: a straw looks bent in water -- light bends!

Absorption: black objects absorb light (get hot). White reflects it (stays cool).

Rainbows! White light is actually ALL colours mixed together. When light passes through water droplets or a prism, it refracts and separates into its component colours: Red, Orange, Yellow, Green, Blue, Indigo, Violet (ROY G BIV)!

Sound Energy

How Sound Travels

Sound is created by vibrations. Vibrating objects push and pull nearby air particles, creating waves that travel outward in all directions.

Sound travels faster through liquids and solids than through air! And sound CANNOT travel through a vacuum (no particles to vibrate -- space is silent!).

Pitch and Volume

High pitch = fast vibrations (piccolo, whistle)

Low pitch = slow vibrations (bass guitar, thunder)

Loud = large vibrations (concert speaker)

Soft = small vibrations (whisper)

Heat Energy (Thermal Energy)

Conduction

Heat transfers through DIRECT CONTACT. Metals are great conductors -- that is why metal spoons get hot in hot soup!

Wood and plastic are poor conductors (insulators) -- great for pot handles!

Convection

Heat transfers through moving fluids. Warm air or water rises; cooler air or water sinks, creating a circulation pattern called a convection current.

Why the top of a room is warmer than the bottom!

Radiation

Heat transfers as electromagnetic waves -- no matter needed! This is the ONLY way heat can travel through space.

The sun heats Earth through 93 million miles of empty space by radiation!

Real-World Connections

Light: Solar panels capture light energy and convert it to electricity -- powering homes across Washington state. Sound: Ultrasound uses high-frequency sound waves to create images inside the human body (used in hospitals!). Heat: Washington's geothermal energy harnesses heat from inside the Earth. Understanding light, sound, and heat opens doors to all of modern technology!

Try It Yourself!

  1. Why does a straw look bent when placed in a glass of water? What property of light causes this?
  2. What would happen to sound in outer space? Why?
  3. Which type of heat transfer warms you when you sit next to a campfire but don't touch it?
  4. Why do dark-coloured cars get hotter in sunlight than light-coloured cars? Use the word "absorption" in your answer.

YOU HAVE COMPLETED ALL OF SCIENCE! Congratulations!

Light: reflects, refracts, and is absorbed. White light = all colours!

Sound: needs matter to travel. High pitch = fast vibrations.

Heat transfers by: Conduction (contact) + Convection (fluid) + Radiation (waves)
