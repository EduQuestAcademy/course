Lesson 3.1

Prefixes and Suffixes

Unlock the meaning of hundreds of new words by learning word parts!

What Are We Learning Today?

Did you know that knowing just a few word parts can help you figure out the meaning of hundreds of new words? Today we learn about prefixes (word parts added to the beginning) and suffixes (word parts added to the end) and how they change the meaning of words!

Key Words to Know

Prefix -- A word part added to the BEGINNING of a word that changes its meaning. Example: un- in unhappy.
Suffix -- A word part added to the END of a word that changes its meaning. Example: -ful in careful.
Base Word -- The main word before any prefixes or suffixes are added. Example: "happy" in unhappy.
Word Part -- Any piece of a word (prefix, suffix, or root) that carries meaning.

How Prefixes and Suffixes Change Meaning

Base Word: HAPPY

happy -- feeling joy

unhappy -- NOT happy

happiness -- the state of being happy

happily -- in a happy way

Base Word: CARE

care -- to feel concern

careful -- full of care

careless -- without care

carefully -- in a careful way

Base Word: WRITE

write -- to put words down

rewrite -- to write again

writer -- a person who writes

writing -- the act of writing

Strategy: When you see a long word you do not know, look for a prefix or suffix first! Cover it up and read the base word. Then add back the prefix or suffix and think about what it adds to the meaning.

  4. teacher -- What is the suffix? What does it tell us about a teacher?
  5. Now make YOUR OWN word using a prefix or suffix from the tables above!

Remember This!

PREFIX -- added to the BEGINNING (un-, re-, pre-, dis-, mis-)

SUFFIX -- added to the END (-ful, -less, -er, -ly, -ness)

Cover the prefix or suffix and read the base word first, then add the meaning back!

Lesson 3.2

Root Words and Word Families

One root word can grow into a whole family of related words!

What Are We Learning Today?

Imagine a tree. It has one trunk, but it grows many branches, leaves, and flowers. Words work the same way! One root word can grow into a whole family of related words. Learning one root word helps you understand many words at once!

Key Words to Know

Root Word -- The basic form of a word, before any prefixes or suffixes are added.
Word Family -- A group of related words that all share the same root word.
Related Words -- Words that share the same root and have connected meanings.
Latin Root -- A word part from the Latin language that forms the base of many English words.

Word Families -- One Root, Many Words!

Root: PLAY

play plays played playing player playful playground replay

Root: HELP

Root: FRIEND

Using Root Words as Clues

Knowing a root word helps you figure out unfamiliar words. Look at this example:

You see the word: interrupt

You know: rupt means "break" and inter means "between"

So interrupt = to break between (someone's speech or activity)

You figured it out without a dictionary!

Try It Yourself!

  1. Write ALL the words you can make from the root word HOPE. (hint: hopeful, hopeless...)
  2. Write ALL the words you can make from the root word KIND.
  3. The word "portable" contains the root "port" (carry). What does "portable" mean?
  4. The word "auditorium" contains the root "aud" (hear). What do you think people do in an auditorium?

Remember This!

A ROOT WORD is the basic form of a word -- the trunk of the word tree!

A WORD FAMILY is all the related words that grow from that root.

Knowing one root helps you understand many words at once!

Lesson 3.3

Multisyllable Words

Break big words into smaller parts and read them with confidence!

What Are We Learning Today?

Have you ever seen a really long word and felt nervous about reading it? Don't worry! Every long word is just a collection of smaller parts called syllables. When you break a big word into syllables, it becomes much easier to read and understand!

Key Words to Know

Syllable -- A word part that has one vowel sound. Every syllable has exactly one vowel sound.
Multisyllable Word -- A word with more than one syllable. Example: "but-ter-fly" has 3 syllables.
Vowel -- The letters A, E, I, O, U (and sometimes Y).
Consonant -- All letters that are NOT vowels (b, c, d, f, g, h, j, k, l, m, n, p, q, r, s, t, v, w, x, y, z).
Decode -- To sound out and read a word by breaking it into parts.

How to Count Syllables

The easiest way to count syllables: put your hand under your chin and say the word out loud. Every time your chin drops, that is one syllable!

Strategies for Reading Multisyllable Words

StepStrategyExample

Lesson 3.4

Irregular Sight Words

Master the tricky words that do not follow normal spelling rules!

What Are We Learning Today?

Most English words can be sounded out using letter patterns. But some words are tricky -- they do not follow the normal rules! These are called sight words or irregular words, and the best way to learn them is to see them and recognise them instantly, without sounding them out.

Key Words to Know

Sight Word -- A common word that readers should recognise instantly by sight, without sounding it out.
Irregular Word -- A word that does not follow normal phonics patterns. Example: "said" (not "sed"), "the" (not "thee").
High-Frequency Word -- A word that appears very often in texts. Knowing these makes reading much smoother.
Fluency -- Reading smoothly and quickly with good understanding.

Why Are Some Words "Irregular"?

English words come from many different languages -- French, Latin, German, and more. This is why some words have surprising spellings!

These words look tricky because...

said -- sounds like "sed" but spelled s-a-i-d

were -- sounds like "wer" not "wee-ree"

would -- has a silent "l" and "ou" sounds like "oo"

laugh -- "gh" makes an "f" sound!

through -- "ough" sounds like "oo"

The best strategy:

See the word often + read it in many sentences = your brain learns to recognise it automatically!

Flash cards, word walls, and reading every day all help build sight word recognition.

The more you read and see these words, the faster you will recognise them!

Lesson 3.5

Reading Fluency and Expression

Read smoothly, accurately, and with feeling -- bring stories to life!

What Are We Learning Today?

You know the words. You understand the story. But are you reading it in a way that brings it to life? Today we learn about reading fluency -- reading smoothly and accurately -- and expression -- reading with feeling to match the text!

Key Words to Know

Fluency -- Reading smoothly, accurately, and at a good pace -- not too fast, not too slow.
Expression -- Reading with feeling and emotion that matches what is happening in the text.
Accuracy -- Reading words correctly without making errors.
Pace / Rate -- How fast or slow you read. Good readers adjust their pace for different texts.
Phrasing -- Reading words in natural groups, not one word at a time.
Punctuation -- Marks like periods, question marks, and exclamation points that tell us how to read.

The Three Parts of Fluency

Accuracy

Reading the words correctly. When you make an error, good readers go back and self-correct!

Rate (Pace)

Not too fast (rushing means missing meaning) and not too slow (sounds robotic). Read at a natural, conversational pace.

Expression

Your voice goes UP for questions, gets LOUD for excitement, gets SOFT for sad moments. Match your voice to the meaning!

Let Punctuation Guide Your Expression

Punctuation MarkWhat It Means for ReadingExample
. PeriodStop. Take a breath. Move on calmly."The dog sat down."
? Question MarkYour voice goes UP at the end."Are you coming with me?"
! Exclamation MarkRead with excitement, surprise, or urgency!"Watch out!"
, CommaA short pause -- keep going but breathe briefly."I like cats, dogs, and birds."
"" Quotation MarksChange your voice to sound like the character speaking."Help me!" she cried.

Practise With This Passage -- Read It Aloud!

"I can't believe it!" Maya whispered, her eyes wide. She had never seen anything like it before. The cave was enormous -- bigger than her whole school -- and every wall was covered in glittering crystals. "Are we really going inside?" asked her friend Theo, his voice shaking slightly. Maya took a deep breath. "Yes," she said quietly. "We have to."

Reading Guide: Read "I can't believe it!" with excitement. Read "her voice shaking slightly" -- Theo sounds nervous. Read Maya's final "Yes... We have to." slowly and with determination. Notice how the punctuation guides your expression!

Try It Yourself!

  1. Read the passage above out loud three times. Each time, try to read more smoothly than before.
  2. Choose a favourite book. Read one page out loud to a parent or family member.
  3. Record yourself reading a paragraph. Play it back -- do you sound smooth and expressive?
  4. Find a sentence with an exclamation mark. Read it in a flat voice first, then with expression. What is the difference?
