Lesson 14.1

What Is an Ecosystem?

A community of living things interacting with each other and their environment!

What Are We Learning Today?

Imagine a pond. It has water, rocks, soil, sunlight, fish, frogs, insects, plants, and bacteria -- all interacting with each other constantly. That whole system working together is an ecosystem! Today we learn what makes up an ecosystem, the different types that exist, and how living and nonliving parts work together.

Key Words to Know

Ecosystem -- All the living things and nonliving things in an area, interacting together.
Biotic -- The living parts of an ecosystem: plants, animals, fungi, bacteria.
Abiotic -- The nonliving parts of an ecosystem: sunlight, water, temperature, soil, air.
Population -- All members of the same species living in an area. Example: all the salmon in a river.
Community -- All the different populations living together in an area.
Biome -- A large geographic area with a similar climate and community of organisms.

The Parts of an Ecosystem

Biotic (Living) Parts

  • Plants and trees
  • Animals (all types)
  • Fungi (mushrooms)
  • Bacteria and microbes

Abiotic (Nonliving) Parts

  • Sunlight and temperature
  • Water and rain
  • Soil and rocks
  • Air and wind
Key Idea: In a healthy ecosystem, the biotic and abiotic parts are perfectly balanced and depend on each other. Changing one part -- even a nonliving part like rainfall -- can affect every living thing in the ecosystem.

Washington State Ecosystems

Washington state is extraordinary -- it has MULTIPLE biomes! The west side has temperate rainforests (Olympic Peninsula receives over 140 inches of rain per year!). The Cascade Mountains create a "rain shadow," making eastern Washington a semi-arid shrub-steppe ecosystem. The Columbia River creates a rich freshwater ecosystem. Washington also borders the Pacific Ocean. Few states have such diverse ecosystems!

An ecosystem = all living AND nonliving things in an area, interacting.

A biome is a large area with a similar climate and community.

Food Chains and Food Webs

Energy flows from one organism to the next -- follow the chain!

What Are We Learning Today?

Every organism needs energy to live. Plants get energy from sunlight. Animals get energy by eating other organisms. A food chain shows this flow of energy from one organism to the next. But in real ecosystems, eating relationships are much more complex -- they form a food web!

Key Words to Know

Food Chain -- A sequence showing how energy passes from one organism to the next through eating.
Food Web -- Multiple interconnected food chains showing the complex feeding relationships in an ecosystem.
Energy Flow -- The transfer of energy from one organism to another through eating.
Arrow in Food Chain -- Points FROM what is eaten TO the eater (shows direction of energy flow).
Trophic Level -- Each level in a food chain (producers are first, primary consumers second, etc.).

From Food Chain to Food Web

Real ecosystems are more complex -- most animals eat more than one type of food, creating an interconnected food web:

Example Forest Food Web Connections:

Mice eat seeds AND berries. Owls eat mice AND rabbits. Rabbits eat grass AND leaves. Foxes eat mice AND rabbits AND berries. A hawk eats mice AND rabbits AND snakes. All these interconnections create a web where removing ONE species affects many others!

Why food webs matter: If one animal in a food web disappears, it affects ALL the others connected to it. This is why the loss of even one species can ripple through an entire ecosystem!

What Happens When a Chain Is Broken?

If this disappears...Effect on the food chain
All the grass (producers)Grasshoppers starve → frogs starve → snakes starve → hawks starve. Entire chain collapses!
All the frogsGrasshoppers multiply out of control (too many). Snakes starve (not enough food).
All the hawks (top predator)Snake population explodes → too many snakes → frogs decline → grasshoppers explode.

Remember This!

Food chains start with producers (plants). Consumers follow.

A food web shows multiple interconnected food chains -- more realistic!

Producers, Consumers, and Decomposers

Every organism plays a role in the ecosystem's energy cycle!

What Are We Learning Today?

In every ecosystem, organisms play one of three key roles: they produce energy, consume energy, or decompose dead matter. Understanding these roles helps us see how energy and nutrients cycle continuously through an ecosystem -- nothing is ever wasted!

Key Words to Know

Producer -- An organism that makes its own food using sunlight (photosynthesis). All plants are producers.
Consumer -- An organism that gets energy by eating other organisms.
Decomposer -- An organism that breaks down dead plants and animals into nutrients that return to the soil.
Herbivore -- A consumer that eats ONLY plants. Example: rabbit, deer, caterpillar.
Carnivore -- A consumer that eats ONLY animals. Example: lion, eagle, shark.
Omnivore -- A consumer that eats BOTH plants and animals. Example: bear, raccoon, humans.
Predator -- An animal that hunts and eats other animals (prey).
Prey -- An animal that is hunted and eaten by predators.

The Three Roles

PRODUCERS

Make their own food

All plants, algae, and some bacteria. Use photosynthesis to convert sunlight into sugar. The FOUNDATION of every ecosystem.

Examples: grass, trees, kelp, phytoplankton

CONSUMERS

Eat other organisms

Must eat producers or other consumers for energy. Divided by what they eat and their level in the food chain.

Examples: deer (herbivore), wolf (carnivore), bear (omnivore)

DECOMPOSERS

Break down dead matter

Break down dead plants and animals, returning nutrients to the soil for new plants to use. ESSENTIAL recyclers!

Examples: mushrooms, bacteria, earthworms

Types of Consumers

Herbivores (plant-eaters)

Eat plants only

Rabbit, deer, cow, caterpillar, koala, horse, sheep, butterfly

Carnivores (meat-eaters)

Eat animals only

Lion, eagle, shark, wolf, orca, spider, dragonfly

Omnivores (both)

Eat plants AND animals

Bear, raccoon, crow, pig, human, chipmunk, box turtle

Why Decomposers Are Heroes!

Without decomposers, dead plants and animals would pile up everywhere -- and the nutrients locked inside them would never return to the soil. Decomposers are nature's recyclers:

Habitats and Niches

Where an organism lives and its role in the ecosystem!

What Are We Learning Today?

Every organism has a habitat -- a specific place where it lives that provides everything it needs. But even within the same habitat, each organism has a unique niche -- a specific role it plays. Understanding habitats and niches helps us see why biodiversity is so valuable!

Key Words to Know

Habitat -- The specific environment where an organism lives, which provides its food, water, shelter, and space.
Niche -- An organism's specific role in its ecosystem -- what it eats, where it lives, how it behaves.
Biodiversity -- The variety of different species in an ecosystem. More biodiversity = healthier ecosystem.
Competition -- When organisms fight for the same limited resource (food, water, space, mates).
Predator-Prey -- A relationship where one organism (predator) hunts and eats another (prey).
Symbiosis -- A close relationship between two different species living together.

Major Habitat Types

HabitatWhat It ProvidesAnimals That Live There
ForestTrees for shelter/food, shade, soilDeer, owls, bears, squirrels, woodpeckers
OceanSaltwater, enormous space, mineral nutrientsWhales, sharks, sea turtles, fish, octopus
Pond / RiverFreshwater, plants, sedimentFrogs, otters, salmon, dragonflies, herons
DesertDry soil, rocks, sparse vegetationCoyotes, rattlesnakes, roadrunners, scorpions
GrasslandOpen space, grasses, seasonal waterBison, prairie dogs, hawks, rabbits
Arctic/TundraCold, ice, short summersPolar bears, Arctic foxes, lemmings, snowy owls

Niche -- Each Species Has a Unique Role!

Even in the same forest, different animals have different niches so they do not compete for exactly the same resources:

Woodpecker

Niche: pecks into tree bark to find insects. Lives in tree cavities. Active in daytime.

Owl

Niche: hunts mice and voles at night using silent flight and night vision. Nests in tree holes.

Deer

Niche: grazes on shrubs and plants on the forest floor. Most active at dawn and dusk.

The Niche Rule: No two species can occupy exactly the same niche in the same ecosystem for long. If they do, they compete intensely until one outcompetes the other. Different niches = less competition = more biodiversity!

Types of Symbiosis

TypeWho BenefitsExample
MutualismBOTH species benefitBee pollinates flower (flower gets pollinated, bee gets nectar)
CommensalismONE benefits, other is not affectedBarnacles attach to whale (barnacle gets transport, whale is fine)
ParasitismONE benefits, other is HARMEDTick on a dog (tick feeds on blood, dog gets irritated/sick)

Niche = the specific ROLE an organism plays (what it eats, when active).

Mutualism = both benefit  |  Parasitism = one benefits, one harmed

Lesson 14.5

How Living Things Depend on Each Other

In nature, everything is connected -- discover the web of interdependence!

What Are We Learning Today?

No organism lives in isolation. Every living thing in an ecosystem affects others in complex ways. When we understand these connections, we see that interdependence -- the way living things depend on each other -- is what keeps ecosystems healthy and balanced. Today we explore these remarkable relationships!

Key Words to Know

Interdependence -- The way living things in an ecosystem depend on each other to survive.
Keystone Species -- A species that has a disproportionately large effect on its ecosystem -- removing it causes major changes.
Balance -- When populations in an ecosystem are stable because resources and needs are in harmony.
Overpopulation -- When a species grows too large and strains the ecosystem's resources.

Amazing Examples of Interdependence

Example 1: Wolves and Yellowstone National Park

Wolves were removed from Yellowstone in the 1920s. Without wolves, deer overpopulated and overgrazed, destroying vegetation near rivers. The banks eroded. Fish habitat disappeared. Then in 1995, wolves were reintroduced. Deer moved away from riverbanks. Vegetation grew back. Rivers stabilised. Fish returned. Songbirds returned. The wolves changed the very shape of the rivers -- this is called a trophic cascade!

Example 2: Sea Otters and Kelp Forests

Sea otters eat sea urchins. Sea urchins eat kelp. Without sea otters, sea urchins multiply and destroy kelp forests. Without kelp forests, hundreds of species lose their habitat. Sea otters are a keystone species -- they hold the whole kelp forest ecosystem together!

Example 3: Trees and Mycorrhizal Fungi

Forest trees share nutrients through underground fungal networks -- scientists call it the "Wood Wide Web"! Fungi help trees absorb water and minerals; trees share sugar with fungi through their roots. Trees even send nutrients through these networks to help dying neighbours. Trees in a forest are literally connected and helping each other!

Predator-Prey Balance

Predators and prey keep each other's populations in balance -- a delicate dance:

When prey increase:

More food for predators → predator population grows → more predators eat more prey → prey population falls → predators have less food → predator population falls → cycle repeats

When predators disappear:

Prey population explodes → they overeat vegetation → plants decline → prey population crashes from starvation → whole ecosystem destabilised

  4. Why is biodiversity important for keeping ecosystems stable?

Interdependence = living things depend on each other.

A keystone species holds the ecosystem together -- removing it causes huge changes.

Predator-prey populations balance each other in a natural cycle.

Lesson 14.6

Changes to Ecosystems

How natural events and human actions change the balance of ecosystems!

What Are We Learning Today?

Ecosystems are always changing -- sometimes slowly, sometimes suddenly. Changes can be natural (wildfires, floods, volcanic eruptions) or caused by humans (deforestation, pollution, invasive species). Today we learn about these changes and -- importantly -- what we can do to help protect ecosystems!

Key Words to Know

Natural Disaster -- A natural event like a wildfire, flood, drought, or volcanic eruption that dramatically changes an ecosystem.
Succession -- The gradual process of an ecosystem recovering and rebuilding after a disturbance.
Invasive Species -- A species introduced to a new ecosystem where it has no natural predators and crowds out native species.
Deforestation -- The large-scale cutting down of forests, destroying habitat for many species.
Pollution -- Harmful substances introduced to an environment that damage living things.
Conservation -- Protecting and preserving natural ecosystems and species.
Extinction -- When ALL members of a species have died and can never return.

Natural Changes to Ecosystems

Natural ChangeShort-Term EffectLong-Term Effect
WildfireDestroys trees and drives animals awayAsh fertilises soil; fire-adapted plants regrow; many species return stronger
FloodDrowns plants, displaces animalsDeposits rich sediment; wetland species thrive
DroughtPlants die; animals migrate or dieEcosystem slowly recovers when rain returns
VolcanoLava covers everything; total destructionNew fertile soil forms; life slowly returns (Mount St. Helens!)
Washington Connection: Mount St. Helens erupted in 1980, devastating 230 square miles of forest. But within years, life began returning -- first wildflowers and insects, then birds and larger mammals. Today it is a thriving ecosystem AND a National Volcanic Monument!

Human Changes to Ecosystems

Harmful Human Actions:

  • Deforestation -- destroys habitat
  • Pollution -- harms water, air, soil
  • Overfishing -- depletes fish populations
  • Invasive species -- outcompete native species
  • Climate change -- shifts temperature and rainfall patterns

Helpful Human Actions:

  • Conservation -- protecting wild areas
  • Reforestation -- planting trees
  • Wildlife corridors -- connecting habitats
  • Reducing pollution -- cleaner energy
  • Species reintroduction -- bringing back lost species

What Kids Can Do!

At School and Home:

  • Reduce, reuse, recycle
  • Plant native plants and flowers for pollinators
  • Turn off lights to save energy
  • Pick up litter, especially near water

In Your Community:

  • Join a local clean-up event
  • Support conservation organisations
  • Learn about local native species
  • Speak up for the environment!
