Lesson 2.1

Main Idea and Key Details

Find the most important idea in any nonfiction text!

What Are We Learning Today?

When you read a nonfiction text — like a science article, a history passage, or an informational book — the author has one most important idea they want you to understand. Everything else in the text supports that idea.

Today we learn how to find the main idea and the key details that support it!

Key Words to Know

Main Idea -- The most important point an author makes about a topic. What the whole text is mostly about.
Key Details -- Facts, examples, or explanations that support and prove the main idea.
Topic -- The subject the text is about. (One or two words: "frogs," "the moon")
Supporting Detail -- A piece of information that backs up the main idea with evidence.
Summarise -- To restate the main idea and key details in your own words, briefly.

Main Idea vs. Topic -- What is the Difference?

Topic

What the text is ABOUT -- just one or two words.

Example: "Bees"

Main Idea

The MOST IMPORTANT point the author makes about the topic -- a full sentence!

Example: "Bees are important pollinators that help plants grow and produce food."

Remember: The topic is just a word or two. The main idea is a complete sentence that tells us the most important thing about the topic!

Read This Informational Passage

Amazing Bees

Bees are one of the most important insects on Earth. They help flowers and plants reproduce by carrying pollen from one plant to another -- a process called pollination. Without bees, many of our favourite fruits and vegetables would not grow. Bees also make honey, which humans have used as food and medicine for thousands of years. A single bee colony can contain up to 80,000 bees, all working together to keep their hive healthy and productive.

Finding the Main Idea and Key Details

Strategy: Ask yourself -- "What is this MOSTLY about?" Your answer in a full sentence is the main idea. Then ask "What details does the author give to PROVE that?" Those are your key details!

Try It Yourself!

Read this short passage: "Dogs make wonderful pets for many reasons. They are loyal and love spending time with their owners. Dogs can also be trained to help people -- guiding the blind, detecting danger, and even helping people with disabilities. Studies show that pet owners who have dogs are often happier and healthier than people without pets."

  1. What is the TOPIC of this passage? (one or two words)
  2. What is the MAIN IDEA? (write a full sentence)
  3. List TWO key details that support the main idea.

Remember This!

TOPIC -- what the text is about (one or two words)

MAIN IDEA -- the most important point (a full sentence!)

KEY DETAILS -- facts and examples that support the main idea

Lesson 2.2

Asking Questions and Finding Answers in Nonfiction

Use questions to dig deeper into informational texts!

What Are We Learning Today?

In Unit 1 we learned to ask questions about stories. Nonfiction texts are different -- they give us real facts and information. Today we learn how to ask smart questions before, during, and after reading nonfiction to help us understand it better.

Key Words to Know

Nonfiction -- Writing about real people, places, events, and facts. It is true!
Fiction -- Made-up stories that are not real.
Prior Knowledge -- What you already know about a topic before you read.
KWL Chart -- A reading tool: Know, Want to Know, Learned. Helps organise your thinking.
Explicit Answer -- An answer stated directly in the text -- it is right there!
Implicit Answer -- An answer you have to figure out using clues from the text.

When to Ask Questions in Nonfiction

WhenWhat to AskWhy It Helps
Before Reading"What do I already know?" "What do I want to find out?"Gets your brain ready and curious!
During Reading"Does this answer my question?" "What does this mean?" "Why is this important?"Keeps you focused and thinking.
After Reading"What did I learn?" "What questions do I still have?"Helps you remember and think deeper.

The KWL Chart -- A Super Helpful Tool!

K -- What I KNOW

Before reading, write what you already know about the topic.

Example for "Volcanoes": I know volcanoes shoot out lava.

W -- What I WANT to Know

Before reading, write your questions -- what do you want to find out?

Example: How hot is lava? Why do volcanoes erupt?

L -- What I LEARNED

After reading, write the answers you found and new things you discovered.

Example: Lava can reach 2,200 degrees Fahrenheit!

Read and Find Answers

Volcanoes

A volcano is an opening in the Earth's surface through which hot melted rock, called magma, can escape. When magma reaches the surface, it is called lava. Lava can reach temperatures of over 2,200 degrees Fahrenheit -- hot enough to melt most metals! Volcanoes can be found on land and even under the ocean. There are about 1,500 potentially active volcanoes on Earth today. While volcanic eruptions can be destructive, they also create new land and enrich the soil, making it excellent for farming.

Questions and Answers from the Passage

Try It Yourself!

Choose any nonfiction topic you find interesting (animals, space, weather, history). Write a KWL chart:

  1. Write THREE things you already KNOW about the topic.
  2. Write THREE questions about what you WANT to know.
  3. Find a book or article about the topic. Write THREE things you LEARNED.

Remember This!

Ask questions before, during, and after reading nonfiction.

Use a KWL chart -- Know, Want to Know, Learned.

Explicit answers are right in the text. Implicit answers need thinking!

Lesson 2.3

Text Features: Headings, Captions, Bold Words, and Diagrams

Nonfiction texts have special features that help you find information fast!

What Are We Learning Today?

Open any nonfiction book and you will notice it looks different from a story! There are headings, bold words, photos with captions, and diagrams. These are called text features -- and they are like a map that helps you find information quickly and understand it better.

Key Words to Know

Text Feature -- A special part of a nonfiction text that helps readers find and understand information.
Heading -- A title at the top of a section that tells you what that section is about.
Caption -- Words below a photo or picture that explain what it shows.
Bold Words -- Words in dark, heavy print that show they are important vocabulary.
Diagram -- A labelled drawing that shows how something works or is organised.
Table of Contents -- A list at the front of a book showing chapters and page numbers.
Index -- An alphabetical list at the back of a book helping you find specific topics.
Glossary -- A mini-dictionary at the back of a book explaining key vocabulary words.

Common Text Features and What They Do

Text FeatureWhat It Looks LikeHow It Helps
HeadingBold title above a section of textTells you what that section is about before you read
SubheadingSmaller title within a sectionBreaks a long section into smaller parts
Bold WordsWords in heavy dark printSignals important vocabulary to learn
CaptionSmall text under a photoExplains what the photo shows
DiagramLabelled drawing or chartShows how something looks or works
Table of ContentsList at the front of the bookShows what chapters are included and where
GlossaryMini-dictionary at the backExplains the meaning of key words
IndexAlphabetical list at the backHelps you find specific topics quickly

Why Do Nonfiction Books Use Text Features?

Find Information Fast

Instead of reading every word, you can use headings and an index to jump straight to what you need.

Understand Better

Diagrams and captions explain things that are hard to describe in words alone -- like how a volcano works.

Learn New Words

Bold words and glossaries help you identify and learn the important vocabulary in the text.

Reading Tip: Before reading a nonfiction text, do a "text feature walk" -- look at the headings, photos, captions, and diagrams FIRST. This gives you a preview of what you are about to learn and helps you read with purpose!

Try It Yourself!

Find any nonfiction book or magazine. Look through it and find as many text features as you can. In your notebook, write:

  1. List FIVE text features you found. What type is each one?
  2. Choose ONE text feature. What information does it give you?
  3. Would the text be harder to understand without that feature? Why?

Remember This!

TEXT FEATURES are special parts of nonfiction texts.

Headings, captions, bold words, diagrams, glossaries, and indexes all help readers find and understand information.

Always do a text feature walk before reading!

Lesson 2.4

Connections Between Events, Concepts, and Ideas

See how ideas in nonfiction texts are linked together!

What Are We Learning Today?

In nonfiction texts, ideas are not just listed randomly -- they are connected to each other. Events happen because of other events. Ideas lead to other ideas. Today we learn how to spot these connections using special signal words that show us how ideas are related!

Key Words to Know

Cause -- The reason why something happens.
Effect -- What happens as a result of a cause.
Sequence -- The order in which events happen (first, next, then, finally).
Compare -- To show how two things are similar.
Contrast -- To show how two things are different.
Signal Words -- Words that show HOW ideas are connected (because, therefore, first, however).

Types of Connections and Their Signal Words

Cause and Effect -- Why something happens

because as a result therefore so caused by led to

Sequence -- The order things happen

first next then after finally before

Compare and Contrast -- Similarities and differences

however but similarly on the other hand both unlike

Read and Find the Connections

The Water Cycle

The water cycle is the continuous movement of water on Earth. First, heat from the sun causes water in oceans, lakes, and rivers to evaporate -- turning from liquid into water vapour. Next, the water vapour rises into the atmosphere and cools down. As it cools, it condenses into tiny droplets, forming clouds. Then, when the clouds become heavy enough, the water falls back to Earth as precipitation -- rain, snow, or hail. Finally, the water flows into rivers and oceans, and the cycle begins again.

Connections in "The Water Cycle"

Connection TypeExample from the Text
Cause and EffectHeat from the sun CAUSES water to evaporate. Clouds becoming heavy CAUSES precipitation.
SequenceFirst (evaporation) → Next (rising and cooling) → Then (condensation) → Finally (precipitation)
Signal Words Used"First," "Next," "Then," "Finally," "causes," "As it cools"
Strategy: When you see a signal word like "because," "first," or "however," STOP and ask yourself: "What kind of connection is the author showing me here?"

Try It Yourself!

Read any nonfiction paragraph from a book or article. Write:

  1. Find ONE cause-and-effect connection. What CAUSED something? What was the EFFECT?
  2. Find ONE sequence. Write the events in order using: First... Next... Then... Finally...
  3. What signal words helped you spot these connections?

Remember This!

CAUSE and EFFECT -- signal words: because, therefore, as a result

SEQUENCE -- signal words: first, next, then, finally

COMPARE and CONTRAST -- signal words: however, similarly, unlike

Signal words are your CLUES for finding connections!

Lesson 2.5

Comparing Two Texts on the Same Topic

What happens when two authors write about the same subject?

What Are We Learning Today?

Imagine two different books about sharks. One author might focus on how dangerous sharks are. Another might focus on how important sharks are to the ocean. Same topic -- very different information!

Today we learn how to compare two nonfiction texts on the same topic and notice what is the same and what is different.

Key Words to Know

Author's Purpose -- The reason an author wrote the text (to inform, to persuade, to entertain).
Point of View (Nonfiction) -- The author's opinion or perspective on the topic.
Fact -- Something that can be proven to be true.
Opinion -- What someone thinks or believes -- cannot be proven true or false.
Source -- Where information comes from. Different sources can give different information!

Read These Two Passages About Wolves

Passage A: Wolves in the Wild

Wolves are magnificent animals that play a vital role in maintaining healthy ecosystems. As predators, they keep deer and elk populations balanced, which prevents overgrazing of plants and forests. When wolves were reintroduced to Yellowstone National Park in 1995, the entire ecosystem improved -- rivers, plants, and other animals all benefited. Wolves live in family groups called packs and show remarkable intelligence and cooperation.

Passage B: Living with Wolves

For farmers and ranchers in rural areas, wolves present a serious challenge. Wolf populations have grown in recent years, leading to increased attacks on livestock such as sheep and cattle. Farmers report losing hundreds of animals each year to wolf predation. While wolves are protected by law, many rural communities feel that current protections make it impossible to keep their animals safe. Finding a balance between wolf conservation and farming needs remains a complex issue.

Comparing the Two Passages

ElementPassage APassage B
TopicWolvesWolves
Main FocusBenefits of wolves to ecosystemsProblems wolves cause for farmers
Author's PurposeTo show wolves are important and helpfulTo show wolves cause challenges for farmers
TonePositive about wolvesConcerned about wolf impact
What's the Same?Both discuss wolves and their relationship with humans. Both include factual information.
Important Lesson: The same topic can be presented very differently depending on the author's purpose and point of view. Smart readers think about WHO wrote the text and WHY!

Try It Yourself!

Find two books or articles about the same topic (animals, weather, a historical event). Compare them:

  1. What FACTS appear in BOTH texts?
  2. What information is in one text but NOT the other?
  3. Does each author seem to have a different point of view? How can you tell?
  4. Which text do you think is more helpful? Why?

Remember This!

Two texts on the same topic can have very different information depending on the author's purpose and point of view.

Look for what is SAME and what is DIFFERENT between two texts.

Always think: WHO wrote this and WHY?

Lesson 2.6

Understanding Vocabulary in Informational Text

Learn powerful strategies for figuring out new words in nonfiction!

What Are We Learning Today?

Nonfiction texts are full of domain-specific vocabulary -- special words used in science, history, maths, and other subjects. When you come across an unfamiliar word, you do not have to give up! Today we learn powerful strategies for figuring out what new words mean.

Key Words to Know

Domain-Specific Vocabulary -- Special words used in a particular subject area (e.g., "photosynthesis" in science).
Context Clues -- Words or sentences around an unknown word that hint at its meaning.
Definition -- A clear explanation of what a word means.
Synonym -- A word that means the same or nearly the same as another word.
Prefix -- A word part added to the beginning of a word to change its meaning (un-, re-, pre-).
Suffix -- A word part added to the end of a word to change its meaning (-ful, -less, -ing).

Strategies for Figuring Out New Words

Strategy 1: Context Clues

Read the sentences AROUND the unknown word. The author often gives hints!

"The volcano erupted, sending hot magma -- melted rock from deep inside the Earth -- shooting into the air."

The dash gives us the definition of "magma" right in the sentence!

Strategy 2: Prefixes and Suffixes

Break the word into parts you know!

uncomfortable = un (not) + comfortable = not comfortable

recycle = re (again) + cycle = to cycle again

careless = care + less (without) = without care

Strategy 3: Use the Glossary

Most nonfiction books have a glossary at the back with definitions of bold or important words. Check it first!

Practice: Figure Out the Bold Words

"Photosynthesis is the process by which plants use sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide to produce their own food. During this process, plants release oxygen as a by-product. Without photosynthesis, most life on Earth would not be possible, as it forms the foundation of nearly every food chain."

Try it: What does "photosynthesis" mean? Use context clues -- the sentence after it explains the process! What does "by-product" mean? "By" means alongside, "product" means something made -- so a by-product is something made alongside the main thing!

Try It Yourself!

Find a nonfiction article or book. Find THREE words you do not know. For each word:

  1. Write the sentence the word appears in.
  2. Use context clues or prefixes/suffixes to guess the meaning.
  3. Check a dictionary or glossary -- were you right?
  4. Write your own sentence using the new word.
