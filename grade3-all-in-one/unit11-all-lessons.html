Lesson 11.1

Telling Time to the Minute

Read any clock -- analog or digital -- with total confidence!

What Are We Learning Today?

Being able to tell time is one of the most practical math skills you will ever learn! Today we read both analog clocks (with hands) and digital clocks (with numbers) to the minute, and understand a.m. and p.m.

Key Words to Know

Analog Clock -- A clock with an hour hand and a minute hand that move around a circular face.
Digital Clock -- A clock that shows time with numbers (e.g., 3:47).
Hour Hand -- The SHORT hand on an analog clock. Points to the hour.
Minute Hand -- The LONG hand on an analog clock. Points to the minutes.
a.m. -- The hours from midnight (12:00 a.m.) to just before noon (11:59 a.m.).
p.m. -- The hours from noon (12:00 p.m.) to just before midnight (11:59 p.m.).

Reading an Analog Clock

The Hour Hand (SHORT)

  • Points directly at a number = that hour exactly
  • Points between two numbers = the lower number is the hour
  • Moves slowly -- takes 12 hours to go all the way around

The Minute Hand (LONG)

  • Points at 12 = 0 minutes (:00)
  • Points at 3 = 15 minutes (:15)
  • Points at 6 = 30 minutes (:30)
  • Points at 9 = 45 minutes (:45)
  • Each small tick = 1 minute
  • Each number = 5 more minutes

The Minute Hand -- Count by 5s!

Minute Hand Points At...Minutes Past the HourExample
120 minutes (:00)4:00 -- "four o'clock"
15 minutes (:05)4:05 -- "four oh five"
210 minutes (:10)4:10 -- "four ten"
315 minutes (:15)4:15 -- "quarter past four"
630 minutes (:30)4:30 -- "half past four"
945 minutes (:45)4:45 -- "quarter to five"
1155 minutes (:55)4:55 -- "five minutes to five"

a.m. and p.m. -- Morning vs Afternoon/Night

a.m. (midnight to noon)

12:00 a.m. -- midnight

7:30 a.m. -- wake up for school!

9:00 a.m. -- school morning

11:59 a.m. -- just before noon

p.m. (noon to midnight)

12:00 p.m. -- noon (lunch!)

3:15 p.m. -- after school

6:00 p.m. -- dinner time

9:00 p.m. -- bedtime!

Memory trick: a.m. = "After Midnight" (morning). p.m. = "Past Midday" (afternoon and evening).

Try It Yourself!

  1. Write the time shown: Hour hand between 7 and 8, minute hand pointing at 4. What time is it?
  2. Write these times in digital format: "quarter past nine," "half past two," "twenty minutes to six."
  3. Is 7:30 a.m. or p.m. the time you usually eat breakfast? What about dinner?
  4. How many minutes are in one hour? How many hours are in one day?

Remember This!

Short hand = hour  |  Long hand = minutes

Minute hand at 12 = :00  |  at 3 = :15  |  at 6 = :30  |  at 9 = :45

a.m. = midnight to noon  |  p.m. = noon to midnight

Lesson 11.2

Elapsed Time

Calculate how much time has passed between a start and end time!

What Are We Learning Today?

Elapsed time is the amount of time that passes between a start time and an end time. "How long did the movie last?" "How many hours until lunch?" These are elapsed time questions! Today we learn two powerful strategies: the number line jump strategy and the counting-up method.

Key Words to Know

Elapsed Time -- The amount of time that passes from a start time to an end time.
Start Time -- When an event begins.
End Time -- When an event finishes.
Duration -- How long something lasts -- another word for elapsed time.

Strategy 1: Count Up on a Number Line

Problem: School starts at 8:15 a.m. and ends at 3:00 p.m. How long is the school day?

Jump from 8:15 a.m. to 3:00 p.m.:
8:15 a.m.
+45 min
9:00 a.m.
+6 hrs
3:00 p.m.

Total: 45 minutes + 6 hours = 6 hours 45 minutes

Strategy 2: Count by Hours Then Minutes

Problem: A movie starts at 2:20 p.m. and ends at 4:45 p.m. How long is the movie?

1
Count the hours: 2:20 p.m. → 4:20 p.m. = 2 hours
2
Count remaining minutes: 4:20 p.m. → 4:45 p.m. = 25 minutes
3
Add together: 2 hours + 25 minutes = 2 hours 25 minutes

Finding the End Time

Problem: Practice starts at 3:30 p.m. and lasts 1 hour 45 minutes. What time does it end?

1
Start: 3:30 p.m. Add 1 hour → 4:30 p.m.
2
Add 45 minutes: 4:30 + 45 min → 5:15 p.m.
3
Practice ends at 5:15 p.m.

Quick Conversion Facts

Time FactValue
Minutes in 1 hour60 minutes
Hours in 1 day24 hours
Days in 1 week7 days
Months in 1 year12 months
Days in 1 year365 days (366 in a leap year)

Try It Yourself!

  1. A movie starts at 1:15 p.m. and ends at 3:40 p.m. How long is the movie?
  2. Sam starts homework at 4:30 p.m. and finishes at 5:45 p.m. How long did he work?
  3. A party starts at 2:00 p.m. and lasts 2 hours 30 minutes. What time does it end?
  4. How many minutes are in 2 hours? In 2 hours 15 minutes?

Remember This!

Elapsed time = end time minus start time.

Jump strategy: count up to the next hour, then count hours, then remaining minutes.

60 minutes = 1 hour  |  24 hours = 1 day

Lesson 11.3

Measuring Length: Inches, Feet, Centimeters, and Meters

Measure everything around you using the right unit!

What Are We Learning Today?

Length is one of the most common things we measure. Today we learn to use both customary units (inches and feet -- used in the USA) and metric units (centimeters and meters -- used in science and most of the world), and choose the right unit for each object.

Key Words to Know

Inch (in.) -- A small customary unit. About the width of your thumb!
Foot (ft.) -- 12 inches. About the length of a ruler.
Yard (yd.) -- 3 feet or 36 inches. About the height of a doorknob.
Centimeter (cm) -- A small metric unit. About the width of your pinky fingernail!
Meter (m) -- 100 centimeters. A little more than 3 feet. About the height of a door handle.
Estimate -- An approximate measurement before you measure exactly.

Customary Units (Used in the USA)

UnitAbbreviationEqualsReal-World Reference
Inchin.--Width of a thumb / a large paperclip
Footft.12 inchesLength of a standard ruler
Yardyd.3 feet = 36 inchesLength from nose to fingertip of outstretched arm

Metric Units (Used in Science and Most of the World)

UnitAbbreviationEqualsReal-World Reference
Centimetercm--Width of a pinky fingernail
Meterm100 centimetersAbout the height of a door handle from the floor

Choosing the Right Unit

Use INCHES or CM for:

  • A pencil
  • A book
  • Your hand
  • A leaf

Use FEET or METERS for:

  • A classroom
  • A person's height
  • A swimming pool
  • A hallway
Rule of thumb: Use smaller units for smaller objects and larger units for larger objects. It would be silly to measure your pencil in miles or your city block in inches!

How to Measure with a Ruler

1
Line up the ZERO mark of the ruler with one end of the object.
2
Read the number at the other end of the object.
3
Include the unit in your answer (e.g., 7 cm, not just 7).
Important: Always start measuring from the zero mark, not the edge of the ruler! Many rulers have a small gap before the zero mark.

Try It Yourself!

  1. Which unit would you use to measure: a crayon? the school hallway? a swimming pool? your fingernail?
  2. Convert: 2 feet = ___ inches  |  36 inches = ___ yards  |  3 meters = ___ centimeters
  3. Find 5 objects around your home. Estimate their length, then measure and record the actual length.
  4. Which is longer -- 2 feet or 20 inches? Show your work.

Remember This!

12 inches = 1 foot  |  3 feet = 1 yard

100 centimeters = 1 meter

Always include the unit with your measurement!

Lesson 11.4

Liquid Volume and Mass

Measure how much liquid fits in a container and how heavy objects are!

What Are We Learning Today?

Not everything can be measured with a ruler! Liquid volume measures how much liquid fits in a container. Mass measures how heavy an object is. Today we learn the units for both, and understand the difference between mass and weight.

Key Words to Know

Liquid Volume -- The amount of liquid a container holds. Measured in liters (L) or milliliters (mL).
Liter (L) -- The standard metric unit of liquid volume. A large water bottle holds about 1 liter.
Milliliter (mL) -- A very small metric unit. 1,000 milliliters = 1 liter. A teaspoon holds about 5 mL.
Mass -- The amount of matter in an object. Measured in grams (g) or kilograms (kg).
Gram (g) -- A small metric unit of mass. A paper clip has a mass of about 1 gram.
Kilogram (kg) -- 1,000 grams. A large textbook has a mass of about 1 kilogram.

Liquid Volume -- Liters and Milliliters

UnitAbbreviationEqualsReal-World Reference

Medicine doses (5 mL)

Eye drops (1-2 mL)

A spoonful of syrup

Use L for large amounts:

Water in a fish tank (20 L)

A bottle of juice (1 L)

A bathtub (150 L)

Mass -- Grams and Kilograms

A backpack full of books (~5 kg)

A bag of flour (~1 kg)

Try It Yourself!

  1. Which unit would you use: a swimming pool (L or mL)? A dose of cough syrup (L or mL)?
  2. Which unit would you use: a feather (g or kg)? An elephant (g or kg)?
  3. Convert: 4 liters = ___ mL  |  5,000 g = ___ kg  |  2.5 kg = ___ g
  4. A recipe needs 500 mL of milk. How much of a 1-liter carton will you use?

Remember This!

1 liter = 1,000 milliliters  |  1 kilogram = 1,000 grams

Use mL and g for small amounts. Use L and kg for large amounts.

Lesson 11.5

Perimeter and Area

Measure around a shape AND the space it covers!

What Are We Learning Today?

Perimeter and area are two different ways to measure a shape. Perimeter measures the distance AROUND the outside. Area measures the space INSIDE. Both are essential for real-world tasks like fencing a yard or laying carpet on a floor!

Key Words to Know

Perimeter -- The total distance around the outside edge of a shape. Add all the side lengths!
Area -- The amount of space INSIDE a shape. Measured in square units.
Square Unit -- A square that is 1 unit x 1 unit -- used to measure area.
Square Centimeter (cm2) -- Area of a 1 cm x 1 cm square.
Square Meter (m2) -- Area of a 1 m x 1 m square.

Perimeter -- Add All the Sides!

Rectangle Perimeter

Add all 4 sides:

Length = 8 cm, Width = 5 cm

P = 8 + 5 + 8 + 5 = 26 cm

Or: P = 2 x (length + width)

P = 2 x (8 + 5) = 2 x 13 = 26 cm

Irregular Shape Perimeter

Just add ALL the sides!

Sides: 4, 3, 5, 3, 2 cm

P = 4 + 3 + 5 + 3 + 2

P = 17 cm

Area -- Count or Multiply the Square Units!

Count Square Units

Count each unit square inside the shape.

4 x 3 grid = 12 square units

Multiply (Faster!)

For rectangles:

Area = length x width

Length = 6 cm, Width = 4 cm

Area = 6 x 4 = 24 cm²

Perimeter vs Area -- Side by Side

FeaturePerimeterArea
What it measuresDistance AROUND the outsideSpace INSIDE the shape
How to find itAdd all side lengthsCount squares OR length x width
Units usedcm, m, in., ft. (regular units)cm², m², in.², ft.² (SQUARE units)
Real-world useFencing a yard, framing a pictureCarpet on a floor, tiles on a wall
Perimeter vs Area confusion buster: Perimeter = going for a run around the EDGE of a park. Area = laying out a picnic blanket to cover the INSIDE of that park.

Try It Yourself!

  1. Find the perimeter and area of a rectangle with length 9 cm and width 4 cm.
  2. A square has a side length of 6 m. What is its perimeter? What is its area?
  3. A room is 8 meters long and 5 meters wide. How many square meters of carpet are needed to cover the floor?
  4. Two rectangles have the same area of 24 cm². Can they have different perimeters? Give an example.

Remember This!

Perimeter = add all sides (unit: cm, m, ft.)

Area = length x width (unit: cm², m², ft.²)

Perimeter is the edge. Area is the inside space.

Reading Graphs and Data

Collect, organise, and make sense of data using different types of graphs!

What Are We Learning Today?

Data is information collected through observation or surveys. Graphs help us organise and visualise data so it is easier to understand and compare. Today we learn to read and interpret three types of graphs: pictographs, bar graphs, and line plots.

Key Words to Know

Data -- Information collected through observation, counting, or measurement.
Tally Chart -- A way to record data using tally marks (||||).
Pictograph -- A graph that uses pictures or symbols to represent data. A key tells you how many each symbol represents.
Bar Graph -- A graph that uses bars of different lengths to show and compare data.
Line Plot -- A graph that shows data points on a number line with X marks above each value.
Scale -- The numbers along the side or bottom of a graph showing what values the bars or lines represent.
