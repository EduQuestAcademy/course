Lesson 18.1

Indigenous Peoples of Washington: Since Time Immemorial

The first peoples of Washington have lived here since time immemorial -- their stories, knowledge, and culture are living and vital today!

What Does "Since Time Immemorial" Mean?

"Since time immemorial" means since before human memory -- since the very beginning. Indigenous peoples of Washington did not arrive at some point in history. They have always been here. Their relationship with this land goes back thousands and thousands of years, long before any written records were kept. This phrase honours their deep, unbroken connection to the land.

What Are We Learning Today?

Washington state requires all students to learn about the history, culture, and government of the Indigenous peoples who have lived here since time immemorial. This is called the Since Time Immemorial (STI) curriculum. Today we begin by learning who these peoples are, how they lived, and why their cultures and sovereignty are important today.

Key Words to Know

Indigenous -- Originating from a particular place; the original inhabitants of a land.
Since Time Immemorial -- Since before memory; from the very beginning of time in this place.
Tribe / Nation -- A community of Indigenous people with shared ancestry, culture, language, and government.
Sovereignty -- The right of a nation to govern itself. Tribes are sovereign nations with their own governments.
Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK) -- Deep knowledge of the natural world developed over thousands of years by Indigenous peoples.
Oral History -- History passed down through spoken stories, songs, and ceremonies rather than written records.

Indigenous Peoples Have Always Been Here

Archaeological evidence shows people have lived in the Pacific Northwest for at least 14,000 years -- and Indigenous oral histories describe an even longer presence. These peoples developed rich, complex cultures perfectly adapted to the diverse environments of Washington state:

Coastal Peoples

Developed sophisticated ocean-going canoes, fished salmon, harvested cedar forests, built large communal longhouses, and created extraordinary art including totem poles.

Plateau Peoples

Lived along rivers like the Columbia and Snake, relied heavily on salmon runs, gathered camas root and other plants, and traded with peoples across a vast network.

Living Cultures -- Not Just History!

Indigenous peoples and their cultures are not things of the past. They are living, thriving communities today. Washington state has 29 federally recognised tribes, each with their own:

Government

Tribal councils, courts, laws, and police. Tribes are sovereign nations that govern their own members and lands.

Culture and Language

Active language revitalisation programmes, traditional ceremonies, arts, music, and cultural practices being passed to new generations.

Economies

Many tribes operate businesses, fisheries, forests, and gaming enterprises that support their communities and create jobs.

Important: When we learn about Indigenous peoples, we must remember we are learning about real people with vibrant cultures today -- not ancient history. Many tribal members are your neighbours, classmates, and community members right here in Washington state!

Traditional Ecological Knowledge

Over thousands of years, Indigenous peoples developed extraordinary knowledge of the natural world -- what scientists now call Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK). This includes:

  • Which plants are edible, medicinal, or useful for building and tools
  • Sustainable fishing and hunting practices that maintained healthy populations
  • Using controlled burns to manage forests and prairies for better habitat
  • Understanding seasonal patterns of fish, animals, and plants
  • Complex trading networks across hundreds of miles
Modern scientists are learning from TEK! Many Indigenous land management practices -- like controlled burns to prevent larger wildfires -- are now being adopted by modern land managers as highly effective and sustainable.

Lesson 18.2

Major Tribes of Washington State

Meet the peoples whose homelands make up what we call Washington state!

What Are We Learning Today?

Washington state has 29 federally recognised tribes, each with a unique culture, language, and history tied to specific parts of the landscape. Today we learn about several of the major tribes, especially those connected to the regions around where you live. Every part of Washington is the ancestral homeland of Indigenous peoples.

Key Words to Know

Reservation -- Land set aside by the federal government for a tribe to live on. Most tribes lost much of their traditional territory and were moved to smaller reservations.
Federally Recognised Tribe -- A tribe that the US government officially recognises as having a special government-to-government relationship.
Longhouse -- A large communal dwelling shared by several families, common among coastal tribes. Also used as a ceremonial gathering place.
Potlatch -- A ceremonial feast and gift-giving tradition of coastal Northwest peoples. Demonstrates wealth through generosity, not possession.
Camas -- A flowering plant whose bulb was a major food source for many Plateau tribes.
Salmon -- The most important food resource for most Washington tribes, both ecologically and culturally.

Tribes Near Your Home: The Plateau Region

Yakama Nation

Traditional Homeland: Yakima River Valley and surrounding areas -- very close to Kennewick!

Traditional Life: Fished salmon in the Yakima and Columbia Rivers, gathered camas and other plants, travelled seasonally to follow food resources.

Today: The Confederated Tribes and Bands of the Yakama Nation is one of Washington's largest tribes, with a reservation near Toppenish, WA. They operate schools, healthcare, forestry, and fisheries programmes.

Confederated Tribes of the Umatilla Indian Reservation (CTUIR)

Traditional Homeland: Columbia Plateau including the area around the confluence of the Columbia, Snake, and Walla Walla Rivers -- near today's Tri-Cities!

Three Member Tribes: Cayuse, Umatilla, and Walla Walla peoples

Today: Located in Oregon but with deep connections to the Kennewick/Tri-Cities area. The famous Kennewick Man -- Ancient One -- is a 9,000-year-old ancestor claimed by CTUIR and other tribes.

Nez Perce Tribe (Nimiipuu)

Traditional Homeland: Snake River region of eastern Washington, Idaho, and Oregon

Traditional Life: Expert salmon fishers and horse people; developed the Appaloosa horse breed

Known for: Chief Joseph's remarkable resistance and the 1877 Nez Perce War

The Importance of Salmon to Northwest Tribes

Salmon is at the heart of Northwest Indigenous cultures -- it is not just food but a spiritual being that gives itself to the people. Tribes developed sustainable fishing practices over thousands of years:

  • Fish wheels and weirs (underwater fences) to catch salmon efficiently
  • Carefully timed fishing to allow enough salmon to pass and spawn
  • Ceremonies to honour and thank the salmon for their gift
  • Preserving salmon through drying and smoking for winter food stores
Today: Washington tribes continue to fight for treaty-protected fishing rights. The 1974 Boldt Decision (US vs. Washington) confirmed that tribes have the right to harvest 50% of all harvestable salmon in their traditional fishing areas -- a landmark ruling for Indigenous rights.

Lesson 18.3

Treaty Rights and the Government-to-Government Relationship

Promises made in treaties are still law today -- understanding them matters!

What Are We Learning Today?

In the 1850s, the US government signed treaties with Washington tribes. In these treaties, tribes gave up most of their land -- but they kept the right to fish, hunt, and gather in their traditional territories. These treaties are still the supreme law of the land today, and understanding them is essential to understanding Washington history and current events.

Key Words to Know

Treaty -- A formal, legally binding agreement between two governments. In the US, treaties with tribes are the supreme law of the land.
Treaty Rights -- Rights that tribes retained (kept) when they signed treaties -- particularly fishing, hunting, and gathering rights.
Government-to-Government Relationship -- The formal relationship between the US federal government and tribal governments as separate, sovereign nations.
Isaac Stevens -- The first Governor of Washington Territory who negotiated most of the Washington treaties in 1855.
Boldt Decision -- The 1974 federal court ruling (US v. Washington) confirming tribes' treaty right to 50% of harvestable salmon.
Trust Responsibility -- The legal obligation of the US federal government to protect tribal lands, resources, and rights.

The Stevens Treaties (1854-1856)

Washington Territory Governor Isaac Stevens negotiated a series of treaties with many Northwest tribes between 1854 and 1856. In these treaties:

What Tribes GAVE UP:

  • Most of their traditional lands (millions of acres)
  • Freedom of movement without restriction
  • Some tribes were forced to share reservations with traditional rivals

What Tribes KEPT (Treaty Rights):

  • Right to fish at "usual and accustomed places"
  • Right to hunt and gather on open unclaimed lands
  • Their own government and self-governance
  • Specific reservation lands
Important context: Tribal leaders who signed these treaties were negotiating under enormous pressure and often did not have access to accurate translations. Many tribes felt they were making temporary agreements, not permanently giving up their lands. The US did not always honour its treaty obligations.

The Boldt Decision -- A Landmark for Treaty Rights

For decades after the treaties, state officials blocked tribes from exercising their fishing rights. Tribal members were arrested for fishing in their traditional locations. Tribes fought back in court.

In 1974, Judge George Boldt ruled in United States v. Washington that tribes were entitled to 50% of the harvestable salmon in their treaty fishing areas. This ruling also recognised tribes as co-managers of the fisheries resource.

Why This Matters: The Boldt Decision was one of the most significant federal Indian law rulings in US history. It reaffirmed that treaties made over 100 years earlier are still legally binding and must be honoured -- even when inconvenient. It established that tribal sovereignty is real and enforceable.

Government-to-Government Relationship Today

Washington state has a unique legal obligation to work directly with tribal governments. This means:

  • State agencies must consult with tribes before making decisions that affect tribal lands or resources
  • Tribes have co-management authority over salmon, shellfish, and other natural resources
  • Tribal governments are respected as equal partners in decisions affecting their communities
  • Washington state law (the STI curriculum) requires schools to teach about tribal history and culture

Lesson 18.4

European Exploration and the Fur Trade

European arrival transformed the Pacific Northwest -- explore this complex history!

What Are We Learning Today?

Beginning in the late 1700s, European and American explorers arrived in the Pacific Northwest. Their arrival led to dramatic changes -- new trade goods, devastating diseases, and eventually conflict over land. Today we learn about this period of contact and change, including the famous Lewis and Clark Expedition.

Key Words to Know

Exploration -- Travelling to unknown or little-known areas to learn about and claim them for a country.
Fur Trade -- The trading of animal furs (especially beaver) between Indigenous peoples and European/American traders.
Lewis and Clark Expedition -- The 1804-1806 expedition led by Meriwether Lewis and William Clark to explore the newly purchased Louisiana Territory and the Pacific Northwest.
Sacagawea -- A Shoshone woman who served as guide and interpreter for Lewis and Clark. Her knowledge and presence were essential to the expedition's success.
Disease -- European diseases like smallpox and measles devastated Indigenous populations who had no immunity to them.
Hudson's Bay Company -- A British trading company that established fur trading posts in the Pacific Northwest, including Fort Vancouver.

Early European Contact

1774
Spanish explorer Juan Peréz becomes one of the first Europeans to sail along the Washington coast, encountering Haida and Makah peoples.
1788-1792
British and American traders arrive seeking sea otter furs, which were enormously valuable in China. The sea otter trade brings new goods -- and new diseases -- to coastal tribes.
1792
American captain Robert Gray becomes the first non-Indigenous person to enter the Columbia River, naming it after his ship. British captain George Vancouver simultaneously explores Puget Sound.
1805
Lewis and Clark arrive at the Pacific coast via the Columbia River -- the first Americans to cross the continent. They could not have done it without Indigenous guides and assistance.

Sacagawea -- The True Guide of Lewis and Clark

Sacagawea was a Shoshone woman who joined the Lewis and Clark Expedition as a guide and interpreter. Her contributions were extraordinary: she helped negotiate with her own people for horses essential to cross the Rocky Mountains, identified edible plants that prevented the expedition from starving, and her presence with her baby signalled to tribes the expedition encountered that these travellers came in peace. Without Sacagawea, the expedition almost certainly would have failed.

The Fur Trade and Its Impact

The Fur Trade Economy

Beaver fur hats were fashionable in Europe. Tribes traded furs for metal tools, blankets, guns, and other manufactured goods. The Hudson's Bay Company built Fort Vancouver (near Portland) as its Pacific Northwest headquarters in 1824.

The Devastating Impact of Disease

European contact brought diseases like smallpox, measles, and influenza. Indigenous peoples had no immunity. Scholars estimate that 50-90% of the Pacific Northwest Indigenous population died from disease in the 18th and 19th centuries -- one of the greatest human catastrophes in history.

Lesson 18.5

Washington Territory and Statehood

From contested territory to the 42nd state -- Washington's path to statehood!

What Are We Learning Today?

Through the mid-1800s, the Pacific Northwest went from contested ground to US territory to a growing settled region. Washington Territory was created in 1853, and Washington became the 42nd state in 1889. This period brought dramatic change -- both for settlers and for the Indigenous peoples who had lived here since time immemorial.

Key Words to Know

Oregon Country -- The region including present-day Washington, Oregon, Idaho, and parts of Canada -- jointly occupied by Britain and the US from 1818.
Oregon Trail -- A 2,170-mile wagon route from Missouri to Oregon used by settlers migrating west in the 1840s-1860s.
Manifest Destiny -- The 19th-century belief that the US was destined to expand across the entire North American continent.
Washington Territory -- Created in 1853 when the northern part of Oregon Territory was split off. The territorial capital was Olympia.
Statehood -- The process of becoming a full US state. Washington became the 42nd state on November 11, 1889.

Key Events on the Road to Statehood

1818
The US and Britain agree to jointly occupy the Oregon Country -- the Pacific Northwest region.
1840s
Thousands of settlers travel the Oregon Trail to the Pacific Northwest. This influx of settlers dramatically changes the region and increases US claims to the territory.
1846
US and Britain divide the Oregon Country along the 49th parallel. Everything south of the line (including present-day Washington) becomes US territory.
1853
Washington Territory is officially created, separated from Oregon Territory. Isaac Stevens becomes the first territorial governor.
1855
Governor Stevens negotiates treaties with many Washington tribes. Tribes give up most of their land but retain fishing, hunting, and gathering rights.
1855-1858
Indian Wars occur in eastern Washington as tribes resist being displaced from their lands.
1883
The Northern Pacific Railroad reaches Tacoma, connecting Washington to eastern US markets. Population booms.
1889
Washington becomes the 42nd state on November 11, 1889. Olympia is the state capital.

Impact on Indigenous Peoples

The period of settlement and statehood was devastating for Indigenous peoples:

  • Millions of acres of tribal land were taken by the government and given to settlers
  • Tribes were forced onto small reservations, often far from their traditional territories
  • Indian boarding schools were established, forcibly removing Indigenous children from their families and forbidding their languages and cultural practices
  • Traditional ways of life were severely disrupted
Boarding Schools: The US government operated Indian boarding schools with the goal of "assimilating" Indigenous children into American culture. Children were taken from families, forbidden to speak their languages or practice their culture, and punished for doing so. This caused generational trauma that Indigenous communities are still healing from today.

Lesson 18.6

Washington State in the 20th Century

Wars, dams, technology, and healing -- Washington's remarkable modern history!

What Are We Learning Today?

The 20th century transformed Washington state into one of America's most important economic and technological regions. From World War II manufacturing to the rise of Boeing, Microsoft, and Amazon, Washington has been at the forefront of American history. It has also been a place where Indigenous peoples fought to reclaim their rights and culture.

Key Words to Know

Grand Coulee Dam -- A massive hydroelectric dam built on the Columbia River (1933-1942). One of the largest dams in the world -- but it flooded tribal lands and blocked salmon migration.
Hanford Site -- A nuclear production complex near Kennewick created during World War II. It produced the plutonium for the first nuclear weapons.
Boeing -- An aircraft manufacturing company headquartered in Seattle that became one of the world's largest employers in the 20th century.
Self-Determination -- The movement and legal right of tribes to govern themselves and make their own decisions, beginning with federal policy changes in the 1970s.

Key Events of 20th Century Washington

1933-42
Grand Coulee Dam built on the Columbia River during the Great Depression. It provided electricity and irrigation -- but permanently flooded tribal fishing sites and blocked salmon from reaching their spawning grounds upstream. The Colville and other tribes lost sacred sites and fishing areas.
1942-45
Hanford Nuclear Site (near Kennewick) secretly built as part of the Manhattan Project. It produced the plutonium used in the first nuclear bomb tested in New Mexico and in the bomb dropped on Nagasaki, Japan, helping end World War II. Today it is the most contaminated nuclear site in the US and is being cleaned up.
1940s-60s
Boeing becomes a dominant employer in the Seattle area, building warplanes, then commercial jets including the 707 and 747, making Seattle a global aviation hub.
1974
Boldt Decision -- a federal court rules tribes have the right to 50% of harvestable salmon, launching a new era of tribal rights recognition. (Reviewed in Lesson 18.3)
1975
Federal Indian Self-Determination Act passes -- tribes gain the right to run their own programmes (education, healthcare, housing) rather than having the federal government manage them.
1980
Mount St. Helens erupts on May 18 -- the most significant volcanic eruption in US history since 1914. 57 people die; 230 square miles are devastated. It becomes a living laboratory for ecological recovery.
1975-present
Microsoft (1975) and Amazon (1994) founded in Washington, making the Seattle area a global technology hub and creating enormous economic growth.

Kennewick and the Hanford Site

Your Community's History: The Hanford Site, located just north of Kennewick near Richland, played a pivotal role in World War II and the Cold War. The plutonium produced there helped end WWII but also contributed to a dangerous nuclear arms race. Today, Hanford is the largest nuclear cleanup project in US history -- the federal government employs thousands of people in Kennewick/Richland to safely contain and clean up radioactive waste. The Hanford Site also sits on Wanapum tribal ancestral territory.

Tribal Recovery and Cultural Renaissance

Despite centuries of loss, Washington's tribes have shown remarkable resilience:

Language Revitalisation

Many tribes are working to revive languages that were nearly lost during the boarding school era. Language immersion programmes, dictionaries, and digital archives help pass on languages to new generations.

Economic Development

Tribal enterprises -- from gaming to fisheries to technology -- have created economic independence. Tribes reinvest profits in schools, healthcare, and cultural programmes.

Cultural Arts

Northwest Coast art -- from totem poles to weavings to canoe carving -- has experienced a magnificent renaissance. Master carvers and artists are passing their skills to new generations.
