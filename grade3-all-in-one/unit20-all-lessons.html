Lesson 20.1

Needs, Wants, and Scarcity

Why can't we have everything we want? Economics begins with this question!

What Are We Learning Today?

Economics is the study of how people make choices about using limited resources. The foundation of economics is a simple but powerful idea: people have unlimited wants but limited resources. Because of this, we must always make choices about what to spend our time and money on. This is called scarcity!

Key Words to Know

Economics -- The study of how people make choices about using limited resources to meet their wants and needs.
Needs -- Things people MUST have to survive: food, water, clothing, shelter, healthcare.
Wants -- Things people would LIKE to have but do not need to survive: toys, video games, vacations, candy.
Scarcity -- When there is not enough of something to satisfy everyone who wants it. Resources are limited but wants are unlimited.
Choice -- Because of scarcity, people must decide what to get or do and what to give up.
Opportunity Cost -- What you give up when you make a choice. If you spend $10 on a book, you give up whatever else you could have bought with that $10.
Trade-off -- Giving up one thing to get another.

Needs vs. Wants -- Know the Difference!

NEEDS (must have to survive)

  • Food and water
  • Shelter (a place to live)
  • Clothing appropriate for the weather
  • Basic healthcare
  • Safety

Without these, life is at risk.

WANTS (nice to have)

  • Toys, games, and electronics
  • Candy and snacks
  • New clothes (beyond what you need)
  • Vacations
  • Streaming services

Life continues without these.

Tricky ones! A phone is mostly a want -- but for someone who needs it to call 911 in an emergency, it might be a need. A fancy restaurant meal is a want -- but basic food is a need. Context matters! The key question: "Would I be in danger without this?"

Scarcity -- The Foundation of Economics

Scarcity means there is never enough of everything to give everyone everything they want. This is true at every level:

Personal Scarcity

You get $10 for your birthday. You want a $10 book AND a $10 game. You can't have both. That is personal scarcity -- not enough money for everything you want.

Community Scarcity

A city has a limited budget. It can build a new park OR a new library -- not both. The city must choose. That is scarcity at the community level.

Global Scarcity

Fresh water, farmland, oil, and even time are limited resources that the whole world must share and manage carefully.

Opportunity Cost -- What You Give Up

Example: Maya has $15.

She can buy: Option A -- a book for $15, OR Option B -- a board game for $15.

She chooses the book (Option A).

Opportunity cost = Option B (the board game) -- that is what she gave up when she chose the book. Every choice has an opportunity cost -- the next-best alternative you didn't choose.

Try It Yourself!

  1. Sort these into NEEDS or WANTS: food, new sneakers, water, a vacation, a house, a video game, medicine, internet.
  2. You have one hour of free time. You want to read AND play outside, but you can only do one. What is your opportunity cost if you choose to read?
  3. A school has budget for one new thing: a new computer lab OR new playground equipment. Why must they choose? What economic concept is this?
  4. Can the same item be a need for one person and a want for another? Explain.

Remember This!

Needs = food, water, shelter, clothing, safety.

Wants = everything else we'd like but don't need to survive.

Scarcity = limited resources + unlimited wants = we must choose!

Opportunity cost = what you give up when you make a choice.

Lesson 20.2

Goods, Services, and Trade

The world economy runs on the exchange of goods and services!

What Are We Learning Today?

Every day you use hundreds of goods (physical things) and benefit from services (work people do). People and communities cannot produce everything they need on their own -- so they trade! Trade allows people to specialise in what they are best at and exchange with others for what they need. Today we explore goods, services, and trade!

Key Words to Know

Goods -- Physical products that people make, buy, and sell. You can touch them! Example: food, clothing, cars, books.
Services -- Work that people do for others. You cannot touch a service! Example: teaching, nursing, repairing, cooking.
Trade -- The exchange of goods or services between people, communities, or countries.
Barter -- Trading goods or services directly without using money.
Specialisation -- When a person, community, or country focuses on producing what it does best, then trades for everything else.
Import -- Goods or services brought into a country from another country.
Export -- Goods or services sent out of a country to another country.
Interdependence -- When communities or countries depend on each other for goods and services.

Why Trade Happens -- Specialisation!

Nobody can produce everything they need alone. So people specialise in what they do best and trade for everything else:

Example: A Farming Community and a City

Farmers in eastern Washington are great at growing apples and wheat -- they specialise in agriculture. People in Seattle are great at building software and aeroplanes -- they specialise in technology and manufacturing. The farmers trade food to the city; the city provides technology and manufactured goods to the farmers. Both are better off by specialising and trading!

Global Trade: The same principle works between countries! The US exports wheat, aircraft, and software. It imports electronics, clothing, and coffee from countries that specialise in those things. International trade makes everyone wealthier by letting each country focus on its strengths.

Lesson 20.3

Producers, Consumers, and Markets

How supply, demand, and price work together in the marketplace!

What Are We Learning Today?

Every economic transaction involves someone who produces (makes or provides) something and someone who consumes (buys or uses) it. The place where buyers and sellers come together is called a market. Today we explore how markets work and the fascinating relationship between supply, demand, and price!

Key Words to Know

Producer -- A person or business that makes or provides goods and services for sale.
Consumer -- A person who buys and uses goods and services.
Market -- Any place (physical or online) where buyers and sellers come together to exchange goods and services.
Supply -- How much of a good or service producers are willing and able to offer for sale.
Demand -- How much of a good or service consumers want and are able to buy.
Price -- The amount of money a buyer pays to a seller for a good or service.
Competition -- When multiple businesses sell similar products and try to attract the same customers, often by offering lower prices or better quality.

Supply and Demand -- How Prices Are Set

When demand goes UP and supply stays the same...

Price goes UP. Everyone wants it but there is only so much available.

Example: New toy released before the holidays -- everyone wants it, few available → price rises.

When supply goes UP and demand stays the same...

Price goes DOWN. There is plenty for everyone.

Example: A great apple harvest in Washington -- tons of apples, prices drop.

SituationSupplyDemandPrice
Big harvest of apples in WAHIGHNormalFalls (more apples than buyers)
Drought destroys wheat cropLOWNormalRises (less wheat, same demand)
New popular toy releasedNormalHIGHRises (same toys, more buyers)
Holiday season passesNormalLOWFalls (nobody wants old toys now)

Competition Benefits Consumers!

When many businesses sell the same product, they must compete for customers. This benefits consumers:

Competition leads to:

  • Lower prices (to attract buyers)
  • Better quality (to beat rivals)
  • More innovation (new features)
  • Better customer service

Without competition (monopoly):

  • Higher prices (no reason to lower them)
  • Less choice for consumers
  • Less pressure to improve quality

Example: When only ONE company supplies internet in an area, prices are high and service may be poor.

Types of Markets

Try It Yourself!

  1. Name a producer and a consumer in each of these transactions: buying groceries, getting a haircut, downloading a song.
  2. If a drought reduces the apple harvest in Washington, what happens to apple prices? Why?
  3. Why is competition usually good for consumers? Give a real-world example.
  4. List THREE different markets you or your family use. What type of market is each one?

Remember This!

Producer = makes/sells. Consumer = buys/uses.

High demand + low supply = price goes UP.

Low demand + high supply = price goes DOWN.

Competition keeps prices low and quality high!

Lesson 20.4

Money, Saving, and Budgeting

Smart money skills you can use RIGHT NOW -- and for the rest of your life!

What Are We Learning Today?

Understanding money is one of the most important life skills you will ever learn. Today we learn how money works, why saving matters, and how to make a budget -- a plan for spending and saving. These skills will help you your whole life!

Key Words to Know

Money -- A medium of exchange that people use to buy goods and services. It replaces barter.
Income -- Money earned from working or other sources.
Expense -- Money spent on goods and services.
Saving -- Setting aside money now to use in the future.
Budget -- A plan that shows income and expenses to help manage money wisely.
Bank -- A business where people deposit (store) money safely and can earn interest on savings.
Interest -- Money a bank pays you for keeping your savings there (or money you pay for borrowing).
Debt -- Money owed to someone else. Borrowing more than you can repay creates debt problems.

Why Money is Important

Before money existed, people used barter (direct trade). But barter had big problems:

  • You had to find someone who had exactly what you wanted AND wanted exactly what you had
  • Hard to divide goods (how do you trade half a cow for some grain?)
  • Difficult to save value for later

Money solves all these problems! It is a universally accepted medium of exchange that everyone in a society agrees to use. Over history, money has taken many forms -- shells, gold, silver coins, paper bills, and now digital currency!

Making a Budget -- The Smart Money Plan

A budget is a plan for your money. The basic rule: income minus expenses = savings. You should always try to spend LESS than you earn!

Example: Sam's Monthly Budget ($30 allowance)

The 3 S's of Smart Spending: Save first (pay yourself first!), then Spend wisely, then Share (donate or give). Making a plan for every dollar means money goes where you intend it to go!

Why Saving Matters

Short-Term Saving

Saving for something you want soon -- like a new toy or book. You set aside a little each week until you have enough.

Example: Save $5/week for 6 weeks = $30 for a new game!

Long-Term Saving

Saving for something big in the future -- college, a car, emergencies. Banks pay you INTEREST on savings, helping your money grow!

The earlier you start saving, the more it grows!

Emergency Fund

Saving money for unexpected events -- a broken phone, unexpected medical bills, job loss. Having savings reduces stress and protects your family.

Try It Yourself!

  2. Why is having a budget helpful? What problems does it prevent?
  3. If you save $3 every week, how much will you have after 10 weeks? After 20 weeks?
  4. What is the difference between a short-term and a long-term financial goal? Give one example of each.

Remember This!

A budget = income - expenses = savings. Always spend less than you earn!

Save first, then spend wisely, then share -- the 3 S's!

Money in a bank earns interest -- your savings grow over time!

Lesson 20.5

Culture: Traditions, Customs, and the Arts

Culture is the way of life of a group of people -- it makes each community unique and beautiful!

What Are We Learning Today?

Culture is the shared beliefs, values, customs, traditions, language, food, music, art, and ways of life of a group of people. Every human community has a culture -- and cultures make our world endlessly rich and fascinating. Today we explore what culture is, why it matters, and how to respect and appreciate cultural diversity!

Key Words to Know

Culture -- The beliefs, values, customs, traditions, language, arts, and way of life shared by a group of people.
Tradition -- A custom or practice passed down from generation to generation.
Custom -- A common practice or behaviour in a culture.
Heritage -- The traditions, achievements, and values passed down from ancestors to future generations.
Cultural Diversity -- The variety of cultures, backgrounds, and traditions in a community. Diversity enriches communities!
Cultural Respect -- Treating other people's cultures with appreciation and understanding, even when they are different from your own.

Elements of Culture

Language

The primary way cultures communicate and express ideas. Language carries cultural knowledge, stories, and values.

Food

What people eat and how they prepare it reflects their history, geography, and values. Food is often central to celebrations and family life.

Music and Dance

Every culture has unique musical traditions -- from Indigenous drum songs to jazz to classical to pop -- expressing joy, sorrow, spirituality, and community.

Art and Craft

Visual art, sculpture, weaving, carving, pottery -- each culture expresses its worldview through unique artistic traditions.

Religion and Beliefs

Spiritual beliefs shape holidays, rituals, moral values, and community life. Many cultures are deeply shaped by religious traditions.

Family and Community

How families are structured, how communities care for each other, and how decisions are made all reflect cultural values.

Cultural Respect -- Celebrating Differences!

Washington state and Kennewick are home to people from many cultural backgrounds. Cultural respect means:

  • Curiosity: Being genuinely interested in learning about other cultures
  • Respect: Treating different customs with dignity, even when unfamiliar
  • Humility: Recognising that your culture is one of many equally valid ways of life
  • Appreciation: Enjoying and celebrating cultural differences as enriching
  • Understanding: Learning the history and meaning behind cultural practices
Cultural Exchange Makes Everyone Richer! The food you eat, the music you listen to, and the words you use are all influenced by dozens of cultures. Pizza (Italian), tacos (Mexican), jeans (originally French), basketball (invented by Canadian James Naismith) -- American culture is a beautiful blend of the world's cultures!

  1. Name THREE elements of YOUR family's culture or heritage. What makes them special?
  2. What is the difference between a tradition and a custom?
  3. Why is cultural diversity valuable for a community?
  4. Find ONE cultural tradition from a culture different from yours. What does it celebrate or teach?

Remember This!

Culture = language, food, music, art, beliefs, and traditions of a group.

Every culture has equal value and deserves respect and curiosity.

Cultural diversity enriches communities -- our differences make us stronger together!

Washington State's Economy and Cultural Diversity

The final lesson -- exploring the incredible economy and culture of your home state!

What Are We Learning Today?

Washington state has one of the most diverse and dynamic economies in the United States. From world-leading technology companies to abundant agriculture, from aerospace to maritime trade, Washington produces and trades goods and services that reach every corner of the world. And its people represent cultures from around the globe, making it one of the most culturally rich states in the nation!

Key Words to Know

Economy -- The system of producing, distributing, and consuming goods and services in a region or country.
Industry -- A branch of economic activity: agriculture, technology, manufacturing, fishing, tourism.
Innovation -- The creation of new ideas, products, or ways of doing things. Seattle is a global hub of innovation!
Immigrant -- A person who moves to a new country to live. Immigrants have shaped Washington's culture and economy throughout its history.

Kennewick and the Tri-Cities Economy

Agriculture

The Columbia Basin is one of America's most productive farming regions. Apples, grapes, potatoes, asparagus, hops, and wheat grow abundantly thanks to irrigation from the Columbia River. The Yakima Valley wine industry has grown to world-class status!

Hanford / Energy

The Hanford Site cleanup employs thousands of scientists, engineers, and workers in the Tri-Cities. It is also home to the Pacific Northwest National Laboratory -- a world-class energy research facility.

Trade and Commerce

The Columbia, Snake, and Yakima Rivers made Kennewick a natural trade hub. Today barge traffic still moves agricultural goods on the Columbia River to Portland and to the Pacific Ocean for export.

Washington's Cultural Diversity

Washington state is home to people from every part of the world. Major cultural communities include:

Indigenous Peoples

29 federally recognised tribes with vibrant living cultures -- Yakama, Tulalip, Makah, Colville, Spokane, and more.

Latino/Hispanic Community

A large and growing community, especially in eastern Washington agricultural areas. Essential to farming and food processing industries.

Asian American Community

Large communities from China, Japan, Korea, Vietnam, Philippines, and India -- especially in Seattle. Major contributions to tech industry.

African American Community

Long history in Washington, especially Seattle area. Important contributions to music (Seattle jazz and R&B), civil rights, and community leadership.

The Tri-Cities / Kennewick: Kennewick's population is about 30% Hispanic/Latino -- one of the highest percentages in Washington state. This reflects the important role of the Latino community in eastern Washington's agricultural economy and cultural life. Dia de los Muertos, Cinco de Mayo, and Mexican cultural traditions are celebrated throughout the Tri-Cities community!
