Lesson 6.1

Context Clues: Figuring Out Unknown Words

Use the words around a mystery word to crack its meaning!

What Are We Learning Today?

You are reading a book and suddenly -- a word you have never seen before! What do you do? Stop and look it up every single time? That would take forever! Instead, smart readers use context clues -- the words and sentences AROUND the unknown word -- to figure out what it means. Today we learn five types of context clues!

Key Words to Know

Context Clues -- Words and sentences surrounding an unknown word that hint at its meaning.
Definition Clue -- The author directly defines the word right in the sentence.
Synonym Clue -- A word with a similar meaning appears nearby to help explain the unknown word.
Antonym Clue -- A word with the opposite meaning appears nearby as a contrast.
Example Clue -- Examples are given that help illustrate what the word means.
Inference Clue -- No direct hint is given -- you have to use overall meaning to make a smart guess.

Five Types of Context Clues

TypeSignal WordsHow It Works
Definitionis, means, is called, refers to, or (followed by definition), dashes (--)The author tells you exactly what the word means right in the sentence.
Synonymalso called, or, in other words, similarly, likewiseA word with a similar meaning appears near the unknown word.
Antonymbut, however, unlike, on the other hand, instead of, althoughA word with the OPPOSITE meaning helps you figure out the unknown word.
Examplefor example, such as, including, likeExamples of the word are given so you can infer its category or meaning.
Inference(no signal words)You use the overall meaning of the passage to make a smart guess.

See Each Type in Action

Definition Clue

"The habitat -- the natural environment where an animal lives and finds food -- of the polar bear is the Arctic."

The dashes signal that a definition is coming right after the word!

Synonym Clue

"The old man was frail, or weak and delicate, after his long illness."

"Or" signals that a synonym or explanation is coming.

Antonym Clue

"Unlike her talkative sister, Mia was reticent and rarely spoke in class."

"Unlike" signals contrast -- if her sister is talkative, Mia must be quiet/shy.

Example Clue

"There were many nocturnal animals at the zoo, such as owls, bats, and raccoons."

All examples are animals active at night -- nocturnal must mean active at night!

Inference Clue

"After the hurricane, the town was in devastation. Houses were destroyed, trees were uprooted, and the streets were flooded."

No signal word -- but the description of destruction tells us devastation means terrible damage.

Strategy: When you hit an unknown word, ask yourself: (1) Is there a definition nearby? (2) Is there a synonym or opposite? (3) Are there examples? (4) What does the rest of the sentence or paragraph tell me? Then make your best guess and keep reading!

Try It Yourself!

  1. "The scientist was meticulous -- she checked every detail three times and never made a careless error." What does meticulous mean? What type of clue helped you?
  2. "He felt jubilant when he won, but his opponent was disappointed." What does jubilant mean? What type of clue is this?
  3. Find an unfamiliar word in a book you are reading. Write the sentence it appears in and explain what context clues you used.

Remember This!

Five types of context clues: Definition, Synonym, Antonym, Example, Inference

Look for signal words: or, but, such as, unlike, for example

Always read the sentences AROUND the unknown word -- the answer is often right there!

Lesson 6.2

Synonyms and Antonyms

Words that mean the same, words that mean the opposite -- build a richer vocabulary!

What Are We Learning Today?

Knowing synonyms helps you avoid repeating the same word over and over. Knowing antonyms helps you understand contrast and opposites. Together, they make your vocabulary much richer and your writing far more interesting and precise!

Key Words to Know

Synonym -- A word that means the SAME or nearly the same as another word. Example: happy / joyful.
Antonym -- A word that means the OPPOSITE of another word. Example: happy / sad.
Precise Language -- Choosing the EXACT right word for the meaning you want.

Common Synonyms and Antonyms

WordSynonyms (same meaning)Antonyms (opposite meaning)
happyjoyful, cheerful, delighted, pleased, contentsad, miserable, unhappy, gloomy, sorrowful
biglarge, enormous, huge, giant, massivesmall, tiny, little, miniature, petite
fastquick, rapid, swift, speedy, hastyslow, sluggish, gradual, unhurried, leisurely
bravecourageous, bold, fearless, daring, heroiccowardly, timid, fearful, hesitant, afraid
smartintelligent, clever, brilliant, wise, sharpfoolish, ignorant, unwise, slow, careless
beautifulstunning, gorgeous, lovely, attractive, elegantugly, unattractive, plain, hideous, dull

Why Synonyms Are Not EXACTLY the Same

Synonyms are similar -- but they are not always interchangeable! Each word has its own shade of meaning.

happy / ecstatic / content

happy = feeling good

ecstatic = EXTREMELY happy, overjoyed

content = quietly satisfied, not necessarily excited

angry / furious / annoyed

annoyed = mildly bothered

angry = clearly upset

furious = extremely, intensely angry

Writing Tip: Using precise synonyms makes your writing more vivid. Instead of "The dog was big," try "The dog was enormous." Instead of "She was happy," try "She was ecstatic." The right synonym adds power to your writing!

Try It Yourself!

  1. Write TWO synonyms and ONE antonym for each word: cold, tired, walk, old
  2. Replace the underlined words with a more precise synonym: "The big storm caused a lot of bad damage to the old building."
  3. Write a sentence using an antonym pair to show contrast: "She was _____, but her brother was _____."

Remember This!

SYNONYM = same or similar meaning (happy / joyful)

ANTONYM = opposite meaning (happy / sad)

Choose the most precise synonym for the exact meaning you want!

Lesson 6.3

Shades of Meaning: Word Relationships

Words on a spectrum -- from mild to strong, learn to choose with precision!

What Are We Learning Today?

Words are not just "same" or "opposite." Many words exist on a spectrum -- a scale from mild to strong, small to large, or quiet to loud. Understanding these shades of meaning helps you choose the EXACT right word every time you write!

Key Words to Know

Shades of Meaning -- The slight differences in meaning between words that are similar but not identical.
Intensity -- The strength or degree of a word's meaning (weak to strong).
Connotation -- The feeling or emotion a word carries beyond its basic definition.
Word Spectrum -- A scale that arranges related words from weakest to strongest meaning.

Positive vs. Negative Connotations

Some words mean nearly the same thing but carry different feelings -- one positive, one negative.

Think about it: Would you rather be called "thrifty" or "cheap"? Both relate to money, but one sounds like a compliment and one sounds like an insult! Word choice matters a lot.

Try It Yourself!

  1. Arrange these words from weakest to strongest: furious, annoyed, irritated, angry, enraged
  2. Arrange these words from smallest to largest: enormous, tiny, big, huge, small, gigantic
  3. Is "curious" positive or negative? Is "nosy" positive or negative? Write a sentence using each one to show the difference.
  4. Choose the BEST word: "She was (upset / devastated) when her dog ran away." Which word is stronger? Which fits better?

Remember This!

Words exist on a spectrum from mild to strong.

Similar words can have different connotations -- positive or negative feelings.

Choosing the right word on the spectrum makes your writing powerful and precise!

Lesson 6.4

Figurative Language: Idioms, Similes, and Metaphors

When words mean MORE than what they say -- the magic of figurative language!

What Are We Learning Today?

We explored literal vs. nonliteral language in Unit 1. Now we go deeper into three powerful types of figurative language -- idioms, similes, and metaphors. These are the tools that make writing come alive, paint pictures in the reader's mind, and make language memorable and beautiful.

Key Words to Know

Figurative Language -- Language that uses words in creative, non-literal ways to create vivid images and comparisons.
Idiom -- A phrase whose meaning is completely different from the literal meaning of its words.
Simile -- A comparison between two different things using "like" or "as."
Metaphor -- A comparison that says one thing IS another thing, without using "like" or "as."
Personification -- Giving human qualities or actions to non-human things.

Idioms -- Hidden Meanings!

Idioms are phrases that do NOT mean what the words literally say. You have to know the hidden meaning!

"It's raining cats and dogs."
Literal: Animals are falling from the sky!  |  Real meaning: It is raining very heavily.
"Break a leg!"
Literal: Injure your leg!  |  Real meaning: Good luck!
"I have butterflies in my stomach."
Literal: Insects inside you!  |  Real meaning: I feel nervous.
"Hit the books."
Literal: Strike books with your hand!  |  Real meaning: Study hard.
"Under the weather."
Literal: Below the clouds!  |  Real meaning: Feeling sick or unwell.

Similes -- Comparisons Using "Like" or "As"

SimileWhat It Means
"She was as brave as a lion."She was extremely brave, like a fearless lion.
"He ran like the wind."He ran incredibly fast.
"The classroom was as quiet as a library."The classroom was completely silent.
"Her smile was like sunshine on a cloudy day."Her smile was warm and uplifting.
"He was as stubborn as a mule."He refused to change his mind at all.

Metaphors -- One Thing IS Another Thing

MetaphorWhat It Means
"Life is a rollercoaster."Life has exciting highs and scary lows, just like a rollercoaster.
"The classroom was a zoo."The classroom was wild and chaotic, like a zoo.
"Time is money."Time is as valuable and limited as money -- don't waste it!
"Her voice was music to my ears."Her voice was extremely pleasant and beautiful to hear.
"He is a shining star."He stands out and is excellent at what he does.
Simile vs. Metaphor: Simile uses LIKE or AS. Metaphor does NOT -- it says one thing IS another. "She was like a lion" = simile. "She was a lion" = metaphor. Both are comparisons, but metaphors are more direct and powerful!

Try It Yourself!

  1. Write the REAL meaning of these idioms: "Bite the bullet." / "Spill the beans." / "The ball is in your court."
  2. Write THREE original similes. Use: "as ___ as ___" or "___ like ___."
  3. Write TWO original metaphors. Remember: no "like" or "as" -- say one thing IS another.
  4. Find ONE example of figurative language in a book you are reading. What type is it? What does it mean?

Remember This!

IDIOM -- phrase with a hidden meaning ("hit the books" = study)

SIMILE -- comparison using LIKE or AS ("brave as a lion")

METAPHOR -- says one thing IS another ("life is a rollercoaster")

Lesson 6.5

Using a Dictionary and Thesaurus

Master the two most powerful vocabulary tools every writer needs!

What Are We Learning Today?

When context clues are not enough, two amazing tools can help: the dictionary (which tells you what a word means) and the thesaurus (which gives you synonyms and antonyms). Today we learn how to use both of these tools effectively to grow your vocabulary and improve your writing!

Key Words to Know

Dictionary -- A reference book (or website) that lists words alphabetically and gives their definitions, part of speech, pronunciation, and example sentences.
Thesaurus -- A reference book (or website) that lists synonyms and antonyms for words -- used to find more precise or interesting alternatives.
Entry Word -- The word being defined in the dictionary (shown in bold at the start of each entry).
Part of Speech -- The grammatical role of a word: noun (n.), verb (v.), adjective (adj.), adverb (adv.).
Guide Words -- The two words at the top of each dictionary page showing the first and last entry on that page.

How to Read a Dictionary Entry

cou·ra·geous
adjective (adj.)
Not afraid of danger or difficulty; brave and determined when facing challenges.
"The courageous firefighter ran into the burning building to save the family."
ven·ture
verb (v.) | noun (n.)
v. To go somewhere or do something that involves risk or uncertainty. | n. A risky or daring project or journey.
"She decided to venture into the dark forest despite her fear."

Parts of a Dictionary Entry

PartWhat It Tells You
Entry wordThe word being defined (syllable dots show where to divide it)
Part of speechWhether it is a noun, verb, adjective, adverb, etc.
DefinitionWhat the word means (some words have multiple definitions!)
Example sentenceShows how to use the word correctly in context

How to Use a Thesaurus

A thesaurus is used when you want to avoid repeating a word or find a more precise/interesting alternative. Look up the word and choose the synonym that best fits your meaning.

Thesaurus Entry for "said"

said (verb) -- Synonyms: whispered shouted exclaimed replied muttered announced declared asked

Instead of: "Help me," he said.   Try: "Help me," he cried. or "Help me," he whispered urgently.

Warning! Do not just pick ANY synonym from the thesaurus. Make sure it fits the meaning and tone of your sentence. "Enormous" and "colossal" are both synonyms for "big" -- but "colossal mistake" sounds much more serious than "enormous mistake"!

Dictionary vs. Thesaurus -- When to Use Each

Use a DICTIONARY when:

  • You find an unknown word and context clues are not enough
  • You need the exact, precise definition
  • You want to check spelling or pronunciation
  • You want to know what part of speech a word is

Use a THESAURUS when:

  • You are repeating the same word too many times
  • You want a more exciting or precise word
  • You want to vary your vocabulary in writing
  • You know the meaning but want a stronger word

Try It Yourself!

  1. Look up THREE words in a dictionary. Write the part of speech and definition for each.
  2. Use a thesaurus to find THREE better alternatives for the word "good" in: "The movie was good." Rewrite the sentence three different ways.
  3. Look up the word "run" in a dictionary. How many different definitions does it have?
  4. Write a paragraph about any topic. Then use a thesaurus to replace at least FOUR words with more precise or interesting synonyms.
