Lesson 4.1

Opinion Writing: Stating and Supporting a View

Learn to share your opinion clearly and back it up with strong reasons!

What Are We Learning Today?

An opinion is what you think or believe about something. In opinion writing, you share your view AND give reasons to support it. Good opinion writing is not just saying "I like it because it's good" -- you need specific, convincing reasons that help the reader understand why you think the way you do!

Key Words to Know

Opinion -- What you think or believe. Cannot be proven true or false.
Reason -- An explanation that supports your opinion and helps convince the reader.
Introduction -- The opening of your piece that states your opinion clearly.
Body -- The middle section where you give your reasons with details.
Concluding Statement -- The ending that wraps up your opinion and reminds the reader of your view.
Persuade -- To convince someone to agree with your opinion.

The Structure of Opinion Writing

1
Introduction -- State your opinion clearly
Tell the reader exactly what you think. Use phrases like: "I believe...", "In my opinion...", "I think..."
2
Reason 1 -- Your first reason + a detail
Give your strongest reason. Add a specific fact or example to back it up.
3
Reason 2 -- Your second reason + a detail
Give another reason. The more specific, the more convincing!
4
Concluding Statement -- Wrap it up
Restate your opinion in a new way. Use phrases like: "That is why I believe...", "For these reasons..."

Example Opinion Paragraph

I believe that every school should have a garden. First, a school garden teaches students about science in a hands-on way. When students grow vegetables and observe plants, they learn about life cycles, soil, and the environment much better than just reading about it. Second, gardens give students a peaceful place to relax and connect with nature, which helps reduce stress and improve focus in class. For these reasons, I strongly believe school gardens would make every student's learning experience richer and more enjoyable.

Key Colour Guide

Green highlight = Opinion statement

Blue highlight = Reasons with details

Pink highlight = Concluding statement

Strong Opinion Writing Tips: Be specific! "Dogs make better pets than cats because they can be trained to help people" is much stronger than "Dogs are better because they are nice." Give real reasons and details that prove your point!

Try It Yourself!

Choose one of these opinion topics and write a paragraph:

  • Should students have homework every night?
  • Is summer or winter a better season?
  • Should schools have longer lunch breaks?
  1. Write your opinion statement clearly.
  2. Give two reasons with details to support your opinion.
  3. Write a concluding statement that restates your opinion.

Remember This!

Opinion -- what you think or believe

Reasons -- WHY you think that (be specific!)

Concluding Statement -- wrap it up and restate your opinion

Lesson 4.2

Linking Words in Opinion Writing

Use powerful linking words to connect your opinions and reasons smoothly!

What Are We Learning Today?

When you write an opinion piece, you need linking words to connect your opinion to your reasons and your reasons to each other. Without linking words, your writing sounds choppy and disconnected. With them, your writing flows smoothly and sounds professional!

Key Words to Know

Linking Word -- A word or phrase that connects ideas in writing, making it flow smoothly.
Transition -- Moving smoothly from one idea to the next using linking words.
Connective -- Another word for a linking word -- it connects two ideas together.

Linking Words for Opinion Writing

PurposeLinking Words and Phrases
To state your opinionI believe, I think, In my opinion, I feel that, It is my view that
To give a reasonbecause, since, due to, as a result of
To add another reasonFirst, Second, Third, Also, In addition, Furthermore, Another reason is
To give an exampleFor example, For instance, Such as, To illustrate
To concludeTherefore, For these reasons, In conclusion, That is why, As a result

See the Difference -- Without and With Linking Words

Without Linking Words (choppy and hard to follow):

Dogs make great pets. They are loyal. They protect your home. They keep you company. Dogs are great.

With Linking Words (smooth and convincing):

I believe dogs make the best pets. First, they are incredibly loyal and always happy to see you. In addition, dogs can be trained to protect your home and keep your family safe. Furthermore, they provide wonderful companionship, especially for people who live alone. For these reasons, I strongly believe that dogs make the perfect pet for any family.

Writing Tip: Do not start every sentence with "I think" or "because." Use a variety of linking words to make your writing more interesting and to show the different types of connections between your ideas.

Try It Yourself!

  1. Take the opinion paragraph you wrote in Lesson 4.1. Add at least THREE linking words from the table above.
  2. Write a new opinion paragraph about your favourite school subject. Use linking words to connect your opinion, reasons, and conclusion.
  3. Find TWO linking words in a paragraph from a book or article. What purpose do they serve?

Remember This!

Linking words connect your opinion, reasons, and conclusion.

Use a variety of linking words -- not just "because" and "also"!

Common opinion linking words: First, In addition, Because, For these reasons, Therefore

Lesson 4.3

Informational / Explanatory Writing

Share what you know about a topic clearly and accurately!

What Are We Learning Today?

Informational writing is all about sharing facts and knowledge about a real topic. Unlike opinion writing, you are not trying to convince the reader of your view -- you are explaining and informing. Your goal is to teach the reader something new!

Key Words to Know

Informational Writing -- Writing that explains or describes a real topic using facts and details.
Topic Sentence -- The first sentence of a paragraph that tells the reader what the paragraph is about.
Supporting Facts -- Real, accurate details that develop and explain the topic.
Definition -- An explanation of what something means -- used to clarify important terms.
Concluding Sentence -- The final sentence that wraps up the paragraph.

Structure of Informational Writing

Introduction

Introduce the topic. Tell the reader what they are about to learn. Write a clear topic sentence.

Body Paragraphs

Each paragraph covers ONE aspect of the topic. Include facts, definitions, and details. Group related information together.

Conclusion

Wrap up by restating the main idea in a new way. Leave the reader with something interesting to think about.

Example Informational Paragraph

Penguins are fascinating birds that cannot fly but are excellent swimmers. Unlike most birds, penguins have wings shaped like flippers, which allow them to glide through the water at speeds of up to 25 miles per hour. They live mainly in the Southern Hemisphere, with most species found in Antarctica. Penguins are also highly social animals -- they live in large groups called colonies, which can contain thousands of birds. With their remarkable adaptations and social behaviours, penguins are truly one of nature's most extraordinary creatures.

Opinion Writing vs. Informational Writing

FeatureOpinion WritingInformational Writing
PurposeConvince the readerInform / explain to the reader
ContentYour beliefs + reasonsFacts + definitions + details
Language"I believe," "I think"Neutral -- no "I believe"
TonePersuasiveFactual and objective

Try It Yourself!

Choose a topic you know about (an animal, a place, a sport, a science topic). Write one informational paragraph:

  1. Write a clear topic sentence that introduces the subject.
  2. Add three facts or details about the topic.
  3. Include at least one definition of an important term.
  4. Write a concluding sentence that wraps up the paragraph.

Remember This!

Informational writing informs and explains -- it does NOT share your opinions.

Use facts, definitions, and details to develop your topic.

Start with a topic sentence and end with a concluding sentence.

Lesson 4.4

Narrative Writing: Story Structure and Sequence

Write your own stories with a strong beginning, middle, and end!

What Are We Learning Today?

Narrative writing is storytelling! You create characters, a setting, and a plot with events that unfold in order. Every great story has a clear structure -- a beginning that draws readers in, a middle where the excitement happens, and an end that satisfies the reader.

Key Words to Know

Narrative -- A story -- real or imagined -- with characters, setting, and a sequence of events.
Narrator -- The voice telling the story (can be a character or an outside voice).
Sequence -- The order in which events happen in a story.
Conflict -- The problem or challenge the main character faces.
Resolution -- How the conflict is solved at the end of the story.
Closure -- A satisfying ending that wraps up the story and gives the reader a sense of completion.

The Story Mountain -- Structure of a Narrative

Beginning (Introduction)

  • Introduce the characters
  • Describe the setting
  • Set the scene -- hook the reader!

Middle (Rising Action + Conflict)

  • The problem appears
  • Events build up
  • Things get more exciting!
  • The highest point = the climax

End (Resolution + Closure)

  • The problem is solved
  • Characters learn something
  • The story wraps up with a satisfying ending

Example Narrative Opening

The morning Zara found the strange door in her grandmother's attic, everything changed. It was painted midnight blue and covered in tiny silver stars that seemed to move when she wasn't looking directly at them. Her heart hammered. She had explored every corner of this attic a hundred times -- so where had this door come from? She reached for the handle, her hand trembling slightly. Behind her, she heard her grandmother's footsteps on the stairs. "Zara," came the old woman's voice, warm but urgent. "Don't open that door. Not yet."

What Makes a Great Narrative Opening?

TechniqueExample from the Passage
Start in the middle of the action"The morning Zara found the strange door..."
Create mystery or suspenseThe door appeared with no explanation
Show character emotions"Her heart hammered," "her hand trembling"
End with a cliffhanger"Don't open that door. Not yet." -- we NEED to know more!

Try It Yourself!

Plan and write the BEGINNING of a short story. Include:

  1. A main character with a name and one character trait.
  2. A clear setting -- where and when.
  3. A hook -- something interesting or unexpected to grab the reader's attention.
  4. A hint of the problem to come.

Remember This!

Beginning -- introduce characters and setting, hook the reader

Middle -- conflict builds, events get exciting

End -- problem solved, satisfying closure

Lesson 4.5

Using Dialogue and Descriptive Details

Make your stories come alive with vivid details and character voices!

What Are We Learning Today?

The difference between a boring story and an exciting one often comes down to dialogue (what characters say) and descriptive details (vivid words that help readers see, hear, smell, and feel what is happening). Today we learn how to use both to make stories jump off the page!

Key Words to Know

Dialogue -- The exact words spoken by characters, shown inside quotation marks.
Descriptive Detail -- A specific, vivid word or phrase that helps the reader picture or feel something.
Show, Don't Tell -- Describing actions and feelings through specific details instead of just stating them.
Sensory Language -- Words that appeal to the five senses: sight, sound, smell, taste, and touch.
Speech Tag -- Words that tell us WHO said something (he said, she whispered, they shouted).

How to Write Dialogue Correctly

RuleExample
Put spoken words inside quotation marks"I found it!" she cried.
Start a new line for each new speaker"Are you ready?" asked Marco.
"Almost," replied Lily.
Use a speech tag to show who is speaking"Watch out!" he shouted.
Use varied speech tags (not just "said")whispered, shouted, asked, replied, exclaimed, muttered
Use punctuation inside the closing quote mark"Let's go," she said. / "Are you ready?" he asked.

Show, Don't Tell -- The Most Powerful Writing Technique

TELLING (weak)

She was scared.

He was happy.

It was cold outside.

SHOWING (vivid and powerful)

Her hands shook and she pressed herself against the wall, hardly daring to breathe.

He let out a whoop and spun around, punching the air.

The wind bit through her coat and her breath made little clouds in the air.

Example -- Dialogue and Description Working Together

The cave smelled of damp stone and something else -- something old and mysterious. Finn pressed forward, his torch casting dancing shadows on the walls.

"I think we should go back," whispered his sister Amal, grabbing his sleeve.

"We've come this far," he replied, his voice steadier than he felt. Somewhere deep in the darkness, something dripped. Something else moved.

"Finn." Amal's voice was barely a breath. "There's something in here with us."

Try It Yourself!

  1. Rewrite these "telling" sentences using "showing" language:
    a) "He was angry."   b) "The food tasted good."   c) "She was tired."
  2. Write a short dialogue (4-6 lines) between two characters who have just discovered something surprising. Use varied speech tags and correct punctuation.
  3. Add ONE sentence of descriptive detail to set the scene for your dialogue.

Remember This!

DIALOGUE -- spoken words in quotation marks, new line for new speaker

DESCRIPTIVE DETAILS -- specific, vivid words that bring scenes to life

SHOW, DON'T TELL -- describe actions and feelings, don't just state them!

Lesson 4.6

Temporal Words: First, Next, Then, Finally

Use time-order words to sequence events and guide your reader through your writing!

What Are We Learning Today?

When you write a story or explain a process, the order of events matters! Temporal words (also called time-order words or sequence words) tell the reader when things happen and help them follow the events in the right order. Without these words, writing can feel jumbled and confusing!

Key Words to Know

Temporal Words -- Words that signal time and order in writing (first, next, then, finally, before, after).
Sequence -- The order in which events happen.
Transition -- Moving smoothly from one event or idea to the next.
Time Signal -- A word or phrase that tells the reader when something happened in relation to something else.

Temporal Words and Phrases to Use

TypeWords and Phrases
BeginningFirst, To begin, At the start, In the beginning, One day, Early that morning
MiddleNext, Then, After that, Soon, Meanwhile, A little later, As soon as, When
EndFinally, At last, In the end, Eventually, After a while, By the time
Specific timeThat morning, The next day, An hour later, Just as, Suddenly, At that moment

See the Difference!

Without Temporal Words (confusing):

Lily woke up. She ate breakfast. She got dressed. She missed the bus. She ran to school. She was late. She was out of breath.

With Temporal Words (clear and flowing):

One morning, Lily woke up much later than usual. First, she rushed through breakfast, barely tasting her cereal. Then she grabbed her bag and ran to get dressed. By the time she reached the bus stop, the bus was already disappearing around the corner. "Oh no!" she gasped. As fast as she could, she sprinted all the way to school. Finally, red-faced and out of breath, she burst through the classroom door -- just as the bell rang.

Writing Tip: Do not use the same temporal word over and over! Instead of writing "first... first... first..." use a variety: "To begin... Next... Shortly after... Eventually... Finally..."

Try It Yourself!

  1. Write the steps for making your favourite sandwich or snack. Use at least FIVE different temporal words to sequence the steps.
  2. Look at a story you have already written. Add THREE temporal words to make the sequence of events clearer.
  3. Write a short paragraph describing your morning routine from waking up to arriving at school. Use temporal words throughout!

Remember This!

Temporal words tell the reader WHEN things happen and in what ORDER.

Use a variety of temporal words -- not just "first, next, then, finally"!

Temporal words make your writing clearer and easier to follow.

Lesson 4.7

Planning, Revising, and Editing

Every great piece of writing goes through multiple steps -- learn the writing process!

What Are We Learning Today?

Did you know that even professional authors do not write a perfect piece on the first try? Great writing is a process -- it happens in stages. Today we learn the three most important stages: planning (before you write), revising (improving your ideas), and editing (fixing your mistakes).

Key Words to Know

Planning -- Organising your ideas BEFORE you write. Using tools like graphic organisers, lists, or outlines.
Draft -- Your first attempt at writing -- not perfect, just getting your ideas down.
Revising -- Going back to improve your IDEAS -- adding details, reordering events, strengthening reasons.
Editing -- Fixing MISTAKES in spelling, punctuation, capitalisation, and grammar.
Publishing -- Creating a final, polished version to share with others.
Peer Review -- When a classmate or partner reads your work and gives helpful feedback.

The Writing Process -- 5 Stages

1
PREWRITING (Planning) -- Brainstorm your ideas. Use a graphic organiser, web, or list. Decide on your topic, audience, and purpose. Answer: What do I want to say? Who am I writing for?
2
DRAFTING -- Write your first draft. Get ALL your ideas down. Do not worry about perfection -- just write! You can fix it later.
3
REVISING -- Improve your IDEAS. Add missing details. Remove things that do not belong. Reorder events or reasons. Strengthen weak sentences. Ask: Does this make sense? Is there anything missing?
4
EDITING -- Fix your MISTAKES. Check spelling, punctuation, capitalisation, and grammar. This is different from revising -- editing fixes errors, not ideas.
5
PUBLISHING -- Create your final version! Write neatly, type it up, add illustrations, or share it with others. Be proud of your work!

Revising vs. Editing -- Know the Difference!

Revising (Big picture -- IDEAS)Editing (Details -- CORRECTNESS)
Add more descriptive detailsFix spelling mistakes
Strengthen a weak reasonAdd missing punctuation marks
Remove sentences that don't fitCapitalise proper nouns and sentence beginnings
Reorder events to make more senseCorrect subject-verb agreement
Make the beginning more engagingCheck that dialogue is punctuated correctly

Editing Checklist -- Use This Every Time!

1
Every sentence starts with a capital letter.
2
Every sentence ends with a period, question mark, or exclamation mark.
3
All proper nouns (names of people and places) are capitalised.
4
Dialogue is inside quotation marks with correct punctuation.
5
Spelling has been checked -- especially sight words!
6
Each paragraph has a clear main idea.
7
The writing has a clear beginning, middle, and end.

Try It Yourself!

  1. Take any piece of writing you have done in this unit. Go through the editing checklist above. Fix at least THREE things.
  2. Now revise the same piece -- add ONE new descriptive detail and strengthen ONE reason or sentence.
  3. Ask a parent or family member to read your revised piece. What feedback do they give? Can you improve it further?

The Writing Process: Plan → Draft → Revise → Edit → Publish

REVISING = improving your IDEAS (add, remove, reorder)

EDITING = fixing MISTAKES (spelling, punctuation, capitalisation)

Every great writer revises and edits -- it is part of the process!
