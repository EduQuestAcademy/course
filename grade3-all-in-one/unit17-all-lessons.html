Lesson 17.1

Types of Communities: Urban, Suburban, and Rural

People live in very different kinds of communities -- discover what makes each unique!

What Are We Learning Today?

A community is a group of people who live in the same area and share resources, services, and a sense of belonging. Communities come in very different sizes and types! Today we explore the three main types: urban, suburban, and rural -- and discover what life is like in each one.

Key Words to Know

Community -- A group of people living in the same place and sharing services and resources.
Urban -- A city. Dense population, tall buildings, lots of businesses and services.
Suburban -- An area surrounding a city. Mix of homes, schools, and shopping. Lower density than urban.
Rural -- Countryside away from cities. Farms, forests, open space. Low population density.
Population Density -- How many people live in a given area. Cities have high density; rural areas have low density.
Goods -- Physical products people buy and sell (food, clothing, tools).
Services -- Jobs that help people rather than making products (teaching, nursing, firefighting).

URBAN Communities (Cities)

Population: Very high (thousands to millions of people)

Housing: Apartments, condos, tall buildings

Transportation: Buses, trains, subways, taxis

Services: Museums, hospitals, universities, sports stadiums

Washington examples: Seattle, Spokane, Tacoma

SUBURBAN Communities (Suburbs)

Population: Medium (thousands of people)

Housing: Single-family homes with yards

Transportation: Cars, some buses

Services: Malls, parks, schools, community centres

Washington examples: Bellevue, Redmond, Kennewick!

RURAL Communities (Countryside)

Population: Low (hundreds or fewer people)

Housing: Houses on large lots, farms

Transportation: Mostly cars, long distances to travel

Services: Farming, forestry, ranching

Washington examples: Walla Walla wine country, Palouse wheat farms, small mountain towns

Kennewick Connection: Kennewick is part of the Tri-Cities area (with Richland and Pasco). With about 80,000 people, it is a mid-sized city -- sometimes called a suburban community. It has the services of a city but a more spread-out feel than a major urban centre like Seattle.

Try It Yourself!

  1. What type of community do you live in? How do you know?
  2. Name TWO advantages of living in an urban community and TWO advantages of rural living.
  3. How do you think transportation is different for someone living on a farm vs. someone living in downtown Seattle?
  4. What goods and services does your community provide to the people who live there?

Remember This!

Urban = city, high density. Suburban = near a city, medium density.

Rural = countryside, low density, farms and open space.

All three types depend on each other -- cities need food from farms, farms need services from cities!

Maps: Keys, Symbols, and Compass Roses

Maps are tools that help us understand the world -- learn to read them like a pro!

What Are We Learning Today?

A map is a flat representation of all or part of Earth's surface. Maps are incredibly useful tools -- they help us navigate, understand geography, plan trips, and see how places relate to each other. Today we learn the key parts of a map and how to read them accurately!

Key Words to Know

Map -- A flat drawing that shows all or part of Earth's surface from above.
Map Key (Legend) -- A box on the map that explains what each symbol or colour means.
Map Symbol -- A picture, shape, or colour used on a map to represent a real feature.
Compass Rose -- A symbol on a map showing the four cardinal directions: North, South, East, West.
Cardinal Directions -- North, South, East, West (N, S, E, W).
Intermediate Directions -- The directions between cardinal directions: Northeast, Northwest, Southeast, Southwest.
Scale -- The ratio between distance on the map and actual distance on Earth.
Grid -- A pattern of lines (latitude and longitude) that helps locate places on a map.

Types of Maps

Political Map

Shows borders between countries, states, and cities. Useful for understanding government boundaries.

Physical Map

Shows natural features -- mountains, rivers, plains. Uses colours: green = low, brown = mountains, blue = water.

Road Map

Shows roads, highways, and how to get from place to place. Used for navigation.

Population Map

Uses colours or dots to show where people live and how densely populated each area is.

  3. Why is a map key (legend) important? What would happen if a map had no key?
  4. What is map scale? If 1 inch = 100 miles on a map, and two cities are 3 inches apart, how far apart are they in real life?

Remember This!

Every map needs: Title, Key/Legend, Compass Rose, Scale, Grid.

Cardinal directions: N, S, E, W -- "Never Eat Soggy Waffles!"

The map key explains what every symbol and colour on the map means.

Landforms and Bodies of Water

Earth's surface is shaped into amazing features -- learn their names and characteristics!

What Are We Learning Today?

Earth's surface has an incredible variety of natural features -- towering mountains, deep valleys, winding rivers, and vast plains. These features are called landforms and bodies of water. Today we learn to identify and describe them -- and find many of them right here in Washington state!

Key Words to Know

Mountain -- A very high, steep landform rising sharply above surrounding land.
Valley -- A low area of land between hills or mountains, often with a river.
Plain -- A large, flat area of land. Good for farming!
Plateau -- A flat area of land at a high elevation -- a "table" of land.
Peninsula -- Land surrounded by water on three sides. Example: Olympic Peninsula.
Island -- Land completely surrounded by water.
River -- A large body of flowing freshwater that moves toward an ocean, lake, or another river.
Bay -- A body of water partially surrounded by land, opening to a larger water body.
Ocean -- The largest body of saltwater covering most of Earth's surface.

Lesson 17.4

Geographic Regions of Washington State

Washington has dramatically different regions -- explore each one!

What Are We Learning Today?

Washington state is one of the most geographically diverse states in the nation! In just one day's drive, you can go from a temperate rainforest to an active volcano to a desert to a major ocean port. Today we explore Washington's distinct geographic regions and what makes each one special.

Key Words to Know

Region -- An area with common characteristics that set it apart from surrounding areas.
Rain Shadow -- An area on the dry side of a mountain range that receives little precipitation.
Cascade Mountains -- A volcanic mountain range that divides Washington into wet west and dry east.
Columbia Plateau -- A large flat volcanic plateau covering much of eastern Washington.
Puget Sound Lowlands -- The low, flat area around Puget Sound in western Washington -- most populated region.

Washington's Five Main Regions

1. Coast and Olympic Peninsula

Features: Pacific Ocean coastline, rainforests, mountains

Climate: Very wet! 140+ inches of rain/year in some spots

Known for: Hoh Rainforest, Olympic National Park, fishing industry

2. Puget Sound Lowlands

Features: Flat land, Puget Sound, islands

Climate: Mild and rainy (38 inches/year)

Known for: Seattle, Boeing, Microsoft, Amazon, port trade

3. Cascade Mountains

Features: Volcanic peaks, forests, ski areas

Climate: Cold, heavy snowfall

Known for: Mount Rainier, North Cascades, skiing, hiking

4. Columbia Plateau (Eastern WA)

Features: Flat volcanic plateau, wheat fields, scablands

Climate: Semi-arid (rain shadow from Cascades)

Known for: Wheat farming, Spokane, Kennewick, Columbia River dams

5. Okanogan Highlands

Features: Rolling hills, forests, valleys

Climate: Cold winters, warm summers

Known for: Apple orchards, mining, cattle ranching

Kennewick and the Tri-Cities: Your Home Region!

Kennewick sits in the Columbia Plateau region, at the confluence (meeting point) of the Columbia, Snake, and Yakima Rivers. This location made it an important crossroads for trade and settlement. The area gets only about 8 inches of rain per year -- but thanks to irrigation from the Columbia River, the surrounding area grows apples, grapes, hops, asparagus, and many other crops. The Tri-Cities area (Kennewick, Richland, Pasco) is also home to the Hanford Site, which played a major role in US history during World War II and the Cold War.

Try It Yourself!

  1. What geographic feature divides Washington into wet western and dry eastern regions?
  2. Why does eastern Washington get so much less rain than western Washington?
  3. Which region of Washington do you live in? Name TWO things that region is known for.
  4. How has the Columbia River been important to the development of the Kennewick/Tri-Cities area?

Remember This!

The Cascade Mountains divide Washington into a wet west side and a dry east side.

The rain shadow effect makes eastern Washington semi-arid.

Kennewick is in the Columbia Plateau region -- shaped by the Columbia River!

Lesson 17.5

How Geography Affects How People Live

The land shapes everything -- where we live, what we eat, and how we work!

What Are We Learning Today?

Geography is not just about maps and landforms -- it shapes every aspect of human life! Where you live determines your climate, what food can be grown nearby, what jobs are available, what sports are popular, how people travel, and even what languages are spoken. Today we explore how geography and human life are deeply connected.

Key Words to Know

Human-Environment Interaction -- How people adapt to, depend on, and modify their natural environment.
Agriculture -- The practice of farming -- growing crops and raising animals for food.
Irrigation -- A system of watering crops using canals, pipes, or sprinklers. Allows farming in dry areas.
Industry -- Economic activity that produces goods or provides services for trade.
Trade Route -- A path used to move goods from one place to another for exchange.
Adapt -- To change behaviour or practices to suit a different environment or situation.

Geography Shapes Human Life in Four Key Ways

1. Where People Settle

People settle near water, flat land, and mild climates. That is why most major cities are near rivers or coasts -- water = transportation + food + power.

Example: Seattle on Puget Sound; Kennewick at the Columbia River confluence.

2. What Food People Eat

Geography determines what can be grown or caught. Coastal people eat seafood. Prairie people raise cattle. River valley people grow fruits and vegetables.

Example: Eastern WA apples, Puget Sound salmon, Palouse wheat.

3. What Jobs People Have

Geography creates industries. Mountains → mining and skiing. Forests → logging and tourism. Oceans → fishing and shipping. Flat land → farming.

Example: Kennewick has agriculture, nuclear technology (Hanford), and river trade.

4. How People Travel

Mountains block travel (passes needed). Rivers are natural highways. Plains allow roads in any direction. Oceans connect distant places by ship.

Example: Columbia River was the highway of the Pacific Northwest for thousands of years.

  2. How has irrigation changed what is possible to grow in eastern Washington?

Remember This!

Geography shapes where people settle, what they eat, what jobs they have, and how they travel.

People both adapt to (change their behaviour) and modify (change the environment) their surroundings.

Our Place in the World: From Neighborhood to Globe

You are part of many communities -- from your street to planet Earth!

What Are We Learning Today?

Every person on Earth belongs to many overlapping communities at the same time -- your street, your city, your state, your country, and the world. Understanding how these layers connect helps us see our place in the bigger picture and understand how we are connected to people around the globe!

Key Words to Know

Address -- The specific location of a place (street number, street name, city, state, ZIP code, country).
Neighbourhood -- The immediate area where you live -- your street and nearby streets.
City -- A large community with many people, businesses, and services.
County -- A division of a state. Washington has 39 counties. Kennewick is in Benton County.
State -- A large political division of a country. The US has 50 states.
Country (Nation) -- A large territory with its own government. The United States is a country.
Continent -- One of the seven major landmasses on Earth.
Globe -- A three-dimensional model of Earth.
