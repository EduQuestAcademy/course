Lesson 12.1

Shapes: Quadrilaterals, Triangles, and Polygons

Explore the world of flat shapes -- and learn their mathematical names!

What Are We Learning Today?

Shapes are everywhere! The screen you are reading on, the pages of your books, the tiles on the floor -- all shapes. Today we learn about polygons -- closed flat shapes made of straight sides -- and meet the most important families of polygons!

Key Words to Know

Polygon -- A closed, flat shape made of three or more straight sides.
Side -- One of the straight lines that make up a polygon.
Vertex (Vertices) -- A corner where two sides of a polygon meet.
Triangle -- A polygon with 3 sides and 3 vertices.
Quadrilateral -- A polygon with 4 sides and 4 vertices. (Quad = 4)
Pentagon -- A polygon with 5 sides. (Penta = 5)
Hexagon -- A polygon with 6 sides. (Hex = 6)
Octagon -- A polygon with 8 sides. (Oct = 8)

The Polygon Family

Triangle
3 sides, 3 vertices
Quadrilateral
4 sides, 4 vertices
Pentagon
5 sides, 5 vertices
Hexagon
6 sides, 6 vertices
Octagon
8 sides, 8 vertices

Not a Polygon! -- What Polygons Must Have

IS a Polygon:

  • Closed shape (no gaps)
  • All straight sides
  • At least 3 sides

NOT a Polygon:

  • A circle (curved sides)
  • An open shape (gap between sides)
  • A shape with curved AND straight sides
Memory trick for sides and vertices: Triangles have 3 of each. The number of sides always equals the number of vertices in any polygon!

Naming Polygons by Their Number of Sides

SidesNameReal-World Example
3TriangleYield sign, tortilla chip, musical triangle
4QuadrilateralDoor, window, book, playing card
5PentagonThe Pentagon building, home plate in baseball
6HexagonHoneycomb cells, a bolt head, snowflake shape
8OctagonStop sign!

Try It Yourself!

  1. Look around your room. Find ONE example of each: a triangle, a quadrilateral, and a hexagon.
  2. Is a circle a polygon? Is an oval a polygon? Explain why or why not.
  3. Draw a polygon with 7 sides. What would it be called? (Hint: hepta = 7)
  4. A shape has 6 vertices. How many sides does it have? What is its name?

Remember This!

A polygon = closed shape + straight sides + at least 3 sides.

Sides always equal vertices in any polygon.

3=triangle, 4=quadrilateral, 5=pentagon, 6=hexagon, 8=octagon

Lesson 12.2

Attributes of Shapes: Sides, Angles, and Vertices

Describe shapes precisely using mathematical attributes!

What Are We Learning Today?

Every shape has specific attributes -- measurable properties that describe exactly what kind of shape it is. Today we learn to identify and describe shapes by counting sides and vertices, and by identifying types of angles.

Key Words to Know

Attribute -- A property that describes a shape (number of sides, type of angles, equal sides).
Angle -- The amount of turn between two sides that meet at a vertex.
Right Angle -- A perfect square corner -- exactly 90 degrees. Looks like the corner of a piece of paper.
Acute Angle -- An angle smaller than a right angle. Sharp and pointy!
Obtuse Angle -- An angle larger than a right angle. Wide and open!
Parallel Sides -- Sides that are always the same distance apart and never meet, like train tracks.
Equal Sides -- Sides that are exactly the same length.

Three Types of Angles

Right Angle

Exactly 90 degrees. The small square in the corner shows a right angle.

Acute Angle

Less than 90 degrees. Sharp and narrow -- like a pointy mountain!

Obtuse Angle

Greater than 90 degrees. Wide and open -- like a big smile!

Parallel Sides

Has Parallel Sides:

Rectangle -- 2 pairs of parallel sides

Most triangles

Irregular quadrilaterals

Pentagons (usually)

How to spot parallel lines: Imagine the lines extending forever in both directions. If they would never meet -- they are parallel! Train tracks are the classic example.

Try It Yourself!

  1. Describe a rectangle using four attributes (sides, angles, parallel sides, equal sides).
  2. Draw a shape with exactly 2 right angles. What shape is it?
  3. Which has more right angles -- a triangle or a rectangle? How many does each have?
  4. Name a shape that has all equal sides but NO right angles.

Remember This!

Right angle = square corner (90°)  |  Acute = less than 90°  |  Obtuse = more than 90°

Parallel sides never meet -- like train tracks.

Shapes are described by their sides, angles, and vertices!

Lesson 12.3

Quadrilaterals: Squares, Rectangles, Rhombuses, and More

Meet the whole family of four-sided shapes!

What Are We Learning Today?

The quadrilateral family is large and fascinating! All quadrilaterals have 4 sides and 4 vertices -- but within that family there are many special shapes with extra attributes. Today we learn how they are related and what makes each one special.

Key Words to Know

Parallelogram -- A quadrilateral with 2 pairs of parallel sides.
Rectangle -- A parallelogram with 4 right angles.
Rhombus -- A parallelogram with 4 equal sides.
Square -- A rectangle AND a rhombus -- 4 equal sides AND 4 right angles.
Trapezoid -- A quadrilateral with exactly 1 pair of parallel sides.

The Quadrilateral Family Tree

All Quadrilaterals (4 sides, 4 vertices)

Parallelogram

2 pairs of parallel sides

Trapezoid

1 pair of parallel sides

Within Parallelograms:

Rectangle

4 right angles

Rhombus

4 equal sides + 4 right angles

The BIG Idea: Squares are Special!

A square IS a rectangle because...

It has 4 right angles. Rectangles just need 4 right angles -- and a square has them!

A square IS a rhombus because...

It has 4 equal sides. Rhombuses just need 4 equal sides -- and a square has them!

Think of it like this: All squares are rectangles, but NOT all rectangles are squares (a rectangle could have sides of different lengths). A square is the most "special" quadrilateral of all!
Rectangle
4 right angles, opposite sides equal
Square
4 right angles, all sides equal
Rhombus
4 equal sides, no right angles
Parallelogram

Try It Yourself!

  1. Can a rectangle also be a square? Can a square also be a rectangle? Explain both answers.
  2. How is a rhombus different from a square if both have 4 equal sides?
  3. Name two quadrilaterals that have EXACTLY 4 right angles.
  4. Which quadrilateral has exactly 1 pair of parallel sides? Draw it!

Remember This!

Square = 4 equal sides + 4 right angles (the most special!)

Rectangle = 4 right angles (not all sides equal)

Rhombus = 4 equal sides (not always right angles)

Trapezoid = exactly 1 pair of parallel sides

Partitioning Shapes into Equal Parts

Divide shapes into equal parts to build deeper fraction understanding!

What Are We Learning Today?

Partitioning means dividing a shape into equal parts. This directly connects geometry to fractions! When you cut a rectangle into 4 equal parts, each part is one-fourth of the whole rectangle. Today we explore different ways to partition shapes and use fraction language to describe the parts.

Key Words to Know

Partition -- To divide a shape into equal parts.
Equal Parts -- Parts that are all the same size, even if they look different in shape.
Halves -- 2 equal parts. Each part = 1/2.
Thirds -- 3 equal parts. Each part = 1/3.
Fourths / Quarters -- 4 equal parts. Each part = 1/4.
Sixths -- 6 equal parts. Each part = 1/6.
Eighths -- 8 equal parts. Each part = 1/8.

The Key Connection: More Parts = Smaller Parts

Number of Equal PartsNameSize of Each Part
2Halves1/2 -- largest parts

Big idea: The MORE equal parts you divide a shape into, the SMALLER each part becomes. Dividing the SAME shape into 8 parts gives much smaller pieces than dividing it into 2 parts. This is exactly why 1/8 < 1/2!

Same Area, Different Shapes -- Still Equal!

Two different ways to divide a square into fourths -- both are correct as long as the parts are the same size:

Vertical + Horizontal lines

Diagonal lines

Both show 4 equal parts, even though the triangles and squares look different in shape. Same area = equal parts!

Try It Yourself!

  1. Draw a rectangle and partition it into thirds. What fraction does each part represent?
  2. Draw a circle and partition it into sixths.
  3. If you partition a square into 8 equal parts, what fraction is each part?
  4. What happens to the size of each part as you increase the number of equal parts? Use an example.

Remember This!

Partitioning connects geometry and fractions!

More equal parts = smaller each part is.

Parts can look different in shape but still be equal in area!

Lesson 12.5

Symmetry and Lines of Symmetry

When a shape folds perfectly in half -- that is the beauty of symmetry!

What Are We Learning Today?

Have you ever looked at a butterfly and noticed both wings look identical? That is symmetry! A shape has symmetry when it can be folded along a line so that both halves match perfectly. The fold line is called a line of symmetry. Symmetry appears everywhere in nature, art, and architecture!

Key Words to Know

Symmetry -- When a shape can be divided into two matching halves that are mirror images of each other.
Line of Symmetry -- The line that divides a shape into two matching halves. Also called an axis of symmetry.
Asymmetrical -- A shape with NO line of symmetry -- it cannot be divided into matching halves.

Lines of Symmetry in Common Shapes

Rectangle
2 lines of symmetry
Square
4 lines of symmetry
Circle
Infinite lines of symmetry!
Equilateral Triangle
3 lines of symmetry
Scalene Triangle
0 lines of symmetry

How to Test for a Line of Symmetry

The Fold Test:

If you could fold the shape along the line and both halves match EXACTLY -- it is a line of symmetry!

Try it: draw a heart shape and fold it down the middle. The left and right halves match -- that is a line of symmetry!

Symmetry in the Real World

Symmetrical things:

  • Butterfly wings
  • Human faces
  • Most letters: A, H, M, O, T, U, V, W, X, Y
  • Snowflakes (6 lines!)

Asymmetrical things:

  • The letter F, G, J, L, R, S, Z
  • A shoe (left vs right)
  • A map of most countries
  • Most cloud shapes

Try It Yourself!

  1. Draw the line(s) of symmetry for each letter: A, B, C, D, H, M.
  2. How many lines of symmetry does a regular hexagon have? (Hint: it has 6 equal sides.)
  3. Draw a shape with exactly 2 lines of symmetry that is NOT a rectangle or square.
  4. Is the letter "G" symmetrical? Draw it and try to find a line of symmetry.

Remember This!

A line of symmetry divides a shape into two matching mirror-image halves.

Square = 4 lines  |  Rectangle = 2 lines  |  Circle = infinite lines!

Use the fold test: if both halves match, it is a line of symmetry!

Lesson 12.6

Solid Figures: 3D Shapes

Step into three dimensions -- explore the shapes that fill our world!

What Are We Learning Today?

The shapes we have studied so far are flat -- they exist in two dimensions (2D). But the real world is three-dimensional (3D)! Boxes, balls, cans, ice cream cones -- these are all 3D shapes called solid figures. Today we learn to identify and describe them using faces, edges, and vertices.

Key Words to Know

Solid Figure (3D Shape) -- A shape that has length, width, AND height -- it takes up space.
Face -- A flat surface of a 3D shape.
Edge -- A line where two faces of a 3D shape meet.
Vertex (Vertices) -- A corner point where three or more edges meet.
Curved Surface -- A round, non-flat surface (like the side of a can).

The 3D Shape Family

Rectangular Prism

Cube

Cone
1 circular face, 1 curved surface, 1 vertex
Cylinder
